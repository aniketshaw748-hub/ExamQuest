# MODULE 1 — INTRODUCTION (Biology for Engineers)

## 1.1 Core Theory
**Biology** is the natural science that studies life and living organisms — their structure, chemical processes, molecular interactions, physiological mechanisms, development and evolution.

Three unifying ideas of biology:
- The **cell** is the basic unit of life.
- The **gene** is the basic unit of heredity.
- **Evolution** is the engine driving creation and extinction of species.

Living organisms are **open systems** that survive by transforming energy and decreasing local entropy to maintain **homeostasis** (a stable internal condition).

**Why biology for engineers?** The genomic + molecular revolutions (e.g., the Human Genome Project, published **2003**; ~25,000–26,000 human genes identified) let us treat biological functions as **"biomolecular machines"** and their regulation as **"biomolecular circuits."** This forged deep links between biology and engineering.

## 1.2 Crisp Definitions (memorize)
- **Biology:** Natural science of life and living organisms.
- **Biomimicry:** An approach to innovation that seeks sustainable solutions to human challenges by **emulating nature's time-tested patterns and strategies** (act of mimicking nature for technology).
- **Biomimetic design:** Human-made processes/devices/systems that imitate nature.
- **Three levels of biomimicry:** (1) the **Organism**, (2) its **Behaviour**, (3) the **Ecosystem**.
- **Bioremediation:** Use of living organisms to clean up / degrade environmental pollutants.
- **Bioprocessing:** Procedure of preparing a biological product (esp. by genetic engineering) for commercial use.
- **Homeostasis:** Maintenance of a stable internal environment.

## 1.3 Examples of Biomimicry
| Inspiration (nature) | Engineering outcome |
|----------------------|---------------------|
| Bird wing/body shape | Aeroplane wing & fuselage design |
| Gecko foot | **Gecko tape** (dry adhesive) |
| Whale fin tubercles | Efficient turbine blades |
| Lotus leaf | Self-cleaning surfaces |
| Human eye | Camera optics |

## 1.4 Science vs Engineering (★ asked as model Q)
| Aspect | Science | Engineering |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| Direction | Physical system → descriptive model (theory) | Descriptive model (design) → physical system |
| Focus | What is **not yet** understood | What is **already** understood |
| Method | **Scientific method** (hypothesis → experiment) | **Design process** (define problem → prototype) |
| Goal | Understand nature | Create new things |

## 1.6 Role of Engineers in Biology (long-answer points)
Life's Matrix (information processing) · Biological Circuits (central dogma as computing) · Signal Transduction (protein-kinase cascade = amplifier/switch) · Control Systems & Feedback (cell-cycle, blood-sugar regulation) · Sensors & Detectors (immune system = computer-security analogy) · Human Genome Project (computing + informatics + imaging).

- Human Genome Project published **2003** (drafts 2000–2001), not 1999/2008.
- "Pearls of the ocean" = **diatoms** (silica walls), not oyster pearls.

- **Science vs Engineering:** "Science **Seeks** understanding; Engineering **Erects** systems."

# MODULE 2 — BIOLOGICAL CLASSIFICATION

## 2.1 Core Theory
**Classification** = scientific arranging of organisms into groups by similarities & differences. Aristotle made the first attempt; **Linnaeus** gave a **two-kingdom** system (Plantae, Animalia) and **binomial nomenclature**; **R.H. Whittaker (1969)** gave the **Five-Kingdom system**.

**Five-kingdom criteria:** (1) cell structure, (2) body organisation, (3) mode of nutrition, (4) reproduction, (5) phylogenetic relationships.

## 2.2 The Five Kingdoms (★★★★)
| Kingdom | Cell type | Nutrition | Examples |
|---------|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Monera** | Prokaryotic | Auto- or heterotrophic | Bacteria, Archaea |
| **Protista** | Eukaryotic, unicellular | Various | Diatoms, dinoflagellates, euglenoids, slime moulds, protozoa |
| **Fungi** | Eukaryotic | Saprophytic (mostly) | Yeasts, moulds, mushrooms |
| **Plantae** | Eukaryotic, multicellular, **chlorophyll** | Autotrophic | Algae, bryophytes, pteridophytes, gymnosperms, angiosperms |
| **Animalia** | Eukaryotic, multicellular, no cell wall | Holozoic (ingestive) | Animals |

> Viruses, viroids and lichens are **NOT** placed in the five-kingdom system.

**Fungi classes (4):** Phycomycetes, Ascomycetes, Basidiomycetes, **Deuteromycetes** ("imperfect fungi" — sexual stage unknown).

## 2.3 Crisp Definitions
- **Binomial nomenclature:** Two-word Latin naming (Genus + species), e.g., *Homo sapiens*; given by Linnaeus.
- **Taxonomy:** Science of identification, naming and classification.
- **Phylogenetic / Evolutionary classification:** Based on evolutionary relationships (a **natural system**).
- **Imperfect fungi (Deuteromycetes):** Fungi whose sexual (perfect) reproductive stage is absent/unknown.
- **Diatoms:** Golden-brown algae with silica cell walls; called **"pearls of the ocean."**

## 2.4 Taxonomic Hierarchy (asked: define Species/Genus/Phylum/Order/Family)
```
Kingdom ► Phylum ► Class ► Order ► Family ► Genus ► Species
(broadest)                                          (narrowest)
```
| Rank | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Species** | Group of organisms that interbreed and produce fertile offspring (basic unit). |
| **Genus** | Group of closely related species. |
| **Family** | Group of related genera. |
| **Order** | Group of related families. |
| **Phylum** | Group of related classes (major body-plan division). |

**Mnemonic:** "**K**ing **P**hilip **C**ame **O**ver **F**or **G**ood **S**oup" = Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species.

## 2.5 Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cell (★★★★★ — asked verbatim, BSC401-2022)
| Feature | Prokaryote | Eukaryote |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Cell type | Always unicellular | Uni- & multicellular |
| Size | 0.2–2.0 µm | 10–100 µm |
| Nucleus | **Absent** (no membrane) | **Present** (membrane-bound) |
| DNA | Circular, naked | Linear, with histones |
| Ribosome | **70S** | **80S** (cytoplasmic) |
| Mitochondria | Absent | Present |
| ER / Golgi / Lysosome | Absent | Present |
| Cell division | Binary fission | Mitosis / Meiosis |
| Examples | Bacteria, Archaea | Plant & animal cells |

# MODULE 3 — GENETICS

## 3.1 Core Theory
**Genetics** = branch of biology dealing with **genes, genetic variation and heredity**.
- **Gene:** Unit of heredity (DNA segment coding a trait). **Allele:** Alternative form of a gene (T = tall, t = short).
- **Genotype:** Genetic makeup (Tt). **Phenotype:** Observable trait (tall).
- **Homozygous:** Two identical alleles (TT/tt). **Heterozygous:** Two different (Tt).
- **Dominant:** Expressed in heterozygote. **Recessive:** Expressed only when homozygous.
- **Mutation:** A sudden, heritable change in a gene.

## 3.2 Mendel's Three Laws (★★★★★ — asked verbatim)
1. **Law of Dominance:** In a heterozygote one allele (dominant) masks the other (recessive).
2. **Law of Segregation (Purity of Gametes):** The two alleles of a gene separate during gamete formation; each gamete carries only one allele.
3. **Law of Independent Assortment:** Alleles of different genes assort independently during gamete formation.

## 3.3 Monohybrid Cross — Punnett Square (★★★★★)
Cross: Tt × Tt (Tall × Tall heterozygous)
```
         T        t
      ┌───────┬───────┐
   T  │  TT   │  Tt   │
      ├───────┼───────┤
   t  │  Tt   │  tt   │
      └───────┴───────┘
```
- **Genotypic ratio = 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt**
- **Phenotypic ratio = 3 Tall : 1 Short = 3 : 1**

## 3.4 Dihybrid Cross
- RrYy × RrYy → **Phenotypic ratio 9 : 3 : 3 : 1** (9 round-yellow : 3 round-green : 3 wrinkled-yellow : 1 wrinkled-green).
- Demonstrates the **Law of Independent Assortment**.

## 3.5 Deviations from Mendel (important short notes)
| Concept | Meaning | Ratio / example |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| **Incomplete dominance** | Heterozygote shows intermediate phenotype | Red × White snapdragon → **Pink**; 1:2:1 |
| **Co-dominance** | Both alleles fully expressed | AB blood group; roan cattle |
| **Epistasis** | One gene masks/modifies another gene at a different locus | Coat colour; modified 9:3:3:1 (e.g., 9:3:4, 12:3:1) |
| **Linkage** | Genes on same chromosome inherited together | Exception to independent assortment |

**Epistasis (★ asked BSC401-2022):** Interaction between **two non-allelic genes** where one masks/suppresses the other. e.g., in Labradors gene B (black/brown) is masked by gene E (ee → yellow regardless of B).

## 3.6 Mitosis vs Meiosis (★★★★★ — asked in all 3 papers)
| Feature | **Mitosis** | **Meiosis** |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Purpose | Growth, repair | Gamete (sex-cell) formation |
| Divisions | One | **Two** (I and II) |
| Daughter cells | **2**, diploid (2n) | **4**, haploid (n) |
| Chromosome no. | Conserved (2n→2n) | Halved (2n→n) |
| Genetic identity | Identical to parent | Genetically variable |
| Crossing over | Absent | **Present** (prophase I) |
| Pairing (synapsis) | No | Yes (homologous pairs) |
| Where | Somatic cells | Germ cells |

Stages of mitosis: **Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase → Cytokinesis** (**PMAT**).

## 3.7 Crisp Definitions
- **Dominance:** One allele masking/overriding another allele of the same gene.
- **Heredity:** Transfer of hereditary characters from one generation to the next.
- **Recessive allele:** Expressed only in homozygous condition.
- **Test cross:** Cross with the homozygous recessive to find an unknown genotype.

## 3.8 Common Mistakes
- **Segregation** acts on alleles of the **same** gene; **independent assortment** on **different** genes.
- Monohybrid phenotype 3:1, genotype 1:2:1; dihybrid phenotype 9:3:3:1.
- Incomplete dominance (blend, pink) ≠ co-dominance (both shown, AB blood).
- **Linkage** and **mutation** are exceptions to Mendel's laws (BSC401 MCQ).

# MODULE 4 — BIOMOLECULES ★★★★★

## 4.1 Overview
Four major biomolecules: **Carbohydrates, Lipids, Proteins (amino acids), Nucleic acids** (+ vitamins, minerals).

## 4.2 CARBOHYDRATES
**Definition:** Polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones (or compounds yielding them on hydrolysis); composition C, H, O in ratio (CH₂O)ₙ. Chief source = **photosynthesis**.

| Class | No. of units | Examples |
|-------|--------------|----------|
| **Monosaccharides** | 1 | Glucose, fructose, galactose, ribose |
| **Oligosaccharides** | 2–10 | Sucrose, lactose, maltose (disaccharides) |
| **Polysaccharides** | >10 | Starch, glycogen, cellulose |

- **Homopolysaccharide:** one type of monomer — starch, glycogen, cellulose.
- **Heteropolysaccharide:** >1 type — glycoproteins, **mucopolysaccharides** (hyaluronic acid, heparin, chondroitin sulphate; contain amino sugars; act as lubricants/structural).
- **Why carbohydrates are optically active:** they contain **asymmetric (chiral) carbon atoms**, so they rotate plane-polarised light (dextro/laevo-rotatory).
- **Reducing sugars** (free –CHO/–C=O) reduce Cu²⁺→Cu⁺ (Fehling/Benedict positive).
- Storage: **starch** (plants), **glycogen** ("animal starch"); structural: **cellulose** (plant wall), **chitin** (fungi/arthropods).

## 4.3 LIPIDS
**Definition (Bloor):** Esters of fatty acids and alcohol; hydrophobic, insoluble in water but soluble in ether/benzene/chloroform.
| Class | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **Simple lipids** | Fatty acid + glycerol | Fats (solid), oils (liquid), waxes |
| **Compound lipids** | + extra group | Phospholipids, glycolipids, chromolipids |
| **Derived lipids** | From hydrolysis | Steroids, fatty acids |

- **Saturated FA:** no double bond, solid, raise cholesterol (palmitic, butyric).
- **Unsaturated FA:** double bonds, liquid, lower cholesterol (plant oils).
- **Phospholipids:** amphipathic (hydrophilic phosphate head + hydrophobic tail) → form the cell-membrane bilayer.
- **Functions:** energy store (**1 g lipid ≈ 39.1 kJ**, > carbs/protein), membranes, heat insulation, hormone synthesis, carry vitamins A, D, E, K.

## 4.4 AMINO ACIDS & PROTEINS
**Amino acid:** Organic acid with a **carboxyl (–COOH)** and an **amino (–NH₂)** group on the α-carbon. General formula **R–CH(NH₂)–COOH**. **20** standard amino acids; **~9 essential**.

ASCII — amino acid & zwitterion:
```
   General form:            Zwitterion (at isoelectric pH):
        R                         R
        |                         |
   H₂N–C–COOH               H₃N⁺–C–COO⁻
        |                         |
        H                         H
   low pH: –NH₃⁺ –COOH (cation)   high pH: –NH₂ –COO⁻ (anion)
```
- At **low pH** → cation; **high pH** → anion; at **isoelectric point (pI)** → **zwitterion** (net charge 0, most stable, least soluble). Amino acids act as **buffers**.

**Essential / non-essential / partially-essential:**
- **Essential:** must come from diet (valine, leucine, lysine).
- **Non-essential:** synthesised by body (glycine, alanine).
- **Partially/semi-essential:** needed extra during growth (arginine, histidine).

### Levels of Protein Structure (★ short note)
| Level | Description | Bond |
|-------|-------------|------|
| **Primary** | Linear sequence of amino acids | Peptide bonds |
| **Secondary** | Local folding: **α-helix** & **β-pleated sheet** | Hydrogen bonds |
| **Tertiary** | 3-D folding of whole chain | H-bonds, disulphide, ionic, hydrophobic |
| **Quaternary** | Assembly of >1 polypeptide | As tertiary |

- **α-helix:** right-handed coil, ~3.6 residues/turn, **diameter ≈ 10 Å**, stabilised by intramolecular H-bonds.
- **β-pleated sheet:** extended strands side-by-side (parallel/antiparallel), H-bonded between strands.
- **Denaturation:** loss of 3-D structure (and function) by heat/pH/chemicals — 2°/3°/4° destroyed, **primary intact** (often irreversible, e.g., boiling an egg).

# MODULE 5 — ENZYMES ★★★★★

## 5.1 Core Theory
**Definition:** Enzymes are **biocatalysts** — proteinaceous substances that **speed up biochemical reactions without being consumed** and without altering the final product (Gk. *en* = in, *zyme* = yeast). ~70% of cell enzymes are in mitochondria.

- **Substrate:** reactant. **Active site:** where substrate binds.
- **Apoenzyme** (protein) + **Cofactor** = **Holoenzyme** (active). Cofactor = metal ion (**activator**) or organic **coenzyme**; tightly-bound organic cofactor = **prosthetic group**.

## 5.2 Classification of Enzymes (★ 6 classes)
| Class | Reaction | Example |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Oxidoreductases** | Transfer H/O atoms or electrons | Dehydrogenases, oxidases |
| **Transferases** | Transfer a group (phosphate/methyl) | Transaminase, kinase |
| **Hydrolases** | Hydrolysis (add water) | Esterases, digestive enzymes |
| **Isomerases** | Change molecular form | Phosphohexose isomerase |
| **Lyases** | Non-hydrolytic add/remove group | Decarboxylases, aldolases |
| **Ligases (Synthetases)** | Join molecules (new bond, ATP) | DNA ligase, citrate synthetase |

**Mnemonic:** **O-T-H-I-L-L** = Oxidoreductase, Transferase, Hydrolase, Isomerase, Lyase, Ligase.

## 5.3 Mode of Action (★ two hypotheses)
1. **Lock-and-Key (Emil Fischer, 1894):** Enzyme active site and substrate have **rigid complementary shapes** — fit like a key in a lock.
2. **Induced-Fit (Daniel Koshland):** Active site is **flexible**; substrate binding induces a conformational change that moulds the site around the substrate, straining bonds → reaction.

ASCII — enzyme action:
```
  Lock & Key:   [E▢] + ◆S  →  [E◆]  →  [E] + ▲▲ products   (rigid site fits S)
  Induced Fit:  [E ) ] + ◆S →  [E◆] (site closes around S) →  [E] + products
       E + S ⇌ ES → E + P
```

## 5.4 Enzyme Kinetics — Michaelis–Menten (★★★★★)
$$v = \frac{V_{max}\,[S]}{K_m + [S]}$$
- **Vmax:** maximum velocity (enzyme saturated).
- **Km (Michaelis constant):** substrate concentration at **half Vmax**. Low Km = high affinity; high Km = low affinity.
- Plot **v vs [S]** = rectangular hyperbola; **Lineweaver–Burk** (1/v vs 1/[S]) = straight line.

ASCII — v vs [S]:
```
  v |            ........  Vmax
    |       ....
Vmax/2|...x
    |  /
    |_/______________________ [S]
       Km
```

## 5.5 Enzyme Inhibition (★★★★★ — long Q in 2 papers)
| Type | Binds | Km | Vmax | Overcome by ↑[S]? |
|------|-------|:--:|:----:|:-----------------:|
| **Competitive** | Active site (resembles substrate) | **↑** | **Unchanged** | **Yes** |
| **Non-competitive** | Allosteric (other site) | Unchanged | **↓** | No |
| **Uncompetitive** | ES complex only | **↓** | **↓** | No |

**Mnemonic:** "**C**ompetitive **C**hanges Km (↑, Vmax same); **N**on-competitive kills **Vmax**; **U**ncompetitive lowers **both**."

## 5.6 Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity
- **Temperature:** rate ↑ to an **optimum (~37 °C human)**, then falls sharply (denaturation) — bell curve.
- **pH:** each enzyme has an **optimum pH** (pepsin ~2, trypsin ~8); activity passes through a **maximum**.
- **Substrate conc.:** rate ↑ then plateaus at Vmax (saturation).
- **Enzyme conc.:** rate ∝ [E] when substrate is in excess.
- **Inhibitors / activators.**

ASCII — pH/temp bell curve:
```
 rate |     .-''-.        ← optimum
      |   .'      '.
      | .'          '.
      |/______________\____  pH / temperature
```

## 5.7 Crisp Definitions
- **Enzyme:** Biological catalyst (usually protein) that speeds a specific reaction and is reused.
- **Coenzyme:** Non-protein organic cofactor (often vitamin-derived: NAD⁺, FAD).
- **Km:** Substrate concentration at half-maximal velocity (affinity measure).
- **Enzyme inhibition:** Decrease in enzyme activity by an inhibitor binding the enzyme.

## 5.8 Common Mistakes
- **Competitive:** Km ↑, Vmax **unchanged** (most-tested fact).
- Lock-and-key = **rigid**; induced-fit = **flexible** (Koshland).
- Enzymes lower **activation energy**; they don't change ΔG or equilibrium.

# MODULE 6 — INFORMATION TRANSFER (DNA, Central Dogma) ★★★★★

## 6.1 Core Theory
**DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid)** — first extracted by **Friedrich Miescher (1869)**, named "nuclein." Carrier of genetic information. **Watson & Crick (1953)** proposed the double-helix.

**Central Dogma (Crick):**
```
         transcription        translation
  DNA ───────────────► RNA ───────────────► PROTEIN
   ▲ │ replication
   └─┘
  (reverse transcription: RNA → DNA, e.g., retroviruses)
```

## 6.2 DNA Structure — Hierarchy (★★★★★ — asked BSC401-2022)
- **Nucleotide** = base + pentose sugar (deoxyribose) + phosphate. (Base + sugar only = **nucleoside**.)
- **Bases:** Purines = **A**denine, **G**uanine (double ring); Pyrimidines = **C**ytosine, **T**hymine (single ring). RNA uses **U**racil instead of thymine.
- **Chargaff / base pairing:** **A=T** (2 H-bonds), **G≡C** (3 H-bonds). So 30% A → T = 30%, G = C = 20% each.
- **Double helix:** two **antiparallel** strands (5'→3' & 3'→5'), right-handed, ~10 bp/turn, sugar-phosphate backbone outside, bases inside.

ASCII — DNA double helix:
```
   5'  3'
   |    |
   A====T     A=T : 2 H-bonds
   |    |
   T====A
   |    |
   G≡≡≡≡C     G≡C : 3 H-bonds
   |    |
   C≡≡≡≡G
   3'  5'   (antiparallel strands)
```
**Hierarchy:** single strand → double helix → nucleosome → chromatin → chromosome (see Module 7).

## 6.3 DNA Replication (★★★★★ — Group-C, BS-EE-301)
**Semi-conservative** (proved by **Meselson & Stahl, 1958**): each new molecule = one old + one new strand.
1. **Initiation:** **Helicase** unwinds DNA at the **origin** → replication fork; single-strand binding proteins stabilise.
2. **Elongation:** **DNA polymerase** adds nucleotides 5'→3'. **Leading strand** continuous; **lagging strand** in **Okazaki fragments** (RNA primers, joined by **DNA ligase**).
3. **Termination:** Forks meet; primers removed; gaps sealed.

```
        ─────────────────►  Leading (continuous)
   5'═══╗ origin
        ║ <helicase unwinds>
   3'═══╝
        ◄──■ ◄──■ ◄──■     Lagging (Okazaki fragments, joined by ligase)
```

## 6.4 Transcription (DNA → RNA)
- Catalysed by **RNA polymerase** (eukaryotes: **RNA Pol II** makes hnRNA/mRNA).
- Stages: Initiation (at **promoter / TATA box**) → Elongation → Termination.
- mRNA processing (eukaryotes): 5' cap, poly-A tail, **splicing** (remove introns, join exons).

## 6.5 Translation (RNA → Protein)
- On **ribosomes**. **Codon** = 3 nucleotides on mRNA coding one amino acid (64 codons, 20 amino acids — code is **degenerate**).
- **Start codon: AUG** (Methionine). **Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA.**
- **tRNA** carries amino acids; its **anticodon** pairs with the codon.
- Stages: Initiation → Elongation → Termination.

## 6.6 RNA Types & Definitions
| RNA | Role |
|-----|------|
| **mRNA** | Carries message DNA → ribosome |
| **tRNA** | Brings specific amino acid (clover-leaf, has anticodon) |
| **rRNA** | Structural part of ribosome |
- **Gene:** DNA segment coding a polypeptide/RNA.
- **Codon:** Triplet of bases specifying an amino acid.
- **TATA box:** Promoter sequence where transcription machinery binds.
- **Nine modes of information transfer** (Crick) fall into 3 classes: **General** (DNA→DNA, DNA→RNA, RNA→protein), **Special** (RNA→DNA, RNA→RNA, DNA→protein), **Unknown** (protein→DNA/RNA/protein).

## 6.7 Common Mistakes
- Replication is **semi-conservative** (Meselson–Stahl), not conservative/dispersive.
- **A pairs with T (RNA: U); G with C.** If 30% A → G = 20% (NOT 30%).
- Start codon **AUG**; central dogma is DNA→mRNA→protein (no DNA→tRNA→protein).
- RNA has **uracil & ribose**; DNA has **thymine & deoxyribose**.

# MODULE 7 — MACROMOLECULAR ANALYSIS (DNA Packaging) ★★★★☆

## 7.1 Core Theory — DNA Packaging in Eukaryotes
~2 m of DNA is packed into a ~10 µm nucleus via multiple folding levels around **histones**.
```
 DNA double helix (2 nm)
        │ wraps around histone octamer
        ▼
 NUCLEOSOME  ("beads on a string", 11 nm)
        │ coiling
        ▼
 30-nm chromatin fibre (solenoid)
        │ looping onto scaffold
        ▼
 Looped domains → CHROMOSOME (condensed, ~1400 nm at metaphase)
```

## 7.2 The Nucleosome (★ directly asked)
- **Nucleosome** = basic packaging unit: ~**147 bp of DNA wrapped ~1.65 turns around a histone octamer**.
- **Histone octamer** = **8 histone proteins** = 2 copies each of **H2A, H2B, H3, H4**.
- **H1** = "linker" histone, binds linker DNA between nucleosomes (not part of core octamer).
- EM appearance: **"beads on a string."**

## 7.3 Crisp Definitions
- **Histones:** Small basic (arginine/lysine-rich, positively charged) proteins DNA winds around.
- **Chromatin:** DNA + histones complex (interphase form).
- **Chromosome:** Highly condensed chromatin (visible at cell division).
- **Euchromatin** (loose, active) vs **Heterochromatin** (tight, inactive).

## 7.4 Common Mistakes
- Core octamer = **8** histones (2× each H2A/H2B/H3/H4). **H1 = linker.**
- Order: helix → nucleosome → 30-nm fibre → loops → chromosome.

# MODULE 8 — METABOLISM & BIOENERGETICS ★★★☆☆

## 8.1 Core Theory
**Metabolism** = sum of all chemical reactions in a cell.
- **Anabolism:** building up (synthesis), **endergonic** — photosynthesis, protein synthesis.
- **Catabolism:** breaking down, **exergonic** — respiration.
- **Amphibolic:** both roles (e.g., Krebs cycle).

## 8.2 ATP — Energy Currency (★ favourite short note)
**ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate):** the **"energy currency of the cell."** Structure = **adenine + ribose sugar + 3 phosphates** (α, β, γ). Terminal phosphate bonds = **high-energy bonds**.
- ATP ⇌ ADP + Pi + **energy** (~7.3 kcal/mol on hydrolysis).
- Uses: muscle contraction, active transport, biosynthesis, cell division, signalling/neurotransmission.
```
   Adenine ─ Ribose ─ P ~ P ~ P
                       α   β   γ   ( ~ = high-energy bond )
   ATP  ⇌  ADP + Pi + Energy
```

## 8.3 Photosynthesis (★)
Overall: **6CO₂ + 6H₂O --light/chlorophyll--> C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂**
| Reaction | Location | Inputs | Outputs |
|----------|----------|--------|---------|
| **Light reaction** | Thylakoid membrane (grana) | H₂O, light, ADP, NADP⁺ | O₂, **ATP, NADPH** |
| **Dark reaction (Calvin)** | Stroma | CO₂, **ATP, NADPH** | Glucose |
- Calvin cycle uses **ATP & NADPH** from the light reaction.
- Central element in **chlorophyll = Magnesium (Mg)**.

## 8.4 Cellular Respiration
Glucose → CO₂ + H₂O + ATP, in **mitochondria** (eukaryotes).
- **Glycolysis** (cytoplasm) → **Krebs cycle** (matrix) → **Electron transport chain** (inner membrane).
- Aerobic respiration of 1 glucose ≈ 36–38 ATP.

## 8.5 Crisp Definitions
- **Exergonic:** releases energy (–ΔG, spontaneous). **Endergonic:** absorbs energy (+ΔG).
- **Glycogenesis:** glucose → glycogen. **Glycogenolysis:** glycogen → glucose. **Gluconeogenesis:** glucose from non-carbohydrate sources.
- **Thermodynamics of living systems:** organisms are **open systems** maintaining low entropy by taking in energy and releasing heat/waste (consistent with the 2nd law overall).

# MODULE 9 — MICROBIOLOGY ★★★☆☆

## 9.1 Core Theory
**Microbiology** = study of microorganisms (bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa, algae). **Antonie van Leeuwenhoek (1676)** = "Father of Microbiology" (first observed microbes). **Louis Pasteur (1857)** proved fermentation is due to microbial activity (disproved spontaneous generation).

## 9.2 Bacteria vs Virus (★ asked)
| Feature | **Bacteria** | **Virus** |
|---------|--------------|-----------|
| Cell | Living, cellular (prokaryote) | Non-cellular (acellular) |
| Living status | Living | Border of living/non-living |
| Genetic material | DNA (+ plasmids) | DNA **or** RNA |
| Cell wall | **Peptidoglycan** | None (capsid = protein coat) |
| Reproduction | Binary fission (independent) | Only inside a host cell |
| Treatment | Antibiotics | Antivirals / vaccines |
| Beneficial example | *Lactobacillus* (curd), *Rhizobium* | Bacteriophage (kills bacteria) |

## 9.3 Immunity (★ short note)
- **Innate (natural):** present from birth, **non-specific**, immediate (skin, mucus, phagocytes, inflammation).
- **Adaptive (acquired):** develops after exposure, **specific**, has **memory** (antibodies, B-cells & T-cells).

## 9.4 Crisp Definitions
- **Antibiotics:** Substances produced by microbes that kill/inhibit other microbes. **Penicillin** discovered by **Alexander Fleming (1928)** from *Penicillium*.
- **Cancer:** Uncontrolled, abnormal cell division (loss of cell-cycle control); forms malignant tumours; can metastasise.
- **Stem cells:** Unspecialised cells that self-renew and **differentiate** into specialised types (embryonic & adult).
- **Growth media:** Nutrient substrate to culture microbes; most common = **agar plates**.
- **Single-cell protein (SCP):** Protein from cultures of yeast/bacteria/algae/fungi for food/feed.

## 9.5 Common Mistakes
- Bacterial wall = **peptidoglycan** (not cellulose).
- Antibiotics treat **bacterial**, not viral infections.
- Father of microbiology = **Leeuwenhoek**; Pasteur = fermentation/germ theory; Fleming = penicillin.
