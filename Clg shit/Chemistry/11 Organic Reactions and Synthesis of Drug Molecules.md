## Chapter at a Glance

### Free Radicals

Free radicals are reactive intermediates formed by **homolytic cleavage** of a covalent bond. These are neutral odd-electron species.

$$\text{CH}_3 : \text{Cl} \xrightarrow{\text{homolytic cleavage}} {}^{\bullet}\text{CH}_3 + {}^{\bullet}\text{Cl}$$

(CH₃• = Methyl free radical; Cl• = chlorine free radical)

### Carbocations

Groups containing a positive charge on the carbon atom are known as **carbocations**. These are formed by **heterolytic cleavage** of a covalent bond.

$$\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2 - \text{Br} \xrightarrow{\text{cleavage}} \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2^{+} + \text{Br}^{-}$$

(CH₃CH₂⁺ = Ethyl carbocation) — *(the arrow in the source is labelled "homolytic cleavage", though carbocations form by heterolytic cleavage)* `[as printed]`

### Carbanions

Groups containing a negative charge on the carbon atom are known as **carbanions**. Carbanions always contain an unshared pair of electrons.

For example: ⁻CH₃ (methyl anion), C₆H₅⁻ (Phenyl anion), C₆H₅CH₂⁻ (benzyl anion).

### Types of attacking reagents

Attacking reagents towards the organic compounds may be broadly classified into two categories:
(a) Electrophilic reagent or electrophile  (b) nucleophilic reagent or nucleophile.

**Electrophilic reagent or electrophile** — an electron-deficient species. Due to the electron deficiency it always attacks at the centre of high electron density. It is again classified into two types: (i) positive electrophile (ii) neutral electrophile.
- (i) **Positive electrophiles:** H⁺, H₃O⁺, ⁺CH₃ (carbocation) etc.
- (ii) **Neutral electrophiles:** BF₃, AlCl₃, FeCl₃, R• (free radical).

**Nucleophilic reagent or nucleophiles** — an electron-rich chemical species. Due to the presence of free electrons it always attacks at the centre of low electron density. It is also classified into three types: (i) negative nucleophile (ii) neutral nucleophile (iii) ambident nucleophile.
- (i) **Negative nucleophiles:** Cl⁻, Br⁻, CN⁻, OR⁻, OH⁻ etc.
- (ii) **Neutral nucleophiles:** H₂O, NH₃, ROH, RNH₂ etc.
- (iii) **Ambident nucleophile:** When a nucleophile contains an electron-rich centre on both sides it is called an ambident nucleophile, e.g. CN⁻ can act as a nucleophile from the carbon end as well as from the nitrogen end.

### Types of Organic reactions

The organic compounds exhibit mainly three types of chemical reactions:
- **Addition reaction:** Here a small molecule combines with an unsaturated molecule.
- **Substitution reaction:** Here an atom or group of atoms is/are replaced by another atom or group of atoms.
- **Elimination reaction:** Here a small molecule is removed from an organic compound to form an unsaturation.

#### Addition Reaction
It may be classified into two types:
- **Nucleophilic addition reaction:** Addition of HCN to an aldehyde or ketone is an example of nucleophilic addition reaction.
- **Electrophilic addition reaction:** Addition to alkenes (>C=C<) or alkynes (−C≡C−) are always electrophilic additions. The mechanism may be explained by a simple example like addition of bromine to ethylene.

#### Substitution Reaction
It may be classified into two types:
- **Electrophilic substitution reaction:** occurs in three steps. Examples: halogenation of benzene, nitration of benzene etc.
- **Nucleophilic substitution reaction:** involves the displacement of a weak nucleophile by a strong nucleophile. The rate expression suggests that the nucleophilic substitution reaction occurs by two different mechanisms:
  - (i) Substitution nucleophilic unimolecular (S<sub>N</sub>1)
  - (ii) Substitution nucleophilic bimolecular (S<sub>N</sub>2)

**S<sub>N</sub>1 (unimolecular):** A two-step process. In the first step a carbocation is formed by ionization of the substrate molecule (rate-determining step). In the second step, the nucleophile rapidly combines with the carbocation to form the final product. Example: formation of *t*-Butyl alcohol from *t*-Butyl chloride with aqueous NaOH.

**S<sub>N</sub>2 (bimolecular):** A one-step process. Formation of a new bond with the nucleophile and cleavage of the old bond with the leaving group take place simultaneously through an activated complex known as the transition state, which involves the nucleophile, the substrate and the leaving group. Example: the reaction between CH₃Cl and KOH to form CH₃OH and KCl.

#### Elimination Reaction
The elimination reaction is of two types: (i) Elimination bimolecular (E2 elimination) (ii) Elimination unimolecular (E1 elimination).

- **E2 elimination:** atoms or groups are lost simultaneously to generate an unsaturation (>C=C< or −C≡C−). One of the atoms lost is usually hydrogen. Example: formation of propene from isopropyl bromide by alcoholic KOH solution.
- **E1 elimination:** elimination occurs in two steps. In the first step a sigma bond is broken to form a stable carbocation. A β-hydrogen is then lost as H⁺ to give the product. Example: when *t*-butyl chloride is warmed with alcoholic KOH, isobutene is formed.

## Short and Long Answer Type Questions

### 2.1. Discuss about S<sub>N</sub>1 and S<sub>N</sub>2 reactions with examples. *[WBUT 2007, 2014(EVEN)]*

**Mechanism of S<sub>N</sub>1 reaction**

It is a two-step process.
- In the first step, a carbocation is formed by the ionization of the substrate molecule. This is the rate-determining step.
- In the second step, the nucleophile rapidly combines with the carbocation to form the final product.
- Example: the formation of *t*-Butyl alcohol from *t*-Butyl chloride with aqueous NaOH.

**First Step** — Tertiary butyl chloride slowly ionizes to form the stable tertiary butyl cation and chloride ion. This is the rate-determining step.

$$(\text{CH}_3)_3\text{C} - \text{Cl} \rightleftharpoons (\text{CH}_3)_3\text{C}^{+} + \text{Cl}^{-}$$

**Second Step** — The nucleophilic reagent OH⁻ combines with the carbocation to form the *t*-Butyl alcohol, the substituted product.

$$(\text{CH}_3)_3\text{C}^{+} + \text{OH}^{-} \longrightarrow (\text{CH}_3)_3\text{C} - \text{OH}$$

**Mechanism of S<sub>N</sub>2 substitution**

This type of reaction is a one-step process. Formation of a new bond with the nucleophile and cleavage of the old bond with the leaving group take place simultaneously through an activated complex known as the transition state, which involves the nucleophile, the substrate and the leaving group. Example: the reaction between CH₃Cl and KOH to form CH₃OH and KCl.

Due to permanent polarization of the C–Cl bond, the carbon atom is slightly electron deficient. The nucleophile attacks this partially electropositive carbon atom from the back side of the leaving group. A transition state is obtained where the carbon atom remains partially attached to both the incoming nucleophile and the leaving group; the centre of substitution, the nucleophile and the leaving group are on the same plane and on the same line. Finally Cl⁻ leaves the substrate molecule and its position is taken up by the nucleophile.

$$\text{HO}^{-} + \text{CH}_3\text{Cl} \longrightarrow [\text{HO}\cdots\text{CH}_3\cdots\text{Cl}]^{\ddagger} \longrightarrow \text{HO–CH}_3 + \text{Cl}^{-}$$

[diagram] back-side attack on methyl chloride: nucleophile HO⁻ → substrate → trigonal-bipyramidal transition state → substituted product, with inversion at carbon.

### 2.3. Distinguish between S<sub>N</sub>1 and S<sub>N</sub>2 reactions giving examples. *[WBUT 2009, 2010(ODD), 2015(EVEN), 2016(EVEN), 2016(ODD)]*
*Write a comparative short note on S<sub>N</sub>2 and S<sub>N</sub>1 reaction covering (i) rate equation (ii) mechanism (iii) potential energy diagram and (iv) implication of stereochemistry, if any. [WBUT 2014(ODD)]*

**i) Mechanism**
- **S<sub>N</sub>1:** A two-step process. First step is slow heterolysis of a covalent bond to form a carbocation. Second step is the rapid reaction between the carbocation and the nucleophile.
  $$(i)\ \text{R–X} \xrightarrow[\text{rds}]{\text{Slow}} \text{R}^{+} + \text{X}^{-} \qquad (ii)\ \text{R}^{+} + \text{Nu}^{-} \longrightarrow \text{R–Nu}$$
- **S<sub>N</sub>2:** One-step process. Approach of the nucleophile from the rear side of the leaving group and displacement of the leaving group occur simultaneously.
  $$\text{Nu}^{-} + \text{R–X} \xrightarrow[\text{rds}]{\text{Slow}} [\text{Nu}\cdots\text{R}\cdots\text{X}]^{\ddagger} \longrightarrow \text{Nu–R} + \text{X}^{-}$$

**ii) Kinetics**
- **S<sub>N</sub>1:** First order, because the rate-determining step involves only one species. Rate ∝ [R–X].
- **S<sub>N</sub>2:** Second order, because the rate-determining step involves two species. Rate ∝ [R–X][Nu].

**iii) Reagent Nucleophilicity**
- **S<sub>N</sub>1:** Does not depend on the reagent nucleophilicity, because first a more stable carbocation is generated and then the nucleophile attacks the carbocation.
- **S<sub>N</sub>2:** Depends on the reagent nucleophilicity, because if we increase the nucleophilicity of the nucleophile it more readily attacks the substrate and displacement of the leaving group takes place more readily.

**iv) Solvent effect**
- **S<sub>N</sub>1:** Increasing the polarity of the solvent increases the rate of the S<sub>N</sub>1 reaction.
- **S<sub>N</sub>2:** Increasing solvent polarity has a much smaller effect, resulting in a slight decrease in reaction rate.

**v) Stereochemistry**
- **S<sub>N</sub>1:** Proceeds via a planar carbocation that can be attacked from either face, giving racemization (a mixture of retention and inversion).
- **S<sub>N</sub>2:** Always leads to inversion of configuration.

### 2.9. Explain Markonikov and anti-Markonikov addition of HBr to Propene. *[WBUT 2013(EVEN)]*

**Markonikov's rule:** During the addition of a polar reagent to an unsymmetrical alkene, the negative part of the reagent (addendum) adds on to that carbon atom of the double bond which is joined to the least number of hydrogen atoms. In the case of halogen acids (HX), the halogen atom (X) is the negative part; a 2-halogenated compound is obtained from propene (CH₃CH=CH₂). Propene gives **2-bromopropane** as the major product.

$$\text{CH}_3\text{CH=CH}_2 + \text{HBr} \longrightarrow \underbrace{\text{CH}_3\text{CH(Br)CH}_3}_{\text{(Major)}} + \underbrace{\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{CH}_2\text{(Br)}}_{\text{(Minor)}}$$

(For an unsymmetrical alkene with the same number of hydrogens on the two double-bond carbons, e.g. CH₃CH₂CH=CHCH₃ + HI, a mixture of addition products in nearly equimolar proportions — CH₃CH₂CHI·CH₂CH₃ and CH₃CH₂CH₂·CHICH₃ — is formed.)

**Anti-Markonikov's rule:** Addition of HBr to an unsymmetrical alkene in the presence of oxygen or organic peroxides (benzoyl peroxide) takes place in the anti-Markownikov way. Thus propene gives **1-bromopropane** as the major product.

$$\text{CH}_3\text{CH=CH}_2 + \text{HBr} \xrightarrow{\text{Peroxide}} \underbrace{\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{CH}_2\text{Br}}_{\text{(Major)}} + \underbrace{\text{CH}_3\text{CH(Br)CH}_3}_{\text{(Minor)}}$$

Here the negative part (Br⁻) becomes joined to the carbon atom of the double bond that has the greater number of hydrogen atoms. The peroxide effect on the addition of halogen acids to an alkene is observed only with HBr; it is absent for HF, HCl and HI.

### 2.10. a) Give one example of nucleophile. *[WBUT 2014(ODD)]*

A nucleophile is an electron-rich chemical species which attacks at the centre of low electron density. Three types:
- (i) **Negative nucleophile:** Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻, CN⁻, OR⁻, OH⁻ etc.
- (ii) **Neutral nucleophile:** H₂O, NH₃, ROH, RNH₂ etc.
- (iii) **Ambident nucleophile:** contains an electron-rich centre on both sides, e.g. CN⁻ acts from the carbon end as well as from the nitrogen end:
  $$\text{R–X} + \text{KCN (alc)} \longrightarrow \text{R–C≡N} + \text{KX}\ \text{(alkyl cyanide / nitrile)}$$
  $$\text{R–X} + \text{AgCN} \xrightarrow{\text{alcohol/water}} \text{R–N=C} + \text{AgX}\ \text{(alkyl isocyanide)}$$

### 2.12. Why does benzene undergo electrophilic substitution rather than addition reaction? *[WBUT 2016(EVEN)]*

Benzene is an aromatic compound; its unusual stability comes from resonance (extensive delocalization of the π-electrons of the ring). Substitution of hydrogen atoms by other atoms/groups does not destroy the aromatic sextet, so the stability of the substituted benzene remains almost the same. An addition reaction destroys the aromatic sextet and the resonance energy (≈ 36 kcal/mole) is lost. Since stability would have to be sacrificed, benzene normally resists addition (the activation energy for addition is high).

### 2.13. Between chlorobenzene and methyl chloride, which will hydrolyse at a faster rate? *[WBUT 2016(ODD)]*

**Methyl chloride** hydrolyses faster. Due to permanent polarization of the C–Cl bond, the carbon is slightly electron deficient; the nucleophile attacks from the back side of the leaving group, passing through a transition state where C is partially bonded to both the incoming nucleophile and the leaving group, finally giving the substituted product (S<sub>N</sub>2-type, as for CH₃Cl). [diagram] back-side attack on methyl chloride.

In chlorobenzene the C–Cl bond is not easily broken because chlorobenzene is a resonance hybrid of several contributing structures (the lone pair on Cl conjugates into the ring, giving partial double-bond character to the C–Cl bond). [diagram] four resonance structures of chlorobenzene.

### 2.14. a) Which one is more stable and why? (CH₃)₃C⁺ and (CH₃)₂CH⁺ *[WBUT 2017(EVEN)]*

**(CH₃)₃C⁺** is more stable than **(CH₃)₂CH⁺** because the positive charge on the carbon is dispersed by the alkyl groups (+I effect) and by hyperconjugation/resonance. The greater the number of +I-effect groups attached to the cationic carbon, the greater the stability. The tertiary carbocation has three methyl groups attached, the secondary has two; hence the tertiary carbocation is more stable than the secondary.

### 2.14. b) Predict the products: *[WBUT 2017(EVEN)]*
CH₃–CH₂–CH(Br)–CH₃ → A + B (alcoholic KOH). Which one is the major product?

$$\text{CH}_3\text{–CH}_2\text{–CHBr–CH}_3 \xrightarrow{\text{alc. KOH}} \underbrace{\text{CH}_3\text{–CH=CH–CH}_3}_{\text{But-2-ene (major)}} + \underbrace{\text{CH}_3\text{–CH}_2\text{–CH=CH}_2}_{\text{But-1-ene (minor)}}$$

When 2-bromobutane is treated with alcoholic KOH, the reactive base is C₂H₅O⁻, formed by: OH⁻ + C₂H₅OH ⇌ C₂H₅O⁻ + H₂O. Loss of H from C₂ gives 2-butene (Saytzev's product); loss of H from C₁ gives 1-butene (Hofmann product). Alkyl halides usually give Saytzev's product (more substituted ethylene) as the major product, and the Hofmann product (less substituted ethylene) when the base is small (e.g. C₂H₅O⁻).

### 2.14. c) Nitrobenzene is less reactive than benzene in electrophilic substitution reactions. Why? *[WBUT 2017(EVEN)]*

The –I effect of the –NO₂ group in nitrobenzene decreases the electron density in the benzene ring; therefore it is less susceptible to electrophilic substitution.

- **Saytzev's elimination:** Unsymmetrical secondary or tertiary alkyl halides usually give the Saytzev's product (more substituted ethylene) when the size of the base is small.

### 2.16. a) Explain the order of reactivity towards S<sub>N</sub>1 reactions: Allyl halide, 2° halide, 1° halide, Benzyl halide, 3° halide, phenyl chloride. *[WBUT 2017(ODD)]*

Reactivity towards S<sub>N</sub>1 depends on the stability of the carbocation. A carbocation is stabilized when the charge is dispersed/delocalized. Alkyl groups are electron-donating (+I effect and hyperconjugation), so the more alkyl groups on the cationic carbon, the more stable the cation. Resonance also stabilizes carbocations: delocalization makes the benzyl and allyl carbocations about as stable as a 2° alkyl carbocation. Because the benzyl cation has a greater number of resonance structures, it is more stable than the allyl cation.

Order of reactivity towards S<sub>N</sub>1:
$$3° \text{ halide} > \text{Benzyl halide} > 2° \text{ halide} > \text{Allyl halide} > \text{phenyl chloride} > 1° \text{ halide}$$

### 2.17. a) Electron affinity of Cl is greater than F. Explain the phenomenon. *[WBUT 2018(ODD)]*

Fluorine has a smaller size than chlorine, so electron–electron repulsions in the compact 2p subshell are higher; the incoming electron is not readily accepted, and the electron affinity of fluorine is smaller. Chlorine is larger, so its outermost electrons are located further from the nucleus, allowing the incoming electron to be accommodated more easily (giving Cl the higher electron affinity).

### 2.17. b) S<sub>N</sub>1 mechanism proceeds through partial racemization of the product. Explain. *[WBUT 2018(ODD)]*

In S<sub>N</sub>1 the substrate first ionizes (slow) to a planar carbocation. Since the nucleophile can attack on either side of the planar carbocation, the S<sub>N</sub>1 reaction proceeds through partial racemization of the product. [diagram] planar carbocation attacked from both faces giving retention + inversion (racemic mixture).

### 2.17. c) Halogens are ortho-para orienting and deactivating. Give reason. *[WBUT 2018(ODD)]*

Electron-donating groups are generally ortho/para directors. Halogens (electron-withdrawing) are also ortho/para directors because they have lone pairs that are shared (by resonance) with the aromatic ring. For example, chlorine withdraws electrons through the inductive effect (–I, which destabilizes the intermediate carbocation) and releases electrons through resonance (+R, which stabilizes the intermediate carbocation). The –I effect is stronger than the +R effect, so halogens reduce the charge density on the ring and thus deactivate it, while the +R effect directs incoming electrophiles to the ortho/para positions. [diagram] resonance/inductive stabilization–destabilization of the arenium ion for ortho attack on chlorobenzene.

### 2.18. Write notes on the following:
a) Markonikov's and anti-Markonikov's rule *[WBUT 2011(EVEN), 2012(ODD)]*
b) E1 and E2 reaction *[WBUT 2012(EVEN), 2013(ODD), 2015(EVEN)]*
c) Carbocation *[WBUT 2013(EVEN)]*
d) Substitution reaction *[WBUT 2015(ODD)]*

**a) Markonikov's and anti-Markonikov's rule:** *Refer to Question No. 2.9.*

**b) E1 and E2 reaction:**

**E1 Reaction:** Elimination occurs in two steps. In the first step a sigma bond is broken to form a stable carbocation; a β-hydrogen is then lost as H⁺ to give the product. Example: *t*-butyl chloride warmed with alcoholic KOH gives isobutene.
- Step I (slow): $(\text{CH}_3)_3\text{C–Cl} \rightleftharpoons (\text{CH}_3)_3\text{C}^{+} + \text{Cl}^{-}$
- Step II ($-\text{H}^{+}$): $(\text{CH}_3)_2\text{C}^{+}\text{–CH}_2\text{H} \rightleftharpoons (\text{CH}_3)\text{C(=CH}_2)\text{–CH}_3 \ \text{(isobutene)}$

**E2 Reaction:** Atoms or groups are lost simultaneously to generate an unsaturation (>C=C< or −C≡C−); one of the atoms lost is usually hydrogen. Example — propene from isopropyl bromide with alcoholic KOH:
$$\text{CH}_3\text{CHBr–CH}_3 + \text{KOH} \xrightarrow{\text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{OH}} \text{CH}_3\text{–CH=CH}_2 + \text{KBr} + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$
OH⁻, acting as a base, takes the β-H to form H₂O while Br⁻ is lost; both processes occur in one step through a transition state. In the transition state Br and the β-H are on opposite sides (anti-periplanar), which is why this is called **trans elimination**. Because the rate-determining transition state involves both the alkyl halide and the base, the elimination is bimolecular (E2). Primary and secondary halides generally eliminate by E2.

**c) Carbocation:** Groups containing a positive charge on the carbon atom; formed by heterolytic cleavage of a covalent bond.
$$\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{–Br} \xrightarrow{\text{cleavage}} \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2^{+} + \text{Br}^{-}\ \text{(Ethyl carbocation)}$$
*(arrow labelled "homolytic cleavage" in source)* `[as printed]`
Examples: ⁺CH₃ (methyl cation), CH₃CH₂⁺ (ethyl cation), (CH₃)₂CH⁺ (isopropyl cation), (CH₃)₃C⁺ (tertiary butyl cation), C₆H₅CH₂⁺ (benzyl cation). Carbocations are classified as primary, secondary and tertiary according to the number of hydrogen atoms attached to the positively charged carbon. Stability order: **tertiary > secondary > primary** (t > s > p).

**d) Substitution reaction:** *Refer to Question No. 2.6(ii).*

### 2.19. What would be the product when HOCl adds to propene? Does it follow Markownikov's rule? *[MODEL QUESTION]*

Reactions with HOCl are usually carried out by treating the alkene with an aqueous halogen solution. In the halohydrin, the halogen atom (Cl) and the hydroxyl group (OH) are added to adjacent carbon atoms; Cl⁺ part of HOCl is the cation and OH⁻ is the anion.

$$\text{CH}_3\text{–CH=CH}_2 + \text{HOCl} \longrightarrow \text{CH}_3\text{–CH(OH)–CH}_2\text{Cl}$$

(Propene + hypochlorous acid → propylene chlorohydrin.)

**2nd part:** Addition of hypohalous acid follows Markownikov's rule — OH⁻ always goes to the carbon atom of the double bond joined to the minimum number of hydrogen atoms.

### 2.20. What will be the product when 1-chloro-2-butanol is treated with sodium hydroxide? *[MODEL QUESTION]*

$$\text{1-chloro-2-butanol} \xrightarrow[-\text{Cl}^{-}]{\text{NaOH}} \text{epoxide (2-ethyloxirane)} \xrightarrow{\text{HCl}} \text{2-chloro-1-butanol}$$

### 2.21. What will be the products formed when propylene is treated with ozone and subsequent decomposition? *[MODEL QUESTION]*

Propylene reacts with ozone to form propylene ozonide:
$$\text{CH}_3\text{–CH=CH}_2 + \text{O}_3 \longrightarrow \text{propylene ozonide}$$
On decomposition (H₂O, Zn — reductive work-up):
$$\text{propylene ozonide} \xrightarrow{\text{H}_2\text{O, Zn}} \text{CH}_3\text{CHO (Acetaldehyde)} + \text{HCHO (Formaldehyde)}$$

### 2.23. How will you synthesize paracetamol? *[MODEL QUESTION]*

Paracetamol is prepared by reacting **4-aminophenol** with **acetic anhydride**:
$$\text{4-HO–C}_6\text{H}_4\text{–NH}_2 + (\text{CH}_3\text{CO})_2\text{O} \longrightarrow \text{4-HO–C}_6\text{H}_4\text{–NHCOCH}_3 + \text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$$
(paracetamol = N-(4-hydroxyphenyl)acetamide). Mechanism: the lone pair on the aromatic –NH₂ attacks a carbonyl carbon of acetic anhydride (nucleophilic acyl substitution), forming a tetrahedral intermediate that collapses to give the acetanilide (paracetamol) and acetic acid. [diagram] amine + anhydride → tetrahedral intermediate → amide + CH₃COOH.

### 2.24. How will you prepare aspirin? *[MODEL QUESTION]*

Aspirin is prepared by reacting **salicylic acid** with **acetic anhydride**:
$$\text{salicylic acid (2-HO–C}_6\text{H}_4\text{–COOH)} + (\text{CH}_3\text{CO})_2\text{O} \longrightarrow \text{Aspirin} + \text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$$
(aspirin = acetylsalicylic acid, 2-acetoxybenzoic acid). Mechanism: the phenolic –OH of salicylic acid attacks a carbonyl of acetic anhydride (acetylation of the –OH), giving the acetyl ester (aspirin) and acetic acid. [diagram] phenol O attacks anhydride → tetrahedral intermediate → aryl acetate (aspirin) + CH₃COOH.

### 2.25. Explain oxymercuration and demercuration of propene. *[MODEL QUESTION]*

When propene reacts with mercuric acetate in a mixture of tetrahydrofuran (THF) and water, a hydroxyalkyl-mercury compound is produced (oxymercuration). This addition compound is then reduced with sodium borohydride to the alcohol (demercuration).

$$\text{CH}_3\text{–CH=CH}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{Hg(OCOCH}_3)_2 \xrightarrow{\text{THF}} \text{CH}_3\text{–CH(OH)–CH}_2\text{–HgOCOCH}_3 + \text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$$
$$\text{CH}_3\text{–CH(OH)–CH}_2\text{–HgOCOCH}_3 \xrightarrow{\text{NaBH}_4 / \text{OH}^{-}} \text{CH}_3\text{–CH(OH)–CH}_3$$

The net result of oxymercuration–demercuration is the addition of H₂O to an alkene in accordance with Markownikov's rule (giving propan-2-ol from propene). *(The OH / HgOCOCH₃ placement in the printed intermediate is transcribed as drawn.)* `[as printed]`

### 2.26. What will be the product when propylene is treated with alkaline KMnO₄? *[MODEL QUESTION]*

Potassium permanganate in alkaline medium converts alkenes to glycols:
$$\text{CH}_3\text{–CH=CH}_2 \xrightarrow[\text{NaOH}]{\text{Cold KMnO}_4} \text{CH}_3\text{–CH(OH)–CH}_2\text{(OH)}\ \text{(propane-1,2-diol)}$$
