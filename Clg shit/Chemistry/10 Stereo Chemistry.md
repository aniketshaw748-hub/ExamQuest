## Chapter at a Glance

### Isomerism

Chemical compounds with identical molar formulas but different structural formula, i.e. the arrangement of atoms in the molecules is in some way different, are known as **isomers**. Isomers have different physical and chemical properties. This phenomenon is known as **Isomerism**.

The three different hydrocarbons of $C_5H_{12}$ are:

- **n-pentane:** $CH_3CH_2\,CH_2\,CH_2\,CH_3$
- **iso-pentane:** $CH_3{-}CH({CH_3}){-}CH_2{-}CH_3$ (branched, with a $CH_3$ on C-2)
- **neo-pentane:** $CH_3{-}C(CH_3)_2{-}CH_3$ (central carbon bearing four $CH_3$ groups) `[diagram]`

### Structural isomerism

Isomerism where the isomers differ in the basic structures of the atomic framework is known as **structural isomerism**.

Structural isomerism is again subdivided into three major classes:

**a) Skeletal isomerism:** Skeletal isomers differ in the arrangement of carbon atoms in the molecules. For example: n-pentane, iso-pentane and neo-pentane represent skeletal isomerism.

**b) Positional isomerism:** Positional isomers differ in the position of a non-carbon group without any change in the carbon skeleton. They belong to the same class of compounds. For examples:

- $CH_3{-}CH_2{-}CH_2{-}OH$ (1-propanol) and $CH_3{-}CH(OH){-}CH_3$ (2-propanol)
- $CH_3{-}CH_2{-}CH{=}CH_2$ (1-butene) and $CH_3{-}CH{=}CH{-}CH_3$ (2-butene)

**c) Functional group isomerism:** Compounds that have the same molecular formula but belong to different classes of organic compounds are called functional group isomers. Functional group isomers differ widely in physical and chemical properties. For examples:

- $CH_3CH_2\,CH_2OH$ (1-propanol, alcohol) and $CH_3{-}O{-}C_2H_5$ (Methoxyethane, ether)
- $CH_3{-}CO{-}C_2H_5$ (Propanone, ketone) and $CH_3CH_2\,CHO$ (Propanal, aldehyde)

### Metamerism

When isomeric compounds of the same homologous series contain a multivalent atom like O, N etc. bearing dissimilar alkyl or aryl groups, then these isomeric compounds are known as **metamers**.

For example, three ethers of the same molecular formula $C_4H_{10}O$ are possible:

- $CH_3CH_2{-}O{-}CH_2CH_3$ (Diethyl ether)
- $CH_3{-}O{-}CH_2CH_2CH_3$ (methyl-n-propylether)
- $CH_3{-}O{-}CH(CH_3)_2$ (methyl isopropylether)

The following amines are also metamers: tri-methylamine, methyl ethylamine, and n-propylamine. `[diagram]`

### Tautomerism

Isomerism in which isomers are reversibly changing from one functional group isomer into another and remain in a dynamic equilibrium is known as **tautomerism**.

For example, ethylacetoacetate is present in keto-form and enol-form in dynamic equilibrium:

- **EAA (Keto-form):** $CH_3{-}CO{-}CH_2{-}CO{-}OC_2H_5$
- **EAA (enol-form):** $CH_3{-}C(OH){=}CH{-}CO{-}OC_2H_5$

### Stereoisomerism

The phenomenon of stereoisomerism occurs when two or more compounds have the same molecular formula and same structural formula but differ in the arrangement of the atoms in space around some chosen centre within the molecule.

Stereoisomerism is divided into two different types such as (i) Optical isomerism (b) Geometrical isomerism. `[as printed]`

Certain organic compounds are found to rotate the plane of vibration of the light when a plane-polarized monochromatic light is passed through them; such compounds are called **optically active**.

- A compound that rotates polarized light to the right or clockwise is termed **dextrorotatory** and represented by '$d$' or $(+)$.
- A compound that rotates polarized light to the left or anticlockwise is termed **laevorotatory** and represented by '$l$' or $(-)$.

### Chirality

The word *chiral* originates from the Greek work "*chier*" that means hand. For example, a right hand is a non-superimposable mirror image of the left hand. An object which is non-superimposable on its mirror image is called **asymmetric** or **chiral**.

*Example:* a chiral carbon bearing $CH_3$, $H$, $OH$ and $C_2H_5$ groups, drawn as a tetrahedral centre and its non-superimposable mirror image. `[diagram]`

Molecular chirality arises when three elements of symmetry are found to be absent. These are:

a) Plane of symmetry
b) Centre of symmetry
c) Alternating axis of symmetry

**a) Plane of symmetry:** A molecule is said to possess a plane of symmetry if an imaginary plane divides it into two halves, each half being a mirror image of the other half. It is symbolically represented by Sigma $(\sigma)$.

For example, meso-tartaric acid has a plane of symmetry: the Fischer-type structure with $HOOC$ and $H$ on each side and $OH$ groups, with a horizontal plane bisecting the molecule into two mirror-image halves. In meso-tartaric acid, one half is the mirror image of the other half, thus it contains a plane of symmetry. `[diagram]`

**b) Centre of symmetry:** A molecule has a centre of symmetry if there is a point (centre) within the molecule such that if a line drawn through that point meets an atom at a certain distance from the point, then it will meet another similar atom on the other side of the point at an identical distance. It is also known as centre of inversion and symbolically represented by $c$.

*Example:* a structure with $C_2H_5$, $H$ and $CH_3$ groups arranged about a central point of inversion. `[diagram]`

**c) Alternating axis of symmetry:** A molecule is said to have an alternating axis of symmetry when rotation of the molecule about that axis by $\dfrac{360°}{n}$ followed by reflection of a plane perpendicular to that axis produces a molecule indistinguishable from the original. The alternating axis of symmetry is also known as rotation–reflection symmetry and it is symbolically represented by $S_n$.

*Example (illustrated):* a structure $(A)$ = original, rotated by 90° about the $S_4$ axis to give $(B)$ = rotated structure, then reflected through a mirror plane to give $(C)$ = reflected structure of $(A)$, showing the $H$, $CH_3$ and $H_3C$ substituents. `[diagram]`

### Enantiomers

Optical isomers that are non-superimposable but related to each other as mirror images are called **enantiomers**.

For example, the 2,3,4-trihydroxybutanal molecule. In Fischer projection:

- **(I):** $CHO$ (top), $H{-}OH$, $H{-}OH$, $CH_2OH$ (bottom)
- **(II):** $CHO$ (top), $OH{-}H$, $OH{-}H$, $CH_2OH$ (bottom)

(I) and (II) are enantiomers (mirror images). `[diagram]`

### Diastereomers

The stereoisomers that are not mirror images of each other are called **diastereomers**.

Example: In the case of the 2,3,4-trihydroxybutanal molecule, the additional Fischer projections are:

- **(III):** $CHO$ (top), $H{-}OH$, $OH{-}H$, $CH_2OH$ (bottom)
- **(IV):** $CHO$ (top), $OH{-}H$, $H{-}OH$, $CH_2OH$ (bottom)

The pairs (I) and (III), (II) and (IV), (II) and (III), and (I) and (IV) are diastereomers of each other. `[diagram]`

### Structural representation of enantiomers: Projection formulas

It is important to know the method of representing a three-dimensional structure of an enantiomer on a two-dimensional surface. Three well-known methods are employed. They are (i) Fischer projections, (ii) Sawhorse projections and (iii) Newman projections.

**Fischer projections**

In Fischer's projections certain rules must be obeyed and certain conventions clearly understood, otherwise the structure will be misinterpreted.

(i) The longest carbon chain is written in a vertical rather than a horizontal form, and the carbon atom bearing the lower number (according to IUPAC nomenclature) is usually written at the top.
(ii) In Fischer projection the chiral or asymmetric atom under consideration lies in the plane of the paper. Two bonds are shown by two vertical lines and the other two by two horizontal lines.
(iii) Although all bonds are represented by plane straight lines, it is to be kept in mind that the two vertical bonds project below the paper and the two horizontal bonds project above the paper. This must be clearly understood when visualizing Fischer's projections. The chiral carbon is presented by the point of intersection of the vertical and horizontal lines.
(iv) For purposes of comparison between the two Fischer projections, one projection may be rotated through 180° in the plane of the paper and then placed over the other. No other manipulation like 180° out-of-the-plane rotation and 90° in-the-plane rotation are permitted.

**Sawhorse projections**

When it is necessary to know the spatial relationship between substituents attached to two adjacent carbon atoms (may be chiral or achiral), Sawhorse projections are found to be very convenient.

In Sawhorse projections, the two key carbon atoms are joined by a line which is taken to be in the plane of the paper, and the remaining bonds (represented by short lines) project above or below that plane. There is normally free rotation about the C–C bond, and the three groups attached to the front carbon atom may be rotated clockwise or anticlockwise in relation to the three groups attached to the rear carbon atom, or vice-versa.

**Newman projections**

In the Newman projection formula, the molecule is observed along the line linking the two carbon atoms (may be chiral or achiral).

The front carbon atom is represented by a dot at the centre of a circle, where the circle itself represents the back carbon atom. The line joining the two carbon atoms cannot be seen in the Newman projection formula. The three other groups are shown by bonds which appear as the spokes of a wheel at an angle of 120° for each carbon atom.

### Methods of assigning R, S — notations

For assigning R, S — nomenclature to any chiral centre of a molecule, certain conventions are to be followed:

- Identify the number of stereocentres in the molecule.
- Identify the four different groups attached to each of the stereocentres. Assign to each of the substituents on a chiral centre a priority symbol $1, 2, 3, 4$ or $a, b, c, d$ based on sequence rules, such that the decreasing order of priority is $1 > 2 > 3 > 4$ or $a > b > c > d$.
- The molecule is then viewed from the position remotest from the lowest priority group, and a hypothetical path is drawn moving from 1 to 2 and then to 3 ($1 \to 2 \to 3$) or $a \to b \to c$.

### Sequence rule for R, S- configuration

- If all four atoms directly attached to a chiral carbon are different, priority is determined by the atomic numbers of the atoms directly attached to the chiral carbon. The atom with the highest atomic number gets the highest priority. For example, $I > Br > Cl > F$.
- If two or more of the atoms directly attached to the chiral carbon are identical, the priority of these groups is determined by comparing the atomic number of the next atoms of the groups, and so on. For example, $Cl > CH_2Br > CH_2Cl > CH_3$.
- If a double or triple bond is present, then the involved atoms are treated as being duplicated or triplicated respectively:
  $${>}C{=}O \text{ equals to } {>}C\!\!\begin{array}{l}{-}O\\{-}O{-}C\end{array} \qquad {-}C{\equiv}N \text{ equals to } {-}C\!\!\begin{array}{l}{-}N\\{-}N{-}C\end{array}$$
  `[diagram — duplicated/triplicated-atom representation]`

### Erythroisomer

In Fischer projection, the **erythroisomer** is that diastereoisomer in which two similar or like groups (each on an adjacent chiral centre) are on the same side of the vertical chain of carbons.

The erythro-form of 2,3-dihydroxybutanoic acid is: $COOH$ (top), $H{-}OH$, $H{-}OH$, $CH_3$ (bottom) — the two $OH$ groups on the same (right) side. `[diagram]`

### Threoisomers

In Fischer projection, the **threoisomer** is that stereoisomer in which similar or like groups are on the opposite sides of the vertical carbon chain.

The threo-form of 3-bromo-2-hydroxylbutanedioic acid is: $COOH$ (top), $H{-}OH$, $Br{-}H$, $COOH$ (bottom) — the $OH$ and $Br$ on opposite sides. `[diagram]`

### E-, Z- system of nomenclature

When the groups of higher precedence (according to the sequence rule) are on the same side of the reference plane, i.e. the double bond, then the symbol $(Z)$ (German: *Zusammen*, meaning together) is used.

For example, $(Z)$-Butenedioic acid or maleic acid: both $HOOC$ and $COOH$ groups on the same (top) side of the $C{=}C$ double bond. `[diagram]`

When the groups of higher precedence (according to the sequence rule) are on the opposite side of the reference plane, i.e. the double bond, then the symbol $(E)$ (German: *Entgegen*, meaning opposite) is used as descriptor to specify the configuration.

Example, $(E)$-Butenedioic acid or fumaric acid: $HOOC$ and $H$ on the top, $H$ and $COOH$ on the bottom, i.e. the two $COOH$ groups on opposite sides of the $C{=}C$ double bond. `[diagram]`

## Short and Long Answer Type Questions

**2.1. a) Define specific rotation for an optically active molecule.** *[WBUT 2018(ODD)]*

**Specific Rotation:** Specific rotation is a property of a chiral chemical compound. It is defined as the change in orientation of monochromatic plane-polarized light, per unit distance–concentration product, as the light passes through a sample of a compound in solution.

A compound that rotates polarised light to the right or clockwise is termed dextrorotatory and represented by '$d$' or $(+)$. A compound that rotates polarised light to the left or anticlockwise is termed laevorotatory and represented by '$l$' or $(-)$.

**b) What is the necessary and sufficient condition for a molecule to be optically active?** *[WBUT 2018(ODD)]*

A chiral complex is optically active if its structure cannot be superimposed on its mirror image. Moreover, molecular chirality arises when three elements of symmetry are found to be absent. These are (i) plane of symmetry, (ii) centre of symmetry and (iii) alternating axis of symmetry.

**c) The following compound does not have a chiral center, still it shows optical activity — comment.** *[WBUT 2018(ODD)]*

*The compound is an allene:* $H_3C/H{-}C{=}C{=}C{-}H/CH_3$ (1,3-disubstituted allene with $H$ and $CH_3$ at each end carbon). `[diagram]`

The optical activity of allene compounds arises out of molecular dissymmetry. The minimum condition for such dissymmetry is that the substituted groups ($-H$ and $-CH_3$) at the two end carbon atoms should not be identical. The reason for the dissymmetry is that the groups $-H$ and $-CH_3$ at one end of the system lie in a plane at right angles to those of the other end. This can be explained from its orbital picture: the central carbon atom has two mutually perpendicular $p$-orbitals; the substituents at one end are in a plane which is perpendicular to that of the substituents at the other end. Consequently, the compound exists in two non-superimposable mirror images. `[diagram — orbital picture]`

**2.2. a) Draw all possible stereoisomers for butane-2,3-diol. Are all of them optically active? Give reason.** *[WBUT 2018(ODD)]*

The structure of butane-2,3-diol is $CH_3{-}CH(OH){-}CH(OH){-}CH_3$.

The molecule is symmetrical and has an even number of chiral carbon atoms, thus the number of optical isomers $= 2^{n-1} = 2^{2-1} = 2$.

$$\text{Number of mesoisomer} = 2^{\frac{n}{2}-1} = 2^{1-1} = 2^{0} = 1$$
$$\text{Total number of stereoisomers} = 2 + 1 = 3$$

The enantiomers and meso-compound (Fischer projections):

- **(I):** $CH_3$ (top), $H{-}OH$, $HO{-}H$, $CH_3$ (bottom)
- **(II):** $CH_3$ (top), $HO{-}H$, $H{-}OH$, $CH_3$ (bottom)
- **(III) Meso-compound:** $CH_3$ (top), $H{-}OH$, $H{-}OH$, $CH_3$ (bottom)

(I) and (II) are enantiomers of each other; (I) and (III), and (II) and (III), are diastereomers of each other. Not all are optically active: the meso-compound (III) is optically inactive (it has a plane of symmetry). `[diagram]`

**2.19. Write the structures of all enantiomers and diastereomers of 2-bromo-3-chloro butane.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

The structure of 2-bromo-3-chloro butane is $CH_3{-}CH(Br){-}CH(Cl){-}CH_3$.

The molecule is un-symmetrical, thus the number of optical isomers $= 2^{n} = 2^{2} = 4$; number of meso-isomers $= 0$; total number of stereoisomers $= (4 + 0) = 4$.

The four stereoisomers (Fischer projections, top→bottom $CH_3$, C-2, C-3, $CH_3$):

- **(I):** $H{-}Br$, $H{-}Cl$
- **(II):** $Br{-}H$, $Cl{-}H$
- **(III):** $H{-}Br$, $Cl{-}H$
- **(IV):** $Br{-}H$, $H{-}Cl$

(I) and (II) are enantiomers of each other; (III) and (IV) are enantiomers of each other. (I) and (III), (I) and (IV), (II) and (III), and (II) and (IV) are diastereomers of each other. `[diagram]`

**2.20. Write the structures of all enantiomers and diastereomers of tartaric acid.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

The structure of tartaric acid is $HOOC{-}CH(OH){-}CH(OH){-}COOH$.

The molecule is symmetrical and has an even number of chiral carbon atoms, thus the number of optical isomers $= 2^{n-1} = 2^{2-1} = 2$; number of mesoisomer $= 2^{\frac{n}{2}-1} = 2^{1-1} = 2^{0} = 1$; total number of stereoisomers $= 2 + 1 = 3$.

The enantiomers and meso compound (Fischer projections, top→bottom $COOH$, C-2, C-3, $COOH$):

- **(I):** $H{-}OH$, $HO{-}H$
- **(II):** $HO{-}H$, $H{-}OH$
- **(III) Meso Compound:** $H{-}OH$, $H{-}OH$

(I) and (II) are enantiomers of each other; (I) and (III), and (II) and (III), are diastereomers of each other. `[diagram]`

**2.21. Write the structure of all enantiomers and diastereomers of 2,3,4-trihydroxy-1,5-pentane dioic acid.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

The structure of 2,3,4-trihydroxy-1,5-pentanedioic acid is $HOOC{-}CH(OH){-}CH(OH){-}CH(OH){-}COOH$.

The molecule is symmetrical and has an odd number of chiral carbon atoms, thus the number of optical isomers:

$$= 2^{(n-1)} - 2^{\left(\frac{n}{2}-\frac{1}{2}\right)} = 2^{(3-1)} - 2^{\left(\frac{3}{2}-\frac{1}{2}\right)} = 4 - 2 = 2$$
$$\text{Number of meso isomers} = 2^{\left(\frac{n}{2}-\frac{1}{2}\right)} = 2^{\left(\frac{3}{2}-\frac{1}{2}\right)} = 2$$
$$\text{Total of stereo isomers} = 2 + 2 = 4$$

The four stereoisomers (Fischer projections, top→bottom $COOH$, C-2, C-3, C-4, $COOH$):

- **(I):** $H{-}OH$, $H{-}OH$, $OH{-}H$
- **(II):** $OH{-}H$, $OH{-}H$, $H{-}OH$
- **(III) meso form:** $H{-}OH$, $OH{-}H$, $H{-}OH$
- **(IV) meso form:** $OH{-}H$, $H{-}OH$, $OH{-}H$

(I) and (II) are enantiomers of each other; (I) and (III), (I) and (IV), (II) and (III), and (II) and (IV) are diastereomers of each other. `[diagram]`

**2.22. What is Sawhorse projection? Draw the Sawhorse projection for the conformation of n-butane.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

**1st Part:** When it is necessary to know the spatial relationship between the substituents attached to two adjacent carbon atoms (may be chiral, prochiral or achiral), Sawhorse projections are found to be very convenient. In this projection, the two key carbon atoms are joined by a diagonal line which is taken to be in the plane of the paper, and the remaining bonds are shown by small lines projected above and below that plane. There is normally free rotation about the C–C bond, and the three groups attached to one carbon may be rotated clockwise or anticlockwise in relation to the three groups attached to the other carbon atom.

**2nd Part:** n-butane can be represented by the following Sawhorse projection when C-2 and C-3 carbon atoms are taken as the two essential carbon atoms:

- **(I) n-butane (staggered):** the bulky methyl groups are oriented as far away from each other as possible.
- **(II) n-butane (eclipsed):** the groups on C-2 and C-3 are nearest. `[diagram]`

**2.23. What is Newman projection? Draw the Newman projection for the most stable conformation of n-butane.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

**1st Part:** In the Newman projection, the molecule is viewed along the bond joining the carbon atoms (chiral, prochiral or achiral), and these atoms are represented as superimposed circles, only one circle being drawn. The front carbon atom is represented by the centre of the circle and the circumference represents the back carbon atom. The remaining bonds on each carbon are shown by small straight lines at angles of 120° joined to the centre and to the circumference.

**2nd Part:** The Newman projection forms of n-butane are the **Staggered n-butane** and **Eclipsed n-butane**. In terms of stability, the staggered conformation is more stable than the eclipsed conformation, because in the eclipsed conformation the positioning of the atoms forces them closer together, increasing the amount of steric hindrance in the molecule. `[diagram]`
