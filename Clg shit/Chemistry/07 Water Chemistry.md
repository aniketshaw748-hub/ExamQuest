## Chapter at a Glance

### Hydrosphere

The hydrosphere includes all types of water resources such as oceans, seas, rivers, lakes, streams, reservoirs, glacier and polar ice caps, and ground water.

- About **97%** of the earth's water supply is in the oceans, where the high salt content does not permit its use for human consumption.
- About **2%** of the water resource is locked in the glaciers and polar ice caps.
- Only **2%** is available as freshwater (surface water, rivers, lakes, streams and ground water) for human consumption and other uses.

The ground water is usually of good quality and thus can be used for drinking and for day-to-day use. The other sweet water often contains suspended matters, undesirable dissolved salts as well as micro-organisms which is harmful for health. This water cannot be used for drinking purpose. The major uses of water are for irrigation (30%) and thermal power plants (50%). Other uses are for domestic (7%) and industrial consumption (≈ 12%).

### The hydrological cycle

The hydrological cycle is a continuous natural process, which helps in exchange of water between the atmosphere, the land, the sea, the living plants and animals.

The hydrological cycle consists of five parts which provide the global water supply:

1. Massive evaporation of water from the oceans.
2. Cloud formation
3. Precipitation
4. Surface run-off and
5. Ground water movements.

### Oxygen Demanding Wastes

One of the most important measures of water quality is the amount of dissolved oxygen (DO) present in it. The saturated value of DO in water is in the order of **8 – 15 mg/L**, depending on temperature and salinity. Optimum DO required for healthy fish and other aquatic life in natural water is **5 – 8 mg/L**, or as low as **3 mg/L** for less desirable species, such as corp.

The oxygen demanding wastes are substances that oxidize in the receiving body of water reducing the amount of dissolved oxygen (DO) available in water.

**Origin:** Oxygen demanding wastes are usually biodegradable organic substances contained in municipal waste waters or effluents from certain industries, such as food processing and paper production. The oxidation of certain inorganic compounds may also contribute to the oxygen demanding wastes.

**Effects of drop of DO:**
1. If DO drops, fish and other aquatic life are threatened and in the extreme case, killed.
2. The effect of reduced DO is undesirable odors, tastes, and colours, reduce acceptability of water as a domestic supply and recreational purpose.

**Measurement of Oxygen Demand:**
1. **Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD):** It is the amount of oxygen required to chemically oxidize the wastes.
2. **Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD):** It is the amount of oxygen required by microorganisms to biologically degrade the wastes.

### Nutrients

Nutrients are chemicals, such as nitrogen, phosphorus, carbon, sulphur, calcium, potassium, iron, manganese, boron and cobalt, that are essential for the growth of living things. The nutrients can be considered as pollutants when concentration are sufficient to allow excessive growth of aquatic plants, particularly algae.

**Effects:**
1. When nutrients stimulate the growth of algae, the attractiveness of water for recreational uses such as drinking water supply and as a viable habitat for other living things can be adversely affected.
2. Nutrient enrichment can lead to blooms of algae, which prevents the entry of sunlight into water bodies. Aquatic plants, along with algae thus die, the bacteria present in water now decompose all these dead plants.
3. Their decomposition removes oxygen from water which ultimately reduces the level of DO in water. The depletion of dissolved oxygen leads to the death of fishes, other aquatic animals and plants.
4. Algae and decaying organic matter add colour, odour, turbidity and objectionable taste to water that are difficult to remove and that may greatly reduce its acceptability as a domestic water source.

### Limiting Nutrients

Plants require relatively large amounts of each of three most important nutrients e.g. carbon, nitrogen and phosphorus which control the growth of algae blooms. The nutrient that is least available relative to the plants need is called the limited nutrient. Carbon is not the limited nutrient, as it is usually available from a number of natural sources like dissolved carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and decaying organic matter.

**Sources:**
1. Major sources of both nitrogen and phosphorus are municipal waste water discharges, run off from animal feed lots and chemical fertilizers.
2. The major unusual sources of nitrogen and phosphorus are soaps and detergents, chemical fertilizers like urea, ammonium sulphate or nitrate, super phosphate etc.
3. Certain bacteria and blue-green algae can obtain nitrogen directly from the atmosphere.
4. Certain forms of acid rain can also contribute nitrogen to lakes.

**Effect of Limiting Nutrients:**
1. When nitrogen is found in drinking water a particular form of it can pose a serious public health threat.
2. Nitrogen in water is commonly found in the form of nitrates ($\text{NO}_3^-$). Certain bacteria can convert nitrates ($\text{NO}_3^-$) to highly toxic nitrites ($\text{NO}_2^-$).
3. Haemoglobin has greater affinity for nitrites ($\text{NO}_2^-$) compared to oxygen, therefore it leads to replacement of oxygen by nitrites ($\text{NO}_2^-$), causing a bluish discolouration of the infant, which is commonly referred to as the **"blue baby" syndrome**.

### Eutrophication

Eutrophication is a process whereby water bodies, such as lakes or slow moving streams, receive excess nutrients that stimulate excessive plant growth. This enhanced plant growth, often called an algal bloom, reduces dissolved oxygen in the water when dead plant material decomposes and can cause other organisms to die. Nutrients can come from many sources, such as fertilizers applied to agricultural fields, golf courses, and suburban lawns; deposition of nitrogen from the atmosphere; erosion of soil containing nutrients and sewage treatment plant discharges. Water with a low concentration of dissolved oxygen is called hypoxic.

**Adverse effect of eutrophication:**
1. Increased biomass of phytoplankton.
2. Increases in blooms of gelatinous zooplankton.
3. Decreases in water transparency.
4. Taste, odour, and water treatment problems.
5. Dissolved oxygen depletion.
6. Increased incidences of fish kills.
7. Loss of desirable fish species.
8. Decreases in perceived aesthetic value of the water body.

### Salts

Water naturally accumulates a variety of dissolved solids or salts. Generally, the cations in the salt are $\text{Na}^+$, $\text{Ca}^{2+}$, $\text{Mg}^{2+}$ and $\text{K}^+$ and the anions are $\text{Cl}^-$, $\text{SO}_4^{2-}$ and $\text{HCO}_3^-$. More commonly used measure of salinity is the concentration of total dissolved solids (TDS).

- Fresh water contains 1500 mg/lit TDS.
- Brackish water may have TDS up to 5000 mg/lit.
- Saline water has TDS above 5000 mg/lit.
- Sea water contains 30,000–35,000 mg/lit TDS.

Drinking water has a recommended maximum TDS of **500 mg/lit** as per WHO's specification, but for Indian conditions, drinking water having TDS up to 1500 mg/lit has been permitted under certain conditions.

**Sources:**
- (a) Irrigation drainage water is always higher in salinity than the supply water and with every reuse, its salt concentration increases more.
- (b) Discharge of high concentration of salt from industries.
- (c) Removal of freshwater by evaporation, also causes salinity problems.

**Remedy:** The only approach for removal of salt from water is desalination. Different desalination technologies are available such as ion exchange resin, reverse osmosis and electrodialysis, but due to advancement of membrane technology the later two processes are energetically and economically favourable.

### Hardness of water

The presence of multivalent cations, specially calcium, magnesium, strontium, aluminium, ferrous and manganous ions are responsible for hardness of water.

**Effect of hard water:** When hard water is heated, calcium carbonate $[\text{CaCO}_3]$ and magnesium hydroxide $[\text{Mg(OH)}_2]$ readily precipitated out from the solution, forming scale which ultimately affect water pipes, reduces the efficiency of water heaters, boilers and heat exchangers.

**Types of hardness:** Hardness are classified into temporary hardness and permanent hardness.
- (i) **Temporary or carbonate hardness** is due to the dissolved salts of bicarbonates of calcium and magnesium and can be removed by boiling.
- (ii) **Permanent or non carbonate hardness** is mainly due to the dissolved salts of chlorides and sulphates of calcium and magnesium.

**Expression of hardness:** Hardness of water is conveniently expressed in terms of parts of calcium carbonate hardness in million ($10^6$) parts of water (ppm).

Thus $X$ ppm of a compound contributing to hardness = $X$ parts of the compound present in $10^6$ parts of water.

$$= \frac{X \times 50}{E} \times \text{Parts of CaCO}_3 \text{ equivalents present in } 10^6 \text{ parts of water}$$

where $E$ = Equivalent weight of the compound.

### Types of water treatment

Water treatment for softening may be broadly classified into two types: (a) internal treatment, (b) external treatment.

**(a) Internal Treatment:** When water is used in boilers, the residual hardness can be eliminated by an internal treatment known as conditioning which means treatment of water within the boiler with chemicals which may capable of forming a sludge, easily removable by blow down operation or a water soluble co-ordination complex of very low in stability constant, with metal ions, regarded as scale formers. The technique of conditioning being strictly internal.

Different types of internal treatment are:
- (i) colloidal conditioning method
- (ii) phosphate conditioning method
- (iii) carbonate conditioning method
- (iv) Calgon conditioning method.

**(b) External treatment:** External treatment of hard water may be of three types:
- (i) Lime-soda process
- (ii) Zeolite process
- (iii) Ion exchange process.

**2.2. What is the reason for alkalinity in water?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

The alkalinity of natural waters primarily due to the salts of weak acids, although weak or strong bases may contribute. Bicarbonates represent the major form of alkalinity, since they are formed in considerable amounts from the action of carbondioxide upon basic minerals in the soil as shown in the following reaction.

$$\text{CO}_2 + \text{CaCO}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{Ca(HCO}_3)_2$$

The bicarbonates along with carbonates and hydroxides which are responsible for the alkalinity of water can be explained by the following reactions.

**Alkalinity:**

1. Reaction (1): $\quad \text{CO}_2 + \text{CaCO}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{Ca(HCO}_3)_2$

2. Reaction (2): $\quad \text{HCO}_3^- + \text{H}^+ \rightarrow \text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O}$

$$\text{CO}_3^{2-} + \text{H}^+ \rightarrow \text{HCO}_3^-$$
$$\text{OH}^- + \text{H}^+ \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

Other minor contributors to the alkalinity of water are salts of weak acids, such as borates, silicates and phosphates.

A few organic acids that is quite resistant to biological oxidation — for example humic acid — form salts that add alkalinity to the natural water. In polluted or anaerobic waters, salts of weak acids such as acetic, propionic and hydrosulphuric may be produced and would also contribute to alkalinity. In other cases, ammonia or hydroxides may make a contribution to the total alkalinity of water.

The alkalinity of water is mainly due to salts of weak acids and strong bases and these substances act as buffers to resist a drop in pH resulting from the acid addition. Alkalinity is thus a measure of buffer capacity and in this sense is used to a great extent in waste water treatment practice.

**2.3. What is the reason for acidity in water?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

The acidity due to dissolved $\text{CO}_2$ is called $\text{CO}_2$ acidity. Mineral acidity is present in many industrial wastes, particularly those of metallurgical industry and some production of synthetic organic materials. Certain natural water may also contain mineral acidity. The drainage from abandoned mines will contain significant amounts of sulphuric acid or salt of sulphuric acid if sulphur, sulphides or iron pyrites are present. Conversion of these materials to $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ and sulphates is facilitated by sulphur oxidizing bacteria under aerobic conditions.

The acidic character of some hydrated metal ion may contribute to acidity as shown in the following reactions.

**Acidity:**

$$2\text{S} + \text{O}_2 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O} \xrightarrow{\text{bacteria}} 2\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$$
$$\text{FeS}_2 + 3\tfrac{1}{2}\text{O}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{FeSO}_4 + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$$

Salts of heavy metals, particularly those of trivalent metal ions such as $\text{Fe}^{+3}$ and $\text{Al}^{+3}$ hydrolyse in water to release mineral acidity.

$$\text{FeCl}_3 + 3\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{Fe(OH)}_3 + 3\text{H}^+ + 3\text{Cl}^-$$

**2.5. How does carbonate and non carbonate hardness can be removed by lime soda process? What are the advantages and disadvantages of lime soda process?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

Either quick lime ($\text{CaO}$) or hydrated lime ($\text{Ca(OH)}_2$) is added to the water, raising the pH to about 10.3, and converting soluble $\text{HCO}_3^-$ ions into insoluble $\text{CO}_3^{2-}$ which precipitate out as $\text{CaCO}_3$.

**Carbonate:**

$$\text{Ca(HCO}_3)_2 + \text{Ca(OH)}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{CaCO}_3 \downarrow + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$$
$$\text{Mg(HCO}_3)_2 + 2\text{Ca(OH)}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{CaCO}_3 \downarrow + \text{Mg(OH)}_2 \downarrow + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

For removal of non carbonate hardness the reagent is a mixture of quick lime or slacked lime and 98% $\text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3$, the lime being taken in slight excess.

**Non-Carbonate:**

$$\text{CaCl}_2 + \text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3 \rightarrow \text{CaCO}_3 \downarrow + 2\text{NaCl}$$
$$\text{CaSO}_4 + \text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3 \rightarrow \text{CaCO}_3 \downarrow + \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4$$
$$\text{MgCO}_3 + \text{Ca(OH)}_2 \rightarrow \text{Mg(OH)}_2 \downarrow + \text{CaCO}_3 \downarrow$$
$$\text{MgCl}_2 + \text{Ca(OH)}_2 \rightarrow \text{Mg(OH)}_2 \downarrow + \text{CaCl}_2$$
$$\text{MgSO}_4 + \text{Ca(OH)}_2 \rightarrow \text{Mg(OH)}_2 \downarrow + \text{CaSO}_4 \downarrow$$

**Advantages:**
- (i) The process is very economical.
- (ii) Treated water is alkaline and hence has less corrosion tendencies.
- (iii) It removes not only hardness causing salts but also minerals.
- (iv) Due to alkaline nature of treated water, amount of pathogenic bacterias in water is considerably reduced.
- (v) Iron and manganese are also removed from the water to some extent.

**Disadvantages:**
- (i) It requires careful operation and skilled supervision for economical and efficient softening.
- (ii) Sludge disposal is a problem.
- (iii) Water softened by this method contains appreciable concentrations of soluble salts, such as sodium sulphate and cannot be used in high pressure boilers.

**2.6. What is permutit or zeolite process? Which materials are used in the zeolite bed? How does the hardness bearing salts are removed in zeolite process? How will you regenerate zeolite bed? What are the advantages and disadvantages of zeolite process?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

Permutits or zeolites are a class of naturally occurring or synthetically prepared base exchanging silicates capable of removing both types of hardness by exchanging sodium of the zeolite with $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ and $\text{Mg}^{2+}$ of water in equivalent amounts.

Two naturally occurring zeolites, natrolite ($\text{Na}_2\text{O}, \text{Al}_2\text{O}_3, 3\text{SiO}_2, 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$) and analcine ($\text{Na}_2\text{O}, \text{Al}_2\text{O}_3, 4\text{SiO}_2, 3\text{H}_2\text{O}$) are the most common variety in use for the purpose.

For softening of water by zeolite process hard water is percolated at a specified rate through a bed of zeolite, housed in a cylindrical unit. Zeolite holds sodium ions loosely and can be simply represented as $\text{Na}_2\text{Ze}$ where Ze represents zeolite. The hardness causing ions ($\text{Ca}^{2+}$, $\text{Mg}^{2+}$ etc.) are retained by the zeolite as $\text{CaZe}$ and $\text{MgZe}$ respectively, while the outgoing water contains sodium salts. In the process, the water becomes free from $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ and $\text{Mg}^{2+}$.

The softening reactions may be represented as follows:

$$\text{Na}_2\text{Ze} + \text{Ca(HCO}_3)_2 \rightarrow \text{CaZe} + 2\text{NaHCO}_3$$
$$\text{Na}_2\text{Ze} + \text{Mg(HCO}_3)_2 \rightarrow \text{MgZe} + 2\text{NaHCO}_3$$
$$\text{Na}_2\text{Ze} + \text{CaCl}_2 \text{ or (CaSO}_4) \rightarrow \text{CaZe} + 2\text{NaCl (or Na}_2\text{SO}_4)$$

After sometimes the zeolite is completely converted into calcium and magnesium zeolites. The exhausted zeolite is converted into sodium zeolite by washing with a weak solution of sodium chloride.

**Regeneration:**

$$\text{CaZe (or MgZe)} + 2\text{NaCl} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{Ze} + \text{CaCl}_2 \text{ (or MgCl}_2)$$

**Advantages of zeolite process:**
- (i) Hardness is nearly completely removed and water of about 10 ppm hardness is produced.
- (ii) The equipment used is compact and occupies less space.
- (iii) It is quite clean and rapid process.
- (iv) Less requirement of skilled maintenance.
- (v) Allows wide range of temperature fluctuations.
- (vi) No sludge formation.
- (vii) Simultaneous removal of Fe (ous) impurities.
- (viii) Operation expenses are lower than that of the lime soda process.

**Disadvantages:**
- (i) If the supplied water is turbid, the suspended matter must be removed before the water is fed to the zeolite bed.
- (ii) Mineral acids present in water will destroy the zeolite bed and the mineral acid must be neutralized by soda.
- (iii) If water contains large quantities of $\text{Mn}^{2+}$ and $\text{Fe}^{2+}$, they must be removed first because these ions produce manganese and iron zeolite which are very difficult to be regenerated.
- (iv) There is always a risk of disintegration of the bed with consequent loss of activity.
- (v) Lead does not seem to be completely exchanged by the technique.
- (vi) Anions are not removed by the process.

**2.7. Which one is the most efficient process for removal of hardness of water? Discuss the process with suitable advantages and disadvantages.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

The most efficient process for removal of hardness of water is ion exchange process.

**Process:** In the demineralisation process, the raw water is first forced through a bed of cation exchanger where the metal ions in water are replaced by hydrogen ions and the water become distinctly acidic. It next passes through an anion exchanger column where the anions are replaced by $\text{OH}^-$ in amounts equivalent to $\text{H}^+$, already formed in the cation exchanger, so that the effluent leaving the anion exchanger is exactly neutral with no trace of mineral impurities. It passes next a degasification unit to be free from dissolved $\text{CO}_2$ and $\text{O}_2$ and then stored.

**Cation exchange resin:** They are mainly sulphonated styrene resins, sulphonated phenol formaldehyde resins or carboxylic acid resin. Resins of this type can exchange all cations from water through hydrogen exchange as a result of which water becomes distinctly acidic after treatment.

$$\text{M(HCO}_3)_2 + 2\text{RSO}_3\text{H} \rightarrow (\text{RSO}_3)_2\text{M} + 2\text{CO}_2 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$$
$$\text{MSO}_4 + 2\text{RSO}_3\text{H} \rightarrow (\text{RSO}_3)_2\text{M} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$$
$$\text{MCl}_2 + 2\text{RSO}_3\text{H} \rightarrow (\text{RSO}_3)_2\text{M} + 2\text{HCl}$$

**Anion exchange resin:** They are polyamine formaldehyde resin, styrene-divinyl benzene copolymer, which contain basic functional groups such as amine, quarternary ammonium or quarternary phosphonium or tertiary sulphonium groups as an integral part of resin matrix. These after treatment with dilute $\text{NaOH}$ solution, become capable of exchange in their $\text{OH}^-$ anions in water and therefore they are known as anion exchange resin.

$$[\text{M} = \text{Ca, Mg, Fe}]$$
$$\text{RNH}_2 + \text{HOH} \rightarrow \text{RN}\overset{(+)}{\text{H}}_3\,\overset{(-)}{\text{OH}}$$
$$\text{RN}\overset{(+)}{\text{H}}_3\,\overset{(-)}{\text{OH}} + \text{HX} \rightarrow \text{RN}\overset{(+)}{\text{H}}_3\,\overset{(-)}{\text{X}} + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$
$$(\text{X} = \text{Cl}^-, \text{HSO}_4^- \text{ etc})$$

**Regeneration — Cation exchange resins:** Regeneration of the cation active bed is carried out by reversing the exchange reaction.

$$(\text{RSO}_3)_2\text{M} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 \text{ (excess)} \rightarrow \text{MSO}_4 + 2\text{RSO}_3\text{H}$$
$$2\text{RSO}_3\text{Na} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 \text{ (excess)} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4 + 2\text{RSO}_3\text{M}$$

**Regeneration — Anion exchange resins:** Regeneration of the anion exchange bed is carried out by a strong $\text{NaOH}$ solution.

**Advantages:**
- (i) Water obtained is of extra pure quality.
- (ii) No extra power requirement and no heating.
- (iii) Long service of the resin bed due to high mechanical and chemical stability.
- (iv) The process can be used to soften highly acidic or alkaline waters.
- (v) Sugar juices with mineral constituents can be processed on such beds.
- (vi) It produces water of very low hardness.
- (vii) Formaldehyde contaminated with formic acid can be purified on an anion exchanger.

**Disadvantages:**
- (i) Capital cost is high since chemical and equipment both are costly.
- (ii) If water contains turbidity then the efficiency of the process is reduced.

**2.8. Describe hydrological cycle.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

The hydrological cycle is a continuous natural process, which helps in exchange of water among the atmosphere, the land, the sea, the living plants and animals.

It consists of five steps which maintain the global water supply:
- a) Massive evaporation of water
- b) Cloud formation
- c) Precipitation
- d) Surface run-off and
- e) Ground water movements.

About 66% of the solar flux absorbed by the earth is used to drive the hydrological cycle i.e., massive evaporation of water from the ocean, seas, river, lakes, ponds etc occurs. The water vapours in the atmosphere form clouds and are transported through moving air masses. When the clouds meet with the cold air, condense and precipitated as rain due to gravitational pull.

Water as rain or snow is precipitated on all land and water surfaces. Water on land surface reaches to the ground water through the soil by the impervious layer of rock and clay.

Below ground there is a natural water level or water table. Water moves up above the table by capillary action and thereby maintain a continuous supply of water to the surface layer of soil, where it is absorbed by plant roots in absence of rain.

**2.12. "Excessive nutrient concentration in water is responsible for water pollution." — Explain.** *OR,* **What are the effects of excessive nutrient concentration in water?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

Nutrients are chemicals, such as nitrogen, phosphorous, carbon, sulphur, calcium, potassium, iron, manganese, boron and cobalt, that are essential for the growth of living things. **2.15. How the TDS from water can be removed by reverse osmosis process?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

In reverse osmosis process, demineralised water is produced by forcing water through semi-permeable membranes.

In ordinary osmosis, if a vessel is divided by a semi-permeable membrane, and one compartment is filled with water and other with concentrated salt solution, water diffused through the membrane towards the compartment containing salt solution until the difference in water levels on the two sides of the membrane creates a sufficient pressure to counteract the original water flow.

- The difference in levels represents the osmotic pressure of the solution.
- The process can be reversed by applying sufficient pressure called hydrostatic pressure to the concentrated solution to overcome osmotic pressure, the flow of solvent reverses as it is forced to move from concentrated side to the dilute side across the membrane.
- The solute concentration builds up in one side of the membrane while relatively pure water passing through the membrane.
- In order to obtain adequate solvent flux through the membrane, pressure of the order of 4000–7000 kN/m² is required.
- Membranes are cellulose acetate, PMMA and etc.

**2.16. What is biochemical oxygen demand? How will you determine the biochemical oxygen demand of water?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

**1st Part:** Biochemical oxygen demand is the amount of oxygen required by micro-organisms to biologically degrade the waste.

The aerobic decomposition of wastes present in water by micro-organisms produces non-objectionable, stable and products such as $\text{CO}_2$, sulphate ($\text{SO}_4^{2-}$), orthophosphate ($\text{PO}_4^{3-}$) and nitrate ($\text{NO}_3^-$).

$$\text{Organic matter} + \text{O}_2 \xrightarrow{\text{Microorganisms}} \text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{New cells} + \text{Stable products}$$

BOD is often expressed in milligram/litre.

**2nd Part:** The BOD of water is determined through 5-day BOD test. The 5 days BOD test or $\text{BOD}_5$ is the total amount of oxygen consumed by micro-organisms during the first 5-day of biodegradation.

- The test is performed over a 5 day period at 20°C in 300 mL bottles incubated in the dark to prevent interference from algal growth.
- A sample of waste water is taken in a stoppered bottle.
- The concentration of dissolved oxygen is determined at the beginning of the test.
- The concentration of dissolved oxygen is determined after 5-day period.

The difference is dissolved oxygen would be the 5-days BOD. It is usually necessary to dilute the sample to keep final dissolved oxygen above zero.

The 5 days BOD of a diluted sample is given by

$$\text{BOD}_5 = \frac{\text{DO}_i - \text{DO}_f}{P}$$

where $\text{DO}_i$ = the initial dissolved oxygen of the diluted waste water; $\text{DO}_f$ = the final dissolved oxygen of the diluted waste water.

$$P = \text{the diluted fraction} = \frac{\text{Volume of waste water}}{\text{Volume of waste water} + \text{Dilution water}}$$

As standard BOD bottle holds 300 mL, so $P$ is just the volume of waste water divided by 300 mL.

**2.17. Describe seeded BOD test and derive the formula:**
$$\text{BOD}_w = \frac{(\text{DO}_i - \text{DO}_f) - (B_i - B_f)(1 - P)}{P}$$
*[MODEL QUESTION]*

In some cases it is necessary to seed the dilution water with micro-organisms to assure there is an adequate bacterial population to carry out biodegradation.

- Two bottles will be prepared, one containing just the seeded dilution water (blank) and the other containing the mixture of both the waste water and seeded dilution water.

The oxygen demand of the waste ($\text{BOD}_a$) can there be determined as:

$$\text{BOD}_m V_m = \text{BOD}_w \times V_w + \text{BOD}_d V_d \qquad \ldots(1)$$

where, $\text{BOD}_m$ = BOD of the mixture of waste water and seeded dilution water; $\text{BOD}_w$ = BOD of waste water alone; $\text{BOD}_d$ = BOD of seeded dilution water alone; $V_w$ = The volume of waste water in the mixture; $V_d$ = The volume of dilution water in the mixture; $V_m$ = The volume of the mixture $= V_d + V_w$.

Let, $P$ = Fraction of waste water in the mixture $= \dfrac{V_w}{V_m}$.

Thus, $1 - P$ = Fraction of seeded dilution water in the mixture $= \dfrac{V_d}{V_m}$.

Thus from equation (1),

$$\text{BOD}_w = \text{BOD}_m \times \frac{V_m}{V_w} - \text{BOD}_d \times \frac{V_d}{V_w}$$

$$= \text{BOD}_m \times \frac{V_m}{V_w} - \text{BOD}_d \left[\frac{V_d}{V_m} \times \frac{V_m}{V_w}\right] = \text{BOD}_m \times \frac{V_m}{V_w} - \text{BOD}_d \times \frac{\frac{V_d}{V_m}}{\frac{V_w}{V_m}}$$

$$= \frac{\text{BOD}_m}{P} - \text{BOD}_d \times \frac{1 - P}{P} = \frac{\text{BOD}_m - \text{BOD}_d(1 - P)}{P}$$

Here, $\text{BOD}_m = \text{DO}_i - \text{DO}_f$ and $\text{BOD}_d = B_i - B_f$,

where $\text{DO}_i$ = Initial dissolved oxygen of the mixture of waste water and seeded dilution water; $\text{DO}_f$ = Final dissolved oxygen of the mixture of waste water and seeded dilution water after 5-day period; $B_i$ = Initial dissolved oxygen in the seeded dilution water (Blank); $B_f$ = Final dissolved oxygen in the seeded dilution water (Blank).

**2.18. Prove that** $\text{BOD}_t = C_a\left(1 - e^{-Kt}\right)$**.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

$\text{BOD}_t$ = Biochemical oxygen demand after time $t$.
$C_a$ = The total amount of $\text{O}_2$ required by the micro-organisms to oxidize the waste.
$K$ = BOD reaction rate constant.

The aerobic decomposition of wastes present in the water by micro-organisms may be represented as:

$$\text{Organic matter} + \text{O}_2 \xrightarrow{\text{Microorganisms}} \text{Product}$$

Assuming first order reaction

$$-\frac{dc}{dt} \propto C$$
$$\text{or,} \quad -\frac{dc}{dt} = KC \qquad \ldots(1)$$

$-\dfrac{dc}{dt}$ is the rate of change of concentration of $\text{O}_2$. $C$ is the concentration of oxygen at any time $t$, $K$ is a constant i.e., velocity constant of BOD reaction rate ($\text{time}^{-1}$). If we assume $C_a$ is the initial value of dissolved oxygen, then from equation (1)

$$-\frac{dc}{C} = K\,dt$$

On integration $-\displaystyle\int \frac{dc}{C} = K \int dt$

$$\text{or,} \quad -\ln C = Kt + Z \qquad \ldots(2), \ (\text{Integration constant})$$

At time $t = 0$, $C = C_a$, then from equation (2), we get $Z = -\ln C_a$.

Hence, $-\ln C = Kt - \ln C_a$

$$\text{or,} \quad \ln \frac{C}{C_a} = -Kt$$
$$\text{or,} \quad C = C_a e^{-Kt}$$
$$(C_a - C) = C_a\left(1 - e^{-Kt}\right)$$

Since $C_a$ is the total amount of $\text{O}_2$ required by the micro-organisms to oxidize the organic waste, $C$ is the amount of $\text{O}_2$ consumed after time $t$. $(C_a - C)$ is the biochemical oxygen demand after time $t$.

$$(C_a - C) = \text{BOD}_t = C_a\left(1 - e^{-Kt}\right).$$

**2.20. What is chemical oxygen demand? Why $\text{KMnO}_4$ is not used for determination of COD of water? How will you determine the COD of water?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

**1st Part:** Chemical oxygen demand is the amount of oxygen required to chemically oxidize the waste present in water.

In COD test the organic compounds present in the water can be fully oxidized to carbon dioxide, water and ammonia.

$$\text{Organic compounds} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{NH}_3$$

The expression does not include the oxygen required due to oxidation of ammonia to nitrate.

$$\text{NH}_3 + 2\text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{NO}_3^- + \text{H}_3\text{O}^+$$

The process by which ammonia is converted to nitrate is known as nitrification.

**2nd Part:** Previously, the strong oxidizing agent potassium permanganate ($\text{KMnO}_4$) was used for measurement of chemical oxygen demand. Potassium permanganate's effectiveness for oxidizing organic compounds varies widely, and in many cases it is formed that the values for BOD is much greater than that of the values of COD measurement which indicates that potassium permanganate was not able to effectively oxidize all organic compounds in water. Therefore, potassium permanganate is not used as oxidizing agent for COD measurement.

**3rd Part:** To determine COD, a fixed volume with known excess of strong oxidizing agent, $\text{K}_2\text{Cr}_2\text{O}_7$ in 50% sulphuric acid in presence of $\text{Ag}_2\text{SO}_4$ catalyst and $\text{HgSO}_4$ (to eliminate interference due to chloride) is added to the sample solution being analysed.

- The resultant mixture is refluxed to oxidize the organic substances present in water.
- Once oxidation is complete the amount of excess potassium dichromate will be determined through the titration with ferrous ammonium sulphate solution, until the entire excess oxidizing agent has been reduced to $\text{Cr}^{+3}$.
- The titration is carried out in presence of ferrous indicator. Once all of the excess dichromate has been reduced, the ferrous indicator changes from blue-green to reddish green.
- A blank titration is carried out to eliminate the accidental presence of organic substances in distilled water.

The amount of ferrous ammonium sulphate added is equivalent to the amount of excess $\text{K}_2\text{Cr}_2\text{O}_7$ added to the original sample.

$$\text{Thus, COD (mg/L)} = \frac{(V_1 - V_2) \times N \times 8 \times 1000}{S}$$

where $V_1$ is the initial volume of ferrous ammonium sulphate solution of normally $N$ required for blank and $V_2$ for remaining untreated dichromate solution, $S$ is the volume of solution taken for testing.
