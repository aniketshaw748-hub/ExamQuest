## Chapter at a Glance

### Electrochemical Cell

Electrochemical cell is an arrangement in which two metal rods (called electrodes), either dipped in the solution of same electrolyte or placed separately in two different electrolyte solutions. They are connected by a salt bridge. When the electrodes are externally connected by a metallic wire, current flow from one electrode to the other and inside the cell chemical changes occur at the surface of the electrodes. The electrode where reduction occurs is called the cathode or positive electrode and where oxidation takes place is called the anode or negative electrode.

### Types of electrochemical cell

Electrochemical cell may be of two types
- Electrolytic cell
- Galvanic cell

### Electrolytic cell

It is a device in which the electrical energy is converted into chemical energy through chemical reaction.

**Example:** Electrolysis of acidified water by using platinum electrode is an example of electrolytic cell.

### Galvanic cell

A galvanic cell is a device in which the free energy of a chemical process is converted into electrical energy. Usually such cells consist of two electrodes partially immersed in one or more suitable electrolytes.

When the electrodes are externally connected by a metallic wire, a current flow from one electrode to another and inside the cell chemical changes occur at the surface of the electrodes.

### Reaction in Galvanic cell

During the production of current, zinc atoms pass into solution as $\text{Zn}^{++}$ ions leaving behind two electrons each in the metal electrode. On the other hand copper ions from the solution are deposited on the copper electrode by accepting two electrons each from the metal. So there is an excess of electrons in the zinc electrode and less electron in the copper electrode.

$$\text{Zn(s)} \longrightarrow \text{Zn}^{++} + 2e \quad \text{(Oxidation)} \qquad \ldots(1)$$
$$\text{Cu}^{++} + 2e \longrightarrow \text{Cu(s)} \quad \text{(Reduction)} \qquad \ldots(2)$$

When the circuit is closed, the electrons from zinc flow to copper through the wire. Therefore the current is produced.

By adding (1) and (2) we get the net cell reaction
$$\text{Zn} \longrightarrow \text{Zn}^{++} + 2e$$
$$\text{Cu}^{++} + 2e \longrightarrow \text{Cu}$$
$$\overline{\text{Zn} + \text{Cu}^{++} \longrightarrow \text{Zn}^{++} + \text{Cu}}$$

### Representation of a Galvanic cell

- An electrode of the Galvanic cell is represented by writing the symbol for the metal/non-metal followed by that of the solution or the ion in which the metal/non-metal is immersed.
- The two species i.e. ions or molecules present in the same phase are separated by a comma.
- The phase boundary between two miscible liquids is represented either by a single dotted vertical line or double solid vertical line which indicates the salt bridge between the two electrolytic solutions used in the electrodes.
- It is the usual convention to write the negative electrode of the cell on the left and the positive electrode to the right in representing a cell.
- The concentration of the substances involved are represented within first bracket followed symbols/formulas of the ions/solutions/solids/gases etc.

The Galvanic Cell is represented as
$$\overset{(-)}{\text{Zn}} \mid \text{Zn}^{++} (\text{Conc } C_1) \parallel \text{Cu}^{++} (\text{Conc } C_2) \mid \overset{(+)}{\text{Cu}}$$

### Salt bridge

Salt bridge is usually an inverted U-tube filled with concentrated solution of inert electrolytes. An inert electrolyte is one whose ions neither involved in any electrochemical change nor do they react chemically with the electrolytes in two half-cells. Generally salts like $\text{KCl}$, $\text{KNO}_3$, and $\text{K}_2\text{SO}_4$ etc. are used. For the preparation of salt bridge, gelatin or agar-agar is dissolved in a hot concentrated aqueous solution of an inert electrolyte and solution thus formed is filled in the U-tube. On cooling the solution set in the form of a gel in the U-tube. The ends of the U-tube are plugged with cotton wool as to minimize diffusion effects.

### Reversible cell

In reversible cell the chemical reaction occurring in the cell may proceed in either direction depending upon direction of flow of current. When the circuit of a cell is closed a reaction occurs in the cell and a current is obtained. If we apply a driving force from an external EMF, so as to cause a current to flow in the opposite direction the chemical action is exactly and fully reversed. When these requirements are satisfied, the cell is reversible.

### Irreversible cell

When the chemical process cannot be exactly reversed by the application of external EMF the cell is irreversible.

### Standard Electrode Potential

Standard electrode potential is the electrode potential of all the species involved in an electrode is in their standard states of unit activity with reference to standard hydrogen electrode. The standard states may be considered as: (a) 1.0 M concentration of each ion in the solution (b) 1 atmospheric pressure for each gas and (c) 25°C or 298K temperature. The standard electrode potential of an electrode is represented by $E^0$.

### Significance of the sign of the standard electrode potential

- If standard electrode potential ($E^0$) of an electrode is positive then reduction occurs in the electrode and the electrode is designated as cathode or (+) ve electrode.
- If standard electrode potential ($E^0$) of an electrode is negative then oxidation occurs in the electrode and the electrode is designated as anode or (−) ve electrode.

### Assignment of (−) ve and (+) ve electrode in a cell

- If the standard reduction potential of one electrode in a cell is (+) ve and another electrode in the cell is (−) ve, then the (+) ve electrode is considered as cathode (reduction occurs) and (−) ve electrode is considered as anode (oxidation occurs).
- If the standard reduction potential of both the electrodes are (+) ve, then the electrode which has more (+) ve value will be considered as (+) ve electrode or cathode (reduction occurs) and the electrode which has less (+) ve value will be considered as (−) ve electrode or anode (oxidation occurs).
- If the standard reduction potential of both the electrodes are (−) ve, then the electrode which has more (−) ve value will be considered as (−) ve electrode or anode (oxidation occurs) and the electrode which has less (−) ve value will be considered as (+) ve electrode or cathode (reduction occurs).

### Standard EMF a Cell

Standard EMF is the EMF of a cell when all the substances present in the cell or participate in the cell reaction are in their standard states of unit activity. The standard EMF of the cell can be computed from the standard electrode potentials of the individual electrodes.

EMF of a Cell can be computed by determining the reduction potential of the positive electrode or cathode and the reduction potential of the negative electrode or anode in the following way.

- The EMF of the cell is obtained by subtracting the reduction potential of the negative electrode from the reduction potential of the positive electrode.

Hence
$$E_{\text{Cell}} = (E_{\text{reduction}})_{+} - (E_{\text{reduction}})_{-}$$

### Nernst equation

Nernst equation is used to determine the EMF of a cell. Let us consider a reaction in a Galvanic Cell where $n_1$ moles of reactant A reacts with $n_2$ moles of another reactant B producing $n_3$ moles of D and $n_4$ moles of E at a given temperature T and activity $a$.

$$n_1 A + n_2 B \rightleftharpoons n_3 D + n_4 E$$

Then according to Nernst equation,
$$E = E^0 - \frac{0.05913}{n} \log \frac{a_D^{\,n_3} \times a_E^{\,n_4}}{a_A^{\,n_1} \times a_B^{\,n_2}}$$

**Advantages of quinhydrone electrode**
- It is simple and easier to set up.
- It does not require constant attention.
- Equilibrium is quickly attained, hence it is not so time consuming.
- A small quantity of solution is needed.
- Air need not be eliminated.
- It can be used in presence of a) heavy ions b) unsaturated substances.

**Limitations quinhydrone electrode**
- In alkaline medium hydroquinone undergoes arial oxidation to form phenoxide ion. Hence it cannot be used in alkaline medium. So it is not of such a general use as $\text{H}_2$ - electrode.
- It is found that when, pH < 7.8 the half cell potential is positive; pH > 7.8 the half cell potential is negative; pH = 7.8 the half cell potential is zero.
- It cannot be used if any other red-ox system is present in experimental solution.
- It cannot be used in solution of very high activity of ions.

## Short and Long Answer Type Questions

**2.1. Discuss the physicochemical principle involved in the measurement of pH of an aqueous solution by using a hydrogen electrode.** *[WBUT 2006, 2011(ODD), 2014(ODD), 2016(EVEN)]*
**OR, Derive the pH of an electrochemical cell with the help of Nernst equation.** *[WBUT 2012(EVEN)]*

**Hydrogen Half Cell**

**Construction**

A solution containing $\text{H}^+$ is taken in a pyrex vessel provided with lid. Though the lid a pt-wire is introduced which carry a pt-foil coated with pt-black and kept immersed in solution, pure $\text{H}_2$ gas at pressure P is continuously bubbled so that the pt-black and the solution are saturated and equilibrium is reached. Guard tube is fitted to ensure that external atmospheric gases do not diffuse in the system. Temperature is maintained constant by placing it in a thermostat.

> *[diagram]* A pyrex vessel containing the $\text{H}^+$ solution, with $\text{H}_2$ gas (P atm) bubbled in through a tube, a platinized Pt foil immersed in the solution, and a guard tube.

**Representation**
$$\text{Pt, H}_2(g, P\text{-atm}) \mid \text{H}^+$$

**Reaction**

The actual reaction for hydrogen half cell is
$$\text{H}^+ + e \rightleftharpoons \tfrac{1}{2}\text{H}_2(g)\ (P\text{-atm.}) \quad \text{(Reduction)}$$

**Expression of potential**

From Nernst equation we get, the reduction potential
$$E_{\text{red}} = E^0_{\text{H}^+ \to \text{H}_2} - \frac{RT}{F} \ln \frac{a_{\text{H}_2(g)}^{1/2}}{a_{\text{H}^+}}$$
$$= E^0_{\text{H}^+ \to \text{H}_2} - \frac{RT}{F} \ln a_{\text{H}_2}^{1/2} + \frac{RT}{F} \ln a_{\text{H}^+}$$
$$= E^0_{\text{H}^+ \to \text{H}_2} - \frac{RT}{2F} \ln a_{\text{H}_2} + \frac{RT}{F} \ln a_{\text{H}^+}$$
$$= E^0_{\text{H}^+ \to \text{H}_2} - \frac{RT}{2F} \ln P_{\text{H}_2} + \frac{RT}{F} \ln a_{\text{H}^+}$$

($P_{\text{H}_2} \to$ Pressure at which $\text{H}_2$ gas is passed)

**Discussion**

1) The hydrogen half cell potential depends on (a) temperature (b) activity of $\text{H}^+$ ion (c) pressure at which $\text{H}_2$ gas is passed.

2) If $\text{H}_2$ gas at one atmosphere pressure is kept in constant with a solution containing $\text{H}^+$ ion of unit activity then it is called standard or normal hydrogen electrode half cell (SHE). Here $E_{\text{H}_2} = E^0$ $\because a_{\text{H}^+} = 1$ and $P_{\text{H}_2} = 1$.

Then $E^0$ is called standard hydrogen electrode potential (SHEP). By convention this potential $E^0$ is arbitrarily taken a zero at all temperature. Thus for
$$\text{Pt, } \underset{p=1\,\text{atm}}{\text{H}_2(g)} \mid \text{H}^+(a_{\text{H}^+} = 1), \quad E = E_{\text{H}_2} = 0 \text{ volt.}$$

3) Thus
$$E = -\frac{RT}{2F} \ln P_{\text{H}_2} - \frac{RT \times 2.303}{F}\,\text{pH}$$

So pH of the solution can be ascertained by measuring the HEP.

**2.2. What is reference electrode? Explain the working principle of one reference electrode.** *[WBUT 2010(EVEN), 2011(EVEN)]*

**1st Part:**

**Reference Electrode:** An electrode whose electrode potential is accurately known or whose electrode potential has been arbitrarily fixed.

Example: Calomel electrode, hydrogen electrode.

**2nd Part:**

**Calomel Electrode:**

**Construction**

The electrode consists of a glass vessel A to which one side arm B is attached for making electrical contact and another arm C for insertion of any desired solution into the bottom of A. A Pt-wire is sealed into the bottom of A over which are placed in turn a layer of specially purified mercury and paste of mercury and calomel and then the appropriate KCl solution saturated with calomel was filled into the cell.

> *[diagram]* A glass vessel with a platinum wire sealed at the bottom, layered (from bottom up) with mercury, a paste of $\text{Hg} + \text{Hg}_2\text{Cl}_2$, and KCl solution saturated with $\text{Hg}_2\text{Cl}_2$; side arm B for electrical contact and arm C for solution insertion.

**Representation**
$$\text{Hg}(l) - \text{Hg}_2\text{Cl}_2(s) \mid \text{Cl}^- \text{ (Saturated with Hg}_2\text{Cl}_2)$$

**Reaction**
$$2\text{Hg} \rightleftharpoons 2\text{Hg}^+ + 2e$$
$$2\text{Hg}^+ + 2\text{Cl}^- \rightleftharpoons \text{Hg}_2\text{Cl}_2$$
$$\overline{2\text{Hg}(l) + 2\text{Cl}^- \rightleftharpoons \text{Hg}_2\text{Cl}_2(s) + 2e}$$

**Expression of Potential**

Let the actual process occurring at the electrode be reduction, then the electrode reaction will be $\text{Hg}_2\text{Cl}_2(s) + 2e \to 2\text{Hg}(l) + 2\text{Cl}^-$.

Then the electrode potential will be given by
$$E_{\text{red}} = E^0_{\text{Hg}_2\text{Cl}_2 \to \text{Hg}} - \frac{RT}{2F} \ln \frac{a_{\text{Hg}(l)}^2 \times a_{\text{Cl}^-}^2}{a_{\text{Hg}_2\text{Cl}_2(s)}}$$
$$= E^0_{\text{Hg}_2\text{Cl}_2 \to \text{Hg}} - \frac{RT}{2F} \ln a_{\text{Cl}^-}^2 = E^0_{\text{Hg}_2\text{Cl}_2 \to \text{Hg}} - \frac{RT}{F} \ln a_{\text{Cl}^-}$$

**Discussion**

(1) Electrode potential depends upon the activity of KCl solution used, usually three standard calomel electrode are used in the laboratory with reduction potential.

| Concentration of KCl | Saturated | 1.0 N | 0.1 N |
|---|---|---|---|
| Electrode Potential (reduction) | 0.2415 V | 0.2800 V | 0.3338 V |

(2) If $a_{\text{Cl}^-} = 1$ then $E_{\text{red}} = E^0_{\text{Hg}_2\text{Cl}_2 \to \text{Hg}} \Rightarrow$ standard calomel potential. Because of the experimental difficulties in the preparation of $\text{H}_2$ – Half Cell, calomel half cell is extensively used as reference half cell. As it is standard and not primary standard $\text{H}_2$ half cell it is called secondary standard half cell.

(3) It can be easily prepared and can attain reversibility readily. Hence it is extensively used over $\text{H}_2$ electrode.

**2.4. What is calomel electrode? How can we measure the pH of a solution using calomel electrode?** *[WBUT 2012(ODD), 2017(EVEN)]*

**Calomel electrode:** Here mercury with one of its sparingly soluble salt mercurous chloride in contact with a solution of potassium chloride saturated with mercurous chloride will form calomel electrode.

**pH measurement:** The experimental solution whose pH is to be measured is taken in a beaker. A pinch of quinhydrone (which is equimolar mixture of compound quinone $\text{C}_6\text{H}_4\text{O}_2$ and Hydroquinone $\text{C}_6\text{H}_4(\text{OH})_2$ are held together by hydrogen bonding. Consequently there is decrease in co-association) is added to it, with stirring to ensure saturation. Then a bright Pt wire (or foil) is suspended in the solution. In solution quinhydrone breaks up to equimolecular proportion of quinone and hydroquinone such that
$$a_{\text{Q}} = a_{\text{H}_2\text{Q}}$$

Thus we have both quinine (Q) and reduction product of hydroquinone ($\text{H}_2\text{Q}$) present together in solution, containing $\text{H}^+$ ions. Consequently the following oxidation and reduction leading to occur,
$$\text{i.e. } \text{H}_2\text{Q} \rightleftharpoons \text{Q} + 2\text{H}^+ + 2e$$

> *[diagram]* Structural formulae showing hydroquinone (benzene ring with two OH groups) $\rightleftharpoons$ quinone (ring with two =O groups) $+\ 2\text{H}^+ + 2e$.

This indeed a red-ox system, so the immersed Pt wire records a potential. Evidently a red-ox half cell known as quinhydrone half cell is set up, $\text{Pt} \mid$ quinhydrone, $\text{H}^+$ of experimental solution. Thus $\text{H}_2\text{Q}$ electrode is then coupled with a saturated calomel electrode, so as to be a complete cell. The liquid junction potential is eliminated by means of a suitable salt bridge. In this combination $\text{H}_2\text{Q}$ acts as the $\oplus$ ve electrode. Hence the complete cell is represented as:
$$\text{Hg} \mid \text{HgCl}_2(s) \mid \text{saturated KCl solution} \parallel \text{Q, H}_2\text{Q (Saturated) experimental solution} \mid \text{Pt}$$

As reduction takes place at cathode hence,
$$\text{Q} + 2\text{H}^+ + 2e \Rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{Q}$$

Reduction potential $E_{\text{red}} = E^0_{\text{Q} \to \text{H}_2\text{Q}} - \dfrac{RT}{F} \times 2.303\,\text{pH}$. As the calomel electrode acts as the (−) ve electrode and consequently involved oxidation types of reduction, its potential will be $E_{\text{calomel (ox)}}$, hence the net EMF,
$$E_{\text{cell}} = (E_+) - (E_-) = E^0_{\text{Q} \to \text{H}_2\text{Q}} - 0.059\,\text{pH} - E_{\text{calomel (reduction)}}$$
$$\text{pH} = \frac{-E_{\text{cell}} + E^0_{\text{Q} \to \text{H}_2\text{Q}} - E_{\text{calomel (reduction)}}}{0.059}$$

$E_{\text{cell}}$ is measured experimentally while $E_{\text{calomel}}$ and $E^0_{\text{Q} \to \text{H}_2\text{Q}}$ can be obtained from standard reduction potential table. Hence pH can be calculated.

**2.5. Calculate the EMF of the following cell:** *[WBUT 2015(ODD)]*
$\text{Ni} \mid \text{Ni}^{2+}(1M) \parallel \text{Pb}^{2+}(1M) \mid \text{Pb}$ at 25°C. Given standard electrode potentials of Ni and Pb are −0.24V and −0.13V respectively at 25°C.

The reduction potential value of $\text{Ni} \mid \text{Ni}^{2+} = -0.24\text{V}$
The reduction potential value of $\text{Pb} \mid \text{Pb}^{2+} = -0.13\text{V}$

The electrode reaction (reduction) of the right hand electrode is $\text{Pb}^{2+} + 2e \longrightarrow \text{Pb}$
$$E_{\text{red}} = E^0_{\text{Pb}} - \frac{RT}{2F} \ln \frac{a_{\text{Pb}}}{a_{\text{Pb}^{2+}}} = -0.13 + \frac{0.0592}{2} \log a_{\text{Pb}^{2+}} \quad [a_{\text{Pb}} = 1]$$
$$= -0.13 + 0.0296 \log 1 = -0.13\text{V}$$

The electrode reaction (reduction) of the left hand electrode is $\text{Ni}^{2+} + 2e \longrightarrow \text{Ni}$
$$E_{\text{red}} = E^0_{\text{Ni}} - \frac{RT}{2F} \ln \frac{a_{\text{Ni}}}{a_{\text{Ni}^{2+}}} = -0.24 + \frac{0.0592}{2} \log a_{\text{Ni}^{2+}}$$
$$= -0.24 + 0.0296 \log 1 = -0.24\text{V}$$

Hence $E_{\text{Cell}} = (E_{\text{reduction}})_+ - (E_{\text{reduction}})_- = (-0.13 + 0.24)\text{ V} = 0.11\text{ V}$

**2.8. Derive the relation of EMF of cell with $\Delta G$ and $\Delta H$.** *[WBUT 2018(ODD)]*

**Relation between EMF of a cell and $\Delta G$:**

If $E$ be the EMF of a cell supplying a quantity of electricity $Q$, then the electrical energy is $EQ$.

If each equivalent of reactant involved is associated one faraday, $F$ of electricity. Then for $n$ equivalent of reaction occurs, electrical energy produced $= nFE$.

Again, according to Helmholtz, "the decrease in free energy of the process is equal to the electrical energy obtained from a Galvanic cell".

So, $-\Delta G = nFE$
or, $\Delta G = -nFE$

So, as '$n$' and '$F$' is known, measuring EMF ($E$) of the cell we can measure $\Delta G$.

**Relation between EMF of a cell and $\Delta H$:**

From Gibbs-Helmholtz equation we know that
$$\Delta G = \Delta H + T\left[\frac{\partial(\Delta G)}{\partial T}\right]_P$$
or, $-nFE = \Delta H + T(-nF)\left(\dfrac{\partial E}{\partial T}\right)_P$
$$\therefore \Delta H = -nFE + TnF\left(\frac{\partial E}{\partial T}\right)_P$$
$$\therefore \Delta H = nF\left[T\left(\frac{\partial E}{\partial T}\right)_P - E\right]$$
where, $\dfrac{\partial E}{\partial T}$ = Temperature coefficient of the cell.

**a) Nernst's electrode potential:**

Let us consider a reaction in a Galvanic Cell where, $n_1$ moles of reactant A reacts with $n_2$ moles of another reactant B producing $n_3$ moles of D and $n_4$ moles of E at a given temperature T and activity $a$.
$$n_1 A + n_2 B \rightleftharpoons n_3 D + n_4 E$$

The change in the thermodynamic potential of the system due to the chemical reaction
$$\Delta G_T = \sum G_{\text{resultant}} - \sum G_{\text{reactant}} \quad [\because G = \text{free energy of the respective species}]$$
$$= (G_D + G_E) - (G_A + G_B)$$
$$= (n_3 \mu_D + n_4 \mu_E) - (n_1 \mu_A + n_2 \mu_B) \quad [\because G_i = n_i \mu_i,\ n_i = \text{number of moles of } i\text{th species}]$$
$$= \{n_3(\mu_D^0 + RT \ln a_D) + n_4(\mu_E^0 + RT \ln a_E)\} - \{n_1(\mu_A^0 + RT \ln a_A) + n_2(\mu_B^0 + RT \ln a_B)\}$$
$$= [(n_3 \mu_D^0 + n_4 \mu_E^0) - (n_1 \mu_A^0 + n_2 \mu_B^0)] + [(RT \ln a_D^{n_3} + RT \ln a_E^{n_4}) - (RT \ln a_A^{n_1} + RT \ln a_B^{n_2})]$$
$$= \Delta G^0 + RT \ln \frac{a_D^{\,n_3} \times a_E^{\,n_4}}{a_A^{\,n_1} \times a_B^{\,n_2}} \qquad \ldots(1)$$
$[\mu_i = \mu_i^0 + RT \ln a_i,$ where $a_i$ = activity of the $i$th component$]$

The decrease in the free energy of the process is equal to the electrical energy obtained from a Galvanic Cell i.e.
$$-\Delta G = EQ = EnF$$

[Where, E is the EMF of the cell supplying a quantity of electricity Q. Such that the electrical energy $= EQ$. $F$ is the faraday of the electricity associated with one equivalent of reactants. $n$ = the no. of equivalents participating in the reaction]

Thus $\Delta G = -nFE$ $\qquad \ldots(2)$
So $\Delta G^0 = -nFE^0$ $\quad (E^0 = $ EMF of the standard cell$)$

Substituting (2) in equation (1) we get
$$-nFE = -nFE^0 + RT \ln \frac{a_D^{\,n_3} \times a_E^{\,n_4}}{a_A^{\,n_1} \times a_B^{\,n_2}}$$
$$E = E^0 - \frac{RT}{nF} \ln \frac{a_D^{\,n_3} \times a_E^{\,n_4}}{a_A^{\,n_1} \times a_B^{\,n_2}}$$

This relation is known as Nernst equation
$$\therefore E = E^0 - \frac{2.303 \times 8.314 \times 298}{96500 \times n} \log \frac{a_D^{\,n_3} \times a_E^{\,n_4}}{a_A^{\,n_1} \times a_B^{\,n_2}} = E^0 - \frac{0.05913}{n} \log \frac{a_D^{\,n_3} \times a_E^{\,n_4}}{a_A^{\,n_1} \times a_B^{\,n_2}}$$

This form of Nernst equation is used in calculation.

$$\overline{\text{Ce}^{4+} + 4e \to \text{Ce}}$$
$$E^0_{\text{Ce}^{3+}/\text{Ce}} = -2.48\text{V} \quad (-\text{ve Electrode})$$
$$E^0_{\text{Ce}^{4+}/\text{Ce}^{3+}} = 1.61\text{V} \quad (+\text{ve Electrode})$$
Thus $E^0_{\text{Ce}^{4+}/\text{Ce}} = E^0_{\text{Ce}^{4+}/\text{Ce}^{3+}} - E^0_{\text{Ce}^{3+}/\text{Ce}}$

**2.24. What is the EMF of the following cell at 25°C?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*
$\text{Zn(s)} \mid \text{Zn}^{2+}(0.01M) \parallel \text{Ag}^+(0.1M) \mid \text{Ag(s)}$
Given that the standard reduction potentials of $\text{Zn} \mid \text{Zn}^{2+}$ and $\text{Ag} \mid \text{Ag}^+$ half cells are −0.76V and +0.80V respectively.

The standard reduction potential of $\text{Zn}^{2+} \mid \text{Zn}$ electrode is $= -0.76\text{V}$
The standard reduction potential of $\text{Ag}^+ \mid \text{Ag}$ electrode is $+ 0.80\text{V}$

Hence $\text{Zn}^{2+} \mid \text{Zn}$ is anode or negative electrode and $\text{Ag}^+ \mid \text{Ag}$ is cathode or positive electrode.

By convention oxidation occurs in anode or negative electrode
$$\text{Zn} \longrightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+} + 2e \qquad \ldots(1)$$
and reduction occurs in cathode or positive electrode
$$\text{Ag}^+ + e \longrightarrow \text{Ag} \qquad \ldots(2)$$
By adding (1) and (2) we get the cell reaction
$$(1) \times 1 \Rightarrow \text{Zn} \longrightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+} + 2e$$
$$(2) \times 2 \Rightarrow 2\text{Ag}^+ + 2e \longrightarrow 2\text{Ag}$$
$$\overline{\text{Zn} + 2\text{Ag}^+ \longrightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+} + 2\text{Ag}}$$

The electrode reaction reduction in the LHE is $\text{Zn}^{2+} + 2e \longrightarrow \text{Zn}$
$$(E_{\text{Zn}^{2+}/\text{Zn}})_- = E^0_{\text{Zn}^{2+}/\text{Zn}} - \frac{0.05912}{2} \log \frac{a_{\text{Zn}}}{a_{\text{Zn}^{2+}}} = -0.76 - 0.0295 \log \frac{1}{0.01} \quad [\text{Since } a_{\text{Zn}} = 1]$$
$$= -0.76 - 0.059 = -0.819\text{V}$$

The electrode reaction reduction in the RHE is $\text{Ag}^+ + e \longrightarrow \text{Ag}$
$$(E_{\text{Ag}^+/\text{Ag}})_+ = E^0_{\text{Ag}^+/\text{Ag}} - \frac{0.05912}{1} \log \frac{a_{\text{Ag}}}{a_{\text{Ag}^+}} = 0.80 - 0.05912 \log \frac{1}{0.1} \quad [\text{Since } a_{\text{Ag}} = 1]$$
$$= 0.80 - 0.05912 = 0.741\text{ V}$$

Thus EMF of the cell is
$$E_{\text{cell}} = (E_{\text{Ag}^+/\text{Ag}})_+ - (E_{\text{Zn}^{2+}/\text{Zn}})_- = 0.741 - (-0.819) = 1.56\text{ V.}$$

**2.26. The standard reduction potential for $\text{Sn}^{2+} + 2e \to \text{Sn}$ and $\text{Sn}^{4+} + 4e \to \text{Sn}$ are −0.136 V and 1.05 V respectively. Calculate $E^0$ and $\Delta G^0$ for $\text{Sn}^{4+} + 2e \to \text{Sn}^{2+}$.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

and reduction occurs in cathode or positive electrode
$$\text{Sn}^{4+} + 4e \longrightarrow \text{Sn} \qquad \ldots(2)$$
By adding (1) and (2) we get the total cell reaction

$$\text{Sn}^{4+} + 4e \longrightarrow \text{Sn}$$
$$\overline{\text{Sn}^{4+} + 2e \longrightarrow \text{Sn}^{2+}}$$

**2.28. Calculate the equilibrium constant K for the following reaction at 298 K** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

**2.29. Calculate the maximum work that can be accomplished by the operation of the following cell at 25°C** *[MODEL QUESTION]*
$\text{Ni(s)} \mid \text{Ni}^{2+}(0.01M) \parallel \text{Cu}^{2+}(0.1M) \mid \text{Cu(s)}$
Given $E^0_{\text{Ni}^{2+}/\text{Ni}} = -0.15\text{V}$ and $E^0_{\text{Cu}^{2+}/\text{Cu}} = +0.34\text{V}$.

Here $\text{Cu}^{2+}/\text{Cu}$ is positive electrode or cathode and $\text{Ni}^{2+}/\text{Ni}$ is negative electrode or anode.

By convention oxidation occurs in anode or negative electrode
$$\text{Ni} \longrightarrow \text{Ni}^{2+} + 2e \qquad \ldots(1)$$
and reduction occurs in cathode or positive electrode
$$\text{Cu}^{2+} + 2e \longrightarrow \text{Cu} \qquad \ldots(2)$$
By adding (1) and (2) we get the total cell reaction
$$\text{Ni} \longrightarrow \text{Ni}^{2+} + 2e$$
$$\text{Cu}^{2+} + 2e \longrightarrow \text{Cu}$$
$$\overline{\text{Ni} + \text{Cu}^{2+} \longrightarrow \text{Ni}^{2+} + \text{Cu}}$$

The electrode reaction in terms of reduction in anode is $\text{Ni}^{2+} + 2e \longrightarrow \text{Ni}$
$$E_{\text{Ni}^{2+}/\text{Ni}} = E^0_{\text{Ni}^{2+}/\text{Ni}} - \frac{0.05912}{2} \log \frac{a_{\text{Ni}}}{a_{\text{Ni}^{2+}}} \quad [\text{since } a_{\text{Ni}} = 1]$$
$$= -0.15 + 0.0296 \log a_{\text{Ni}^{2+}} = -0.15 + 0.0296 \log 0.01 = -0.2092\text{ V}$$

**2.30. How does the equilibrium constant of a reversible chemical reaction is determined from standard EMF of the cell?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

Let us consider a reversible chemical reaction
$$n_1 A + n_2 B + \cdots \rightleftharpoons n_3 D + n_4 E + \cdots$$

The equilibrium constant of this reaction is related to the standard free energy change of the process as
$$-RT \ln K_{(eq)} = \Delta G^0 \qquad \ldots(1)$$
But standard free energy change is related to the standard EMF of the cell as
$$\Delta G^0 = -nFE^0 \qquad \ldots(2)$$
From (1) and (2) we have
$$-RT \ln K_{(eq)} = \Delta G^0 = -nFE^0$$
or, $\log K_{(eq)} = \dfrac{nFE^0}{RT \times 2.303}$
or, $K_{(eq)} = 10^{\frac{nFE^0}{RT \times 2.303}}$

So knowing $E^0$ and the temperature we can calculate the equilibrium constant.

**2.31. How does the valency of an ion is determined from the EMF of a cell?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

Let us construct an electrolyte concentration cell without transference such that the activity of the metal ion of the left hand electrode is 10 times greater than that of the right hand electrode.

The cell is given by $M \mid \overset{}{M^{+n}(a)} \parallel \overset{}{M^{+n}(a/10)} \mid M$

So EMF of the left hand electrode is
$$E_1 = E^0 - \frac{0.059}{n} \log a_1 \qquad \ldots(1)$$
So EMF of the right hand electrode is
$$E_2 = E^0 - \frac{0.059}{n} \log \frac{a}{10} \qquad \ldots(2) \quad [\text{Where '}n\text{' is the valency of ion}]$$
Subtracting,
$$E_2 - E_1 = \frac{0.059}{n} \log \frac{a}{a/10} = \frac{0.059}{n} \log 10 = \frac{0.059}{n}$$
or, $E = \dfrac{0.059}{n}$
or, $n = \dfrac{0.059}{E}$ (at 25°C)

So measuring EMF of the cell we can measure the valency ($n$) of the metal at a definite temperature.
