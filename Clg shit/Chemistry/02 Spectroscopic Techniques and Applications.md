## Chapter at a Glance

### Introduction

With the spectacular advancement in instrumentation and automation of analytical operations that have taken place during the last four decades, the instrumental methods of analysis are now being extensively used for the qualitative analysis and identification of organic compounds. The term **spectroscopy** was originally applied to the branch of science that was based upon the resolution of visible radiation into its component wavelength. With the passage of time however, the meaning of the term has been broadened to encompass studies involving the entire electromagnetic radiation and even some techniques which do not at all involve electromagnetic radiation (e.g. mass and electron spectroscopy). All chemical species interact with electromagnetic radiations and in this process, there is a decrease in the intensity of the radiant beam. The measurement of the decrease in the intensity of radiation brought about by the species is the basis of absorption spectroscopy. The instrument used for spectroscopy measurement is called a "spectrophotometer".

### Visible and UV spectroscopy

When an electromagnetic radiation in the UV region (source is hydrogen discharge lamp) and visible region (source is tungsten filament lamp) is made to pass through a compound having multiple bonds in its structure, a part of the incident radiation is absorbed which involves the transitions of valency electrons. UV spectroscopy is useful for the determination of structure of several vitamins, dissociation constants of acids and bases, but it is not helpful in the detection of individual functional groups.

### Infrared (IR) spectroscopy

Here the source of electromagnetic radiation is an electrically heated rod of rare-earth oxides. The essential requirement for a substance to absorb in the infrared region is that vibrations in the molecule must give rise to an unsymmetrical charge distribution. Thus it is not necessary for the molecule to possess a permanent dipole moment. The study of IR spectra leads to a great deal of information — the presence of various functional groups, hydrogen bonding (intra and intermolecular), identification of cis-trans isomer, conformational orientation in aromatic compound etc.

### Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) Spectroscopy

Here the source is a powerful magnet of stable field strength. The NMR spectra are normally carried out on liquids or solutions. The best solvents are those which do not contain protons. Any nucleus consisting of either an odd number of protons or neutrons or both has the property of nuclear spin. A spinning proton generates a circulating electric current which in turn produces a magnetic field, and thus a spinning proton behaves like a magnet. Now, when a proton is placed in a magnetic field, it can align itself in one of two possible orientations with respect to the applied field: (i) low energy alignment (magnetic field of nucleus and applied magnetic field in the same direction) (ii) high energy orientation (two magnetic fields are opposite to each other). These are the basis of NMR spectroscopy. NMR can tell how many protons are present in a molecule and also their environment.

### Mass Spectroscopy

Here the molecules are bombarded with a beam of energetic electrons, and converted to highly energetic positively charged ions, which can break up into smaller ions. The different volatilized fragments are then separated by deflection in a variable magnetic field according to their $m/z$ ratio (mass/charge). They also generate a current at the collector in proportion to their relative abundance. A mass spectrum is a plot of relative abundance against the ratio $m/z$. The applications of mass spectrometry are to measure the molecular weight and molecular formula of a compound, to know the presence of recognizable groupings within the molecule and to identify the unknown compound by comparison of their mass spectra with that of known compounds.

## Short & Long Answer Type Questions

**2.1. a) What is Beer-Lambert law? Show that absorption is linearly proportional to concentration of the solution.** *[WBUT 2018(ODD)]*

**1ˢᵗ Part:**

**Lambert's law:** When a beam of monochromatic radiation passing through a homogeneous, transparent, absorbing medium, the rate of decrease of intensity of radiation with thickness of the medium is directly proportional to the intensity of the incident radiation.

Mathematically, this can be represented as
$$-\frac{dI}{dx} \propto I \qquad \ldots(1)$$
$$\text{or,}\quad -\frac{dI}{dx} = KI$$
where $I$ of radiation is the intensity after passing through a thickness $x$ of the medium and $dI$ is the infinitesimal small change in the intensity of radiation on passing through the infinitesimally small thickness $dx$ of the medium.

**Beer's law:** When a beam of monochromatic radiation is passed through a solution of an absorbing substance, the rate of decrease of intensity of radiation with thickness of the absorbing solution is directly proportional to the concentration of the solution.

Mathematically, this can be represented as
$$-\frac{dI}{dx} \propto c \qquad \ldots(2)$$

Now combining equation (1) and (2), we get
$$-\frac{dI}{dx} \propto I \cdot C$$
$$\text{or,}\quad -\frac{dI}{dx} = \alpha_{v}\cdot I \cdot C$$
$$\text{or,}\quad -\frac{dI}{I} = \alpha_{v}\cdot C\,dx$$
where $\alpha_{v}$ is the proportionality constant.

Now integrating Eqn. (3) with the boundary condition we get
$$\int_{I_{0}}^{I}\frac{dI}{I} = -\int_{0}^{x}\alpha_{v}\,c\,dx$$
$$\text{or,}\quad \ln\frac{I_{0}}{I} = \alpha_{v}\,c\,x$$
where $\alpha_{v}$ is the absorption coefficient and is a function of frequency or wavelength of incident radiation. In the logarithm scale
$$\log\frac{I_{0}}{I} = \frac{\alpha_{v}}{2.303}\,c\,x$$
$$\text{or,}\quad \log\frac{I_{0}}{I} = \varepsilon_{v}\,c\,x$$
where $\varepsilon_{v} = \dfrac{\alpha_{v}}{2.303}$ is called the molar extinction coefficient and is a function of frequency or wavelength.

**2ⁿᵈ Part:**

Absorbance, $A = \dfrac{I_{0}}{I}$

Transmittance, $T = \dfrac{I}{I_{0}}$

Non absorbing samples are transparent in nature. Thus, $I = I_{0}$, $T = 100\%$. Whereas, absorbing samples are opaque in nature. Thus, $I = 0$, $T = 0\%$.

$$\text{Absorbance, } A = \log\left(\frac{1}{T}\right) = \log\frac{I_{0}}{I}$$

Thus according to Lambert-Beer law, $A = \log\dfrac{I_{0}}{I} = \varepsilon_{v}\,c\,x$

**2.2. Write the short note on Fluorescence and its application.** *[WBUT 2018(ODD)]*

Fluorescence is the emission of light by a substance that has absorbed light or other electromagnetic radiation. Generally the emitted light has a longer wavelength and therefore lower energy than the absorbed radiation. The fluorescence occurs when the absorbed radiation is in the ultraviolet region of the spectrum, and thus invisible to the human eye, whereas the emitted light is in the visible region, which gives the fluorescent substance a distinct colour.

When the substance in the ground state absorbs light ($h\nu$), its one electron is promoted from the ground state molecular orbital to the first excited state molecular orbital or to higher excited states. When the electron returns from the excited state to the ground state, it emits radiation which is of lower energy or longer wave length than $h\nu$. It is also called fluorescent radiation ($h\nu_{f}$).

**Application**

(i) Fluorescent is used for diagnosis of different medical diseases such as premalignant and malignant cancer.
(ii) Fluorescent used in chemical sensor.
(iii) Fluorescent is used in mineralogy.
(iv) Green fluorescent protein (GFP): fluoresces in the green region of electromagnetic spectrum. The discovery of green fluorescent protein is very important for cell biology research.

**2.3. Write notes on UV – visible spectroscopy?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

When an electromagnetic radiation in UV region (wave length 100 – 400 nm) or visible region (wave length 400 – 750 nm) is made to pass through a compound having multiple bonds in its structure, it is observed that a part of the incident radiation is usually absorbed. The absorption of UV and visible light by organic compounds involves invariably transition of valency electrons. Since $\sigma$ electron is held tightly and good deal of energy is required to excite it; so it is outside the range of usual spectrophotometer. The modes of electronic energy changes which occur, when radiation in UV and visible region is absorbed by an organic compounds are: $\sigma \rightarrow \sigma^{*}$ or $\pi \rightarrow \pi^{*}$, $n \rightarrow \sigma^{*}$ or $n \rightarrow \pi^{*}$ (where $n$ = nonbonding or unshared pair of electron).

Structural units which absorb selective wavelengths and even small structural changes bring about perceptible changes in wavelength of radiation absorbed, are called **Chromophores**. Simple chromophores, $\text{C}=\text{C}$, $-\text{C}\equiv\text{C}-$, $-\text{C}=\text{N}$, $-\text{N}=\text{N}$, $\text{C}=\text{O}$, undergo $\pi - \pi^{*}$ transitions in the short wavelength regions of ultra violet radiations. Polar groups such as $-\text{OH}$, $-\text{OR}$, $-\text{NH}_{2}$, $-\text{SH}$ and $-\text{X}$ (halogen) having unshared pair of electrons may show absorption above 190 nm. Such groupings are called **auxochromes** when conjugated with Chromophore, since they modify the position of absorption band relative to the parent chromophore alone.

The greater the number of double bonds present in a given compound, the longer is the wavelength at which it absorbs UV radiation. Compounds containing carbon – oxygen double bond absorb light in the UV region. For example, acetone has a broad absorption peak at 280 nm, corresponding to excitation of an electron from a nonbonding to $\pi^{*}$ orbital of the C–O double bond. If the C–O double bond is conjugated with C–C double bond, then the absorption maxima correspond to $n \rightarrow \pi^{*}$ excitations and $\pi \rightarrow \pi^{*}$ excitation. The $n \rightarrow \pi^{*}$ excitation absorption maxima occurs at longer wavelength but is much weaker. For example, the compound $\text{CH}_{2}=\text{CH}-\text{CO}-\text{CH}_{3}$ gives $n \rightarrow \pi^{*}$ $\lambda_{\max}$ at 324 nm and $\pi \rightarrow \pi^{*}$ $\lambda_{\max}$ at 219 nm. The value of $\lambda_{\max}$ for compound in UV visible spectra also depends upon the solvent employed, but the variation is generally quite small.

In Visible–UV Spectrometer, a beam of light is split into two equal halves. One half of the beam (the sample beam) is directed through a transparent cell containing a solution of the compound being analyzed, and one half (the reference beam) is directed through an identical cell that contains only the solvent. $$\text{where, } A = \log\left(\frac{I_{0}}{I}\right).$$

**2.6. What information we are getting from Infra-red Spectroscopy and how?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

The study of IR Spectra leads to a great deal of information e.g. the presence of various functional group, hydrogen bonding (intermolecular and intramolecular), the identification of cis and trans isomers, conformational orientations, orientations in aromatic compounds etc.

The essential requirement for a substance to absorb in the infrared region is that vibrations in the molecule must give rise to an unsymmetrical charge distribution. Thus it is not necessary for the molecule to possess a permanent dipole moment.

Absorption in the near infra-red is due to changes in vibrational energy levels. A non linear molecule can undergo a number of vibrational motions, the two main types being stretching (vibration along the bonds) and deformation (bending-displacement, perpendicular to bonds). Different types of molecular vibrations for a non-linear molecule are as follows:

[diagram: Classification tree of "Molecular Vibration" branching into **Stretching** (Symmetric, Asymmetric) and **Bending**. Bending divides into **In plane** (Rocking, Scissoring) and **Out plane** (Wagging, Twisting), each illustrated for a bent XO₂-type group.]

The stretching regions have higher frequencies (shorter wavelengths) than the deformation regions, and the intensities of former are much greater than those of the latter. The nature of neighbouring atoms, masses of the bonded atoms, steric effects predominantly influence the frequency of the absorption. Thus, for a particular group there will not be a fixed maximum absorption wavelength, but will have region of absorption. The spectrum also depends on the physical state of the compound: gas, liquid (as a thin film), solid (as a thin film) or solution (preferably in dilute CCl₄, CHCl₃, CS₂). In IR, the identification of a compound is carried out by comparison with published spectra (or with the spectrum of an authentic specimen). The region 1400–650 cm⁻¹ is known as "finger – print region" – this is the region usually checked for identification, since it is the region associated with vibrational (and rotational) energy changes of the molecular skeleton and so is characteristic of the compound.

Ketoenol tautomerism can be investigated by using I.R spectroscopy. A typical example of 1,3 or $\beta$ diketone is acetyl acetone. For example in acetyl acetone the enol content of the compound is found to be very large and it gives a very characteristic peak for intra molecular H-bonded O–H in chelate form. This value is around 2800 cm⁻¹. Along with this the other characteristic peak that are present in enol form around 1680 cm⁻¹ (chelated $\text{C}=\text{O}$ stretching) and 1640 cm⁻¹ (for $\text{C}=\text{C}$ stretching in $\alpha,\beta$ unsaturated carbonyl compound). But this peaks are not observed in case of 1,2 and 1,4 diketone. Consequently 1,2($\alpha$), 1,3($\beta$) and 1,4($\gamma$) diketone can be distinguished by this method.

**2.21. What is the order of $\text{C}=\text{O}$ stretching frequency of the following compounds?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

The compounds (as labelled in the source):

| Label | Compound |
|---|---|
| (3) | $\text{H}_{2}\text{C}=\text{O}$ |
| (4) | $\text{(CH}_{3})(\text{H})\text{C}=\text{O}$ |
| (5) | $\text{(CH}_{3})_{2}\text{C}=\text{O}$ |
| (2) | $\text{(CF}_{3})_{2}\text{C}=\text{O}$ |
| (1) | $\text{F}_{2}\text{C}=\text{O}$ |

Since electronic factors like inductive effect and mesomeric effect can cause change in the bond strength of a particular bond in a molecule, this factors has predominant effect on the absorption position of bonds in different types of molecules.

The order of bond strength of the $\text{C}=\text{O}$ group is
$$(1) > (2) > (3) > (4) > (5)$$
So the order of $\text{C}=\text{O}$ stretching frequency will be same.

**2.22. Give the structure of molecular formula $\text{C}_{3}\text{H}_{8}\text{O}$ from the following data: $\nu_{\max} = 2950$ and $2820\ \text{cm}^{-1}$, $1110\ \text{cm}^{-1}$.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

By using above molecular formula we can write down three isomeric structure given below:
$$\text{CH}_{3}\text{CH}_{2}\text{CH}_{2}\text{OH}, \quad \text{CH}_{3}\text{CH(OH)CH}_{3}, \quad \text{CH}_{3}\text{OCH}_{2}\text{CH}_{3}$$

As there is no characteristic IR peak at 3300 cm⁻¹, so the first two structure is not the actual structure of the given molecular formula. The peak at 1110 cm⁻¹ is for ether linkage and at 2950 and 2820 cm⁻¹ is due to the C–H stretching. So the actual structure of the compound is $\text{CH}_{3}\text{OCH}_{2}\text{CH}_{3}$.
