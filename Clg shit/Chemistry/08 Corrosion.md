## Chapter at a Glance

### Types of corrosion

The process of corrosion of metals depends on the environment in which the metals are exposed. It may be divided into two types: (a) dry corrosion and (b) wet corrosion.

**Dry corrosion:** When a metal is exposed to a dry environment, the corrosion of metal occurs due to the direct attack of $\text{O}_2$ or corrosive gases such as $\text{Cl}_2$, $\text{SO}_2$, or chemical action on metals by chemical reactions.

The possible reasons for dry corrosion are:
- a) Oxidation corrosion
- b) Corrosion by gases
- c) Liquid metal corrosion

### Oxidation corrosion

In this type of corrosion, metals are attacked by dry oxygen under extreme conditions such as low or high temperature, leading to oxidation type of corrosion.

The alkali and alkaline earth metals are prone to oxidize at low temperature, but metals like Fe, Al etc. are oxidized at high temperature in presence of oxygen.

The following reaction takes place during oxidation of metals:

$$\text{Mg} \longrightarrow \text{Mg}^{2+} + 2e \quad \text{(oxidation)}$$
$$\tfrac{1}{2}\text{O}_2 + 2e \longrightarrow \text{O}^{2-} \quad \text{(reduction)}$$
$$\text{Mg} + \tfrac{1}{2}\text{O}_2 \longrightarrow \text{Mg}^{2+} + \text{O}^{2-} \longrightarrow \text{MgO}$$

### Corrosion by gases

Certain gases like $\text{SO}_2$, $\text{Cl}_2$, $\text{F}_2$ attack the metal in the absence of moisture, and the extent of corrosion depends on the formation of a thin film which may be protective or non-protective on the surface of the metal.

### Liquid metal corrosion

Liquid metal corrosion is caused due to chemical reaction of solid metal with a flowing liquid metal at high temperature. Such kind of corrosion occurs in nuclear power production.

### Mechanism of electrochemical corrosion

The absorption of oxygen type corrosion occurs during rusting of iron.

During rusting at the anodic area of the metal, iron dissolves as $\text{Fe}^{2+} + 2e$ (oxidation). The corrosion occurs in the anodic area.

The liberated electrons move from the anodic area to the cathodic area where reduction reaction occurs with the absorption of oxygen and forms $\text{OH}^-$ ions.

$$\tfrac{1}{2}\text{O}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} + 2e \longrightarrow 2\text{OH}^- \quad \text{(reduction)}$$

Thus $\text{Fe(OH)}_2$ is precipitated on the surface of the metal.

$$\text{Fe}^{2+} + 2\text{OH}^- \longrightarrow \text{Fe(OH)}_2 \downarrow$$

### Pilling–Bedworth rule

The protective or non-protective oxide layer formation in metals is governed by a rule known as the Pilling–Bedworth rule.

The rule states that "If the volume of the oxide is equal to or greater than the volume of the metal from which it is formed, the oxide layer is non-porous and therefore protective in nature (e.g., $\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3$), whereas if the volume of the oxide is less than the volume of the metal from which it is formed, the oxide layer is porous and therefore non-protective in nature (e.g., alkali or alkaline metal oxides)."

### Factors influencing the rate of corrosion

- **Nature of metals:** Metals with low reduction potential exhibit high reactivity, and hence are more prone to corrosion.
- **Electrode potential differences:** Potential difference between the anodic and cathode area is known as open circuit potential difference. Larger the open circuit potential difference, higher the rate of corrosion.
- **Surface state of the metal:** State of the surface of the metal affects the rate of corrosion. If part of the surface is covered by oil or dust particles, severe pitting corrosion occurs at the metal surface.
- **Hydrogen over voltages:** A metal with low hydrogen over voltage on its surface is more susceptible to corrosion. Low hydrogen over voltage results in faster cathodic reaction.
- **Formation of protective films by metals:** A few metals have the tendency to form passive but protective films on exposure to corrosive medium. The nature of such passive layer greatly decides the rate of corrosion.
- **pH:** In general, lower the pH of the corrosion medium, higher the corrosion rate. At pH > 10, corrosion of Fe ceases due to the formation of a protective coating of $\text{Fe(OH)}_2$. Al, Zn undergo fast corrosion in highly alkaline solution. At pH < 3, severe corrosion occurs even in the absence of air due to continuous evolution of $\text{H}_2$ gas at the cathodic area.
- **Temperature:** Increase in temperature increases the conductance of the medium, reduces passivity of the metal, and therefore increases the rate of corrosion.
- **Cathodic and anodic area:** The relative size of cathodic and anodic area greatly affects the rate of corrosion.

**2.2. Why do metals corrode?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

Most metals do not exist in nature as a solid metal piece of material. In their natural state, they exist in the form of oxides (or other salts such as carbonates, bi-carbonates, sulphides, chlorides or silicates etc.) which must be refined to create the pure metals or alloys, which become useful structural materials. Corrosion is a natural process and is a result of the natural tendency of metals to revert to their more stable compounds, usually oxides. In the refining process, energy is added to the ore to produce the metal. It is the same energy which provides the driving force causing the metal to revert to the more stable compound. Pure metals and alloys have a much higher energy state and there is a natural tendency to return to their lower energy state.

**2.5. Discuss the mechanism of electrochemical corrosion.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

According to the electrochemical theory of corrosion, when a metal (or dissimilar metals) is exposed to an acidic environment, the process of corrosion occurs in separate anodic and cathodic area of the metal surface.

A driving force is necessary for electrons to flow between the anode and the cathode. The driving force is the difference in potential between the anodic and cathodic areas. This difference occurs because each oxidation or reduction reaction has associated with it a potential determined by the tendency for the reaction to take place spontaneously.

Corrosion always occurs at the anodic area of the metal due to the oxidation process, and therefore electrons are liberated. As a result, the metal ions form either dissolved in the medium or form a thin film of oxide on the metal surface.

$$\text{M} \longrightarrow \text{M}^{n+} + ne \quad \text{(oxidation)}$$

On the other hand, the cathodic area of the metal is protected due to reduction process.

$$\text{M}^{n+} + ne \longrightarrow \text{M} \quad \text{(reduction)}$$

**2.6. Describe the mechanism of electrochemical corrosion by hydrogen evolution.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

The evolution of $\text{H}_2$ type corrosion occurs by the action of very dilute acid solution in iron.

The dissolution of iron as $\text{Fe}^{2+}$ ions takes place at the anodic area of the metal when exposed to an acid medium.

$$\text{Fe} \longrightarrow \text{Fe}^{2+} + 2e \quad \text{(oxidation)}$$

The liberated electrons flow from the anodic to the cathodic area of the same metal and give rise to the corrosion current. The electrons thus liberated reduce $\text{H}^+$ ions at the cathode and produce $\text{H}_2$ gas at the cathode.

$$2\text{H}^+ + 2e \longrightarrow \text{H}_2 \uparrow \quad \text{(reduction)}$$

**2.10. What do you mean by water line corrosion?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

Water line corrosion is a case of differential aeration corrosion. Ocean-going ships and water storage steel tanks undergo this type of corrosion.

In this type of corrosion, the metal portion just below the water line is more anodic than the portion above the water line. Therefore, the metal just below the water line undergoes corrosion.

$$\text{Fe}^{2+} + 2\text{OH}^- \longrightarrow \text{Fe(OH)}_2$$

**2.11. What do you mean by Crevice Corrosion? What are the mechanisms involved in Crevice Corrosion? How do you prevent Crevice Corrosion?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

**1st Part:**
**Crevice Corrosion:** Crevice corrosion is often associated with a small volume of stagnant solution or electrolyte trapped in crevices of joints, rivets, bolts, gaskets, surfaces, lap joints, nuts, washers or in surface deposits.

This corrosion occurs when oxygen cannot penetrate a crevice, leading to the setup of a differential aeration cell. Corrosion occurs rapidly in the area with less oxygen.

Crevice corrosion also takes place when the surface of a painted metal is scratched. The scratched portion would act as a small anode, while the rest of the part would act as cathode, resulting in the formation of a local differential aeration cell.

**2nd Part:**
Generally crevice corrosion occurs rapidly in the area with less oxygen. Increase in acidity and chloride content of crevice solution initiates passive film breakdown process, and thereby enhances the rate of corrosion reaction. Then, corrosion propagation continues, leading to further deterioration of the material.

The Fontana and Greens model describes the crevice corrosion mechanism, which consists of four stages:

**Stage 1:** Corrosion normally occurs both inside and outside the crevice with the following reactions:
$$\text{M} \longrightarrow \text{M}^{n+} + ne \quad \text{(anode reaction)}$$
$$\tfrac{1}{2}\text{O}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} + 2e \longrightarrow 2\text{OH}^- \quad \text{(Cathode reaction)}$$
The positively charged metallic ions are electrostatically counter-balanced by $\text{OH}^-$ ions.

**Stage 2:** The cathode reaction inside the crevice consumes most of the available oxygen.

**Stage 3:** The $\text{Cl}^-$ and $\text{OH}^-$ ions diffuse into the crevice to maintain minimum potential energy, to form metal chloride. Hydrolysis of metal chloride lowers the pH.
$$\text{MCl} + n\,\text{H}_2\text{O} \longrightarrow \text{M(OH)}_n + n\,\text{HCl}$$

**Stage 4:** Maximum number of $\text{H}^+$ ions attack maximum number of $\text{Cl}^-$, leading to lower pH inside crevice. This accelerates the dissolution of metal and more $\text{H}^+$ ions will be produced which further lower the pH.

**3rd Part:**
- (a) Increasing resistance by using alloys that contain a high percentage of chromium, molybdenum and/or nitrogen.
- (b) Design or fabricate to avoid crevices.
- (c) Frequently inspecting for inadvertent crevices and cover crevices by continuous welding, caulking or soldering.

**2.13. What is stress corrosion? Explain caustic embrittlement in boilers.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

**1st Part:**
Mechanical forces within the metal are responsible for stress corrosion. The internal stress of metal develops when the metal is subjected to mechanical operations like pressing, hammering, bending, welding or reverting. The metal may also experience external stress during service condition. The metal atom under stress is always at higher energy levels as compared to a stress-free atom.

The stressed part of the metal, therefore, becomes more reactive than the stress-free part. Therefore, a galvanic cell is formed with the stressed part acting as anode and stress-free part acting as cathode. Under specific corrosion environments, the stressed part undergoes corrosion, initiating the crack.

**2nd Part:**
**Caustic embrittlement of steel boiler:**
Steel boilers undergo corrosion at the stressed portion when the operating pressures are between 10 – 20 atm. Hair line cracks may develop at the stressed portion of the boiler. Boiler water containing alkaline impurities passes into these cracks through capillary action. This water evaporates and leaves behind caustic soda in the cracks, whose concentration thus increases.

Thus, a galvanic cell is formed between the iron under stress and the iron in the main body. It can be represented as follows:

| Fe | Conc NaOH | Dil NaOH | Fe |
|----|-----------|----------|----|
| (Under Stress) | (in the cracked) | (in boiling water) | (main body) |

Iron under stress acts as the anode and gets corroded, resulting in boiler failure. The boiler water contains $\text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3$, which is hydrolyzed at high temperature to give NaOH.

$$\text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \longrightarrow 2\,\text{NaOH} + \text{CO}_2$$

The NaOH thus formed flows into the fine cracks and crevices. There it reacts with iron and forms sodium ferroate ($\text{Na}_2\text{FeO}_2$) which decomposes to form $\text{Fe}_3\text{O}_4$ and NaOH.

$$2\,\text{NaOH} + \text{Fe} \longrightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{FeO}_2 + \text{H}_2$$
$$3\,\text{Na}_2\text{FeO}_2 + 4\,\text{H}_2\text{O} \longrightarrow \text{Fe}_3\text{O}_4 + 4\,\text{H}_2 + 6\,\text{NaOH}$$

NaOH so formed further reacts with iron to cause corrosion. It is called caustic embrittlement.

**2.15. What is microbiological corrosion? Explain microbiological corrosion with example.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

**1st Part:**
In microbiological corrosion, degradation of material occurs by bacteria, fungi or their by-product. Certain types of bacteria consume oxygen and cause differential aeration type of system, which is responsible for corrosion. The corrosion occurs at the regions of poor oxygen concentration.

**2nd Part:**
Sulphate reducing bacteria such as *Desulphovibrio*, which grows in oxygen-free condition, assist the corrosion of iron by consuming hydrogen produced in the cathodic area, thus increasing the rate of cathodic reaction.

$$\text{H}^+ + e \longrightarrow \text{H}$$

The bacteria reduce $\text{SO}_4^{2-}$ concentration by using $\text{H}^+$ ion. The overall reaction is as follows:

$$8\text{H}^+ + e \longrightarrow 8\text{H}$$ `[as printed]`
$$\text{SO}_4^{2-} + 8\text{H}^+ \longrightarrow \text{S}^{2-} + 4\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

The corrosion product FeS is formed.

**2.16. Which factors influence the rate of corrosion and how?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

Following factors influence the rate of corrosion:

- **a) Nature of metals:** The susceptibility of metal to undergo corrosion depends on its nature. Metals with low reduction potential exhibit high reactivity, and hence are more prone to corrosion.
- **b) Electrode potential differences:** Potential difference between the anodic and cathode area is known as open circuit potential difference. Larger the open circuit potential difference, higher the rate of corrosion.
- **c) Surface state of the metal:** State of the surface of the metal affects the rate of corrosion. If part of the surface is covered by oil or dust particles, severe pitting corrosion occurs at the metal surface.
- **d) Hydrogen over voltages:** Hydrogen over voltage is the measure of the tendency of the electrode to liberate hydrogen gas. A metal with low hydrogen over voltage on its surface is more susceptible to corrosion. Low hydrogen over voltage results in faster cathodic reaction.
- **e) Formation of protective films by metals:** A few metals have the tendency to form passive but protective films on exposure to corrosive medium. The nature of such passive layer greatly decides the rate of corrosion. The passive layer (metal oxide) formed on the surface of the metal is stable, highly insoluble and of low conductivity, and ultimately prevents further corrosion.
- **f) pH:** In general, lower the pH of the corrosion medium, higher the corrosion rate. At pH > 10, corrosion of Fe ceases due to the formation of a protective coating of $\text{Fe(OH)}_2$. Al, Zn undergo fast corrosion in highly alkaline solution. At pH < 3, severe corrosion occurs even in absence of air due to continuous evolution of $\text{H}_2$ gas at the cathodic area.
- **g) Temperature:** Increase in temperature increases the conductance of the medium, reduces passivity of the metal, and therefore increases the rate of corrosion.
- **h) Cathodic and anodic area:** The relative size of cathodic and anodic area greatly affects the rate of corrosion. When the ratio of anodic to cathodic region is small, that is, smaller anodic region and larger cathodic region, then faster and intensive anodic reaction occurs, thus making the overall corrosion effective. When the ratio of anodic to cathodic region is large, that is, larger anodic region and smaller cathodic region, then slower anodic reaction occurs; as a result, corrosion occurs very slowly.
