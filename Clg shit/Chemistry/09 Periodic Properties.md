## Chapter at a Glance

### Periodic properties
The properties which are repeated at regular intervals are called periodic properties. The main cause of periodicity in properties is the repetition of similar electronic configuration at regular intervals.

### Screening or shielding effect
The effect in which the electrons of inner orbits work as a curtain between the nucleus and the electrons of outermost orbit is called screening or shielding effect.

- The magnitude of screening effect depends upon the number of inner electrons i.e. higher the number of inner electrons, greater shall be the value of screening effect.
- Screening effect of all the inner electrons is not equal. It also depends upon the sub-shell in which electrons are present. For a given orbit, the screening effect of electron belonging to different sub-shells decreases in the order $s > p > d > f$.
- The screening effect of $d$ and $f$ orbitals is taken as almost constant.

### Atomic radius
It is defined as the distance from the centre of the nucleus to the outermost shell of electrons. The definition is imaginary because according to quantum mechanics there is no certainty about the exact position of an electron in an atom. Thus for practical purposes, atomic radius can be defined in three ways:

**Covalent radius**
It is defined as the half of the internuclear distance between two identical or almost identical atoms bonded by a single covalent bond.

- The above relation holds good only when the bond is purely covalent.

**van der Waals' radius**
It is defined as the half of the internuclear distance between the nuclei of two non-bonded adjacent atoms belonging to different molecules having identical atoms in solid state.

- The covalent radius is always smaller than van der Waals' radius for a given element.

**Crystal radius**
The atomic radius of metals and inert gases in solid state is called their crystal radius and is defined as half of the inter-nuclear distance between two non-bonded adjacent atoms in closed packed crystal lattice.

- A crystal radius is 10–15% greater than the covalent radius of a particular atom but is smaller than the van der Waals' radius.

### Trends in atomic radius
- Atomic radii of elements increases on moving down the group due to increase in the number of shells by a factor of 1 and increase in screening effect, which reduce the effective nuclear charge.
- Atomic radius of elements decreases while moving from left to right in a period. This is due to the fact that effective nuclear charge increases as number of inner electrons remain same throughout the period and the change occurs only in the number of valence electrons.
- However, in case of transition elements, atomic radii almost remains same because the electrons add to penultimate i.e. $(n-1)d$ subshell. Consequently, the increased nuclear charge is almost compensated by the screening effect.

### Ionic radius
Ionic radius is the distance between the centre of nucleus and its outermost shell or the distance between the nucleus and the point at which nucleus exerts its influence, in an ion. For determination of cationic and anionic radii, X-ray diffraction techniques are used.

### Trends in ionic radius
- The radius of cation is always smaller than its neutral atom, because of increase in the effective nuclear charge on electrons left and decrease in the number of orbits for most of the cases.
- If various cations have same number of electrons (isoelectronic), the cation having greater number of proton is smaller in size.

  $$\text{Na}^+ > \text{Mg}^{2+} > \text{Al}^{3+}$$

  | | $\text{Na}^+$ | $\text{Mg}^{2+}$ | $\text{Al}^{3+}$ |
  |---|---|---|---|
  | No. of electron: | 10 | 10 | 10 |
  | No. of proton: | 11 | 12 | 13 |

- Greater is the positive charge on the cation of a given atom, smaller is its ionic radius. e.g.,
  $$\text{Fe} > \text{Fe}^{2+} > \text{Fe}^{3+}$$
  Similarly, $\text{Sn} > \text{Sn}^{2+} > \text{Sn}^{4+}$
- Radius of anion is always greater than its neutral atom. It is due to decrease in effective nuclear charge.
- In case of isoelectronic anions, the anion having greater number of protons is smallest in size. e.g.,
  $$\text{N}^{3-} > \text{O}^{2-} > \text{F}^{-} \quad \text{[as printed]}$$

  | | | | |
  |---|---|---|---|
  | No. of electron: | 10 | 10 | 10 |
  | No. of proton: | 7 | 8 | 9 |

- Greater the negative charge on an atom, greater is its radius. e.g., out of $\text{O}^{2-}$, $\text{O}^{-}$ and $\text{O}$ the order of size is
  $$\text{O}^{2-} > \text{O}^{-} > \text{O}$$
- In a group, ionic radius increases on moving down.

### Ionization potential
The minimum energy required to remove an electron completely from gaseous atomic species is called the first ionization potential of the atom.

$$\text{Atom} + \text{Ionisation energy} \rightarrow \text{unipositive ion} + e^-$$

The following factors influence the magnitude of the ionization potential:

- **The distance of the electron from the nucleus** — the greater the distance of an electron from the positively charged nucleus of an element, the weaker will be the attraction and hence lower the ionization potential.
- **The charge on the nucleus** — the higher the charge on the nucleus the more difficult it is to remove an electron and hence the higher is the value of ionization energy.
- An increase in the overall charge on the ionizing species e.g. $\text{M}^+$, $\text{M}^{2+}$ etc. will enormously influence the ionization potential since electron withdrawal from a positively charged species is more difficult than from a neutral atom.

### Electronegativity
Pauling has defined electronegativity as the ability of an atom in a molecule to attract electron onto itself.

Let us consider a pair of atoms, A and B, which combine to form a compound AB with a single bond between them. The two electrons making the single bond can assume three different positions as

| I | II | III |
|---|---|---|
| $\text{A}^+ \;\; \text{:B}^-$ | $\text{A:B}$ | $\text{A}^{\delta-}\;\text{:}\;\text{B}^{\delta+}$ |

In the Ist case electronegativity of B is far greater than that of A and an ionic bond results. In the IInd case electronegativities of both A and B are equal and a non polar covalent bond is formed. But in IIIrd case the two bonding electrons are closer to A than B. In this case the electronegativity of A is slightly greater than that of B and a polar covalent bond results.

### Trends of electronegativity
- Electronegativity increases across the period and decreases down the group.
- **Diagonal relationship and electronegativity:** Moving one place to the right across period, the increased nuclear charge holds the electrons more tightly to the atom. Moving down one place in a group, the extra shell of electrons decreases the attraction of nucleus for the outer electrons. A diagonal move means that these effects tend to compensate for one another.
- The electronegativity increases as we go from Li to Be but it decreases as we move from Be to Mg. As we move diagonally, these two effects partly cancel each other and there is no marked change in electronegativity and thus, Li and Mg have close value of electronegativities. Because of this, Li and Mg show similar properties — a diagonal relationship. e.g., Li – Mg, Be – Al, and B – Si.

### Electron Affinity
The electron affinity of an atom is defined as the energy liberated when a gaseous atom captures an electron to produce an uninegative ion.

$$\text{atom} + e^- \rightarrow \text{uni-negative ion} + \text{electron affinity}$$

### Trends of electron affinity
- The electron affinity increases across the period and decreases down the group.
- Electron affinity of the elements of group 2 (IIA) and group 15 (VA) is negative. It is due to the presence of outer fully filled $s$-orbital in group 2 and half filled $p$-orbital in group 15 because of which these atoms do not have the tendency to accept electron.
- Noble gases also have a large negative electron affinity as energy required to add electron and break the stable noble gas configuration.
- In general, the electron affinity values of the elements of 3rd period are higher than the electron affinity values of the elements of 2nd period. It is due to small radius and high electron density in the outermost orbit of second period elements. The small size signifies limited capacity to release energy, while high electron density signifies greater repulsive forces which extra electron experiences while it is to be added e.g., the electron affinity of Cl is higher than that of F as Cl belongs to 3rd period while F belongs to 2nd period.
- It has been found that elements having higher values of electron affinity e.g., $\text{F}_2$, $\text{Cl}_2$, $\text{O}_2$, $\text{Br}_2$ etc. are good oxidants.

### Isomerism in transition metal compounds
Two or more compounds which have the same chemical formula but different structural arrangements or properties are called isomers. 'Isos' means 'the same' and 'meros' means 'the parts'. Isomers have the same number of same kind of atoms but these are bonded in different manner. The phenomenon that gives rise to isomers is called isomerism. Because of the complicated formulae of many co-ordination compounds, the variety of bond types and the number of shapes possible, different types of isomerism occur.

**i) Ionization Isomerism:** These are produced by an interchange of position of ligands inside the complex zone and anions outside the complex zone. $[\text{Co(NH}_3)_5\text{Br}]\text{SO}_4$ is red-violet and in solutions gives a precipitate with $\text{BaCl}_2$ confirming the presence of $\text{SO}_4^{2-}$. On the other hand $[\text{Co(NH}_3)_5\text{SO}_4]\text{Br}$ is red, and does not give a sulphate test in solution, but instead gives a precipitate of AgBr with $\text{AgNO}_3$. Other examples are
$$[\text{Co(NH}_3)_4\text{Cl}_2]\text{NO}_2 \quad \text{and} \quad [\text{Co(NH}_3)_4\text{ClNO}_2]\text{Cl}$$

**ii) Hydrate Isomerism:** This isomerism arises out of different disposition of aqua molecules inside and outside the first sphere of attraction. The examples are Chromium (III) chloride hydrates. The three hydrates are 1) violet $[\text{Cr(H}_2\text{O})_6]\text{Cl}_3$ which does not lose water over conc. $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ and the entire chloride is precipitated by silver ion, 2) green $[\text{Cr(H}_2\text{O})_5\text{Cl}]\text{Cl}_2\cdot\text{H}_2\text{O}$ loses one $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ over conc. $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ and two chloride ions are readily precipitated as AgCl, 3) $[\text{Cr(H}_2\text{O})_4\text{Cl}_2]\text{Cl}\cdot 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$, also green, loses two $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ over conc. $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ and only one chloride gets immediately precipitated by $\text{AgNO}_3$.

**iii) Coordination Isomerism:** When both positive and negative ions are complex, isomerism may be caused by the interchange of ligands between the two complex ions. For example:
$$[\text{Co(NH}_3)_6][\text{Cr(CN)}_6] \quad \text{and} \quad [\text{Cr(NH}_3)_6][\text{Co(CN)}_6]$$

**iv) Linkage Isomerism:** Certain ligands contain more than one atom which could donate an electron pair. In the case of $\text{NO}_2^-$ ion, either a nitrogen or an oxygen atom may act as donor, hence the possibility of isomerism. Two different complexes $[\text{Co(NH}_3)_5\text{NO}_2]\text{Cl}_2$ have been prepared. One is red (a) and is easily decomposed by acids to give nitrous acid, while the other is yellow (b) and is stable to acids. This behaviour is analogous to the behaviour of organic nitrates $\text{R–ONO}$ and nitrocompounds $\text{R–NO}_2$.

*[diagram: two octahedral cobalt-ammine cations — the red (a) nitrito/nitro linkage isomer and the yellow (b) linkage isomer of $[\text{Co(NH}_3)_5\text{NO}_2]^{2+}$.]*

**v) Coordination position Isomerism:** In polynuclear complexes an interchange of ligands between the different metal nuclei gives rise to positional isomerism.

*[diagram: a bridged binuclear cobalt complex of the form $[(\text{NH}_3)_3\text{Co}(\text{NH}_2)(\text{O}_2)\text{Co(NH}_3)_3\text{Cl}]\text{Cl}_2$ illustrating coordination position isomerism.]*

**vi) Geometric or Stereoisomerism:** In disubstituted complexes, the substituted groups may be adjacent or opposite to each other. This gives rise to geometric isomerism. Thus square planar complexes such as $[\text{Pt(NH}_3)_2\text{Cl}_2]$ can be prepared in two forms. If $\text{NH}_3$ is added to the $[\text{PtCl}_4]^{2-}$ ion, the complex formed has a finite dipole moment. However, the complex prepared by the action of HCl on $[\text{Pt(NH}_3)_4]^{2+}$ has no dipole.

*[diagram: cis and trans square-planar forms of $[\text{Pt(NH}_3)_2\text{Cl}_2]$.]*

Similarly disubstituted octahedral complexes such as $[\text{Co(NH}_3)_4\text{Cl}_2]$ also exists in cis and trans forms.

*[diagram: cis (violet) and trans (green) octahedral forms of $[\text{Co(NH}_3)_4\text{Cl}_2]$.]*

**vii) Optical Isomerism:** Coordinating group which occupy two positions in a coordination complex are known as chelate groups, e.g. ethylene diamine ('en' in short), oxalate ion etc. When two chelate groups are present in an octahedron, the groups may be arranged cis or trans to each other. The salts of the type $[\text{Cr(en)}_2\text{Cl}_2]$ exist in cis and trans forms. In the trans form the unidentate groups, namely the Chlorine atom, are in the trans position and the complex has a plane of symmetry and is optically inactive. The cis form can exist in two forms related as an object to its mirror image, i.e., the cis form gives rise to optical isomerism.

*[diagram: trans form and the two enantiomeric cis forms of $[\text{Cr(en)}_2\text{Cl}_2]$.]*

**b) Define Pauling's Scale of electronegativity.** *[WBUT 2018(ODD)]*

**Answer:**

Pauling first proposed the concept of electronegativity in 1932 to explain the fact that the covalent bond between two different atoms ($A - B$) is stronger than the average of the strengths of the bonds formed by like atoms such as $A - A$ and $B - B$ bonds.

The difference in electronegativity between A and B atoms is given by,

$$|\chi_A - \chi_B| = (ev)^{\frac{1}{2}}\sqrt{E_d(AB) - \frac{\left[E_d(AA) + E_d(BB)\right]}{2}}$$

where the dissociation energies ($E_d$) of the $A - B$, $A - A$ and $B - B$ bonds are expressed in ev. $\chi_A$ and $\chi_B$ are the electronegativity of the atom A and B respectively.

**2.2. Write short notes on the following:**
**a) Hard soft acids and bases** *[WBUT 2018(ODD)]*
**b) Fajan's rule** *[WBUT 2018(ODD)]*

**Answer:**

**a) Hard soft acids and bases:**

Soft bases are those in which the donor atoms are easily polarized and have low electronegativity.

Hard bases are those in which the donor atoms have low polarizability and high electronegativity.

In periodic table, softness of lewis bases increases with increasing in the size of the donor atoms. So for halide ions, $F^-$ is hardest base and $I^-$ is softest base.

Example:

| Hard base | Intermediate | Soft base |
|---|---|---|
| $\text{H}_2\text{O}$, $\text{OH}^-$, $F^-$, $\text{SO}_4^{2-}$, $\text{Cl}^-$, $\text{NH}_3$ etc. | $\text{N}_3^-$, $\text{SO}_3^{2-}$, $\text{NO}_2^-$ | $\text{RSH}$, $I^-$, $\text{SCN}^-$, $\text{CN}^-$ |

Soft acid consists of large size and low charge cation whereas, hard acid consists of small size and high charge cation.

Example:

| Hard acid | Intermediate | Soft acid |
|---|---|---|
| $H^+$, $\text{Li}^+$, $\text{Na}^+$, $K^+$, $\text{Al}^{3+}$, $\text{Cr}^{3+}$ etc. | $\text{Fe}^{2+}$, $\text{Co}^{2+}$, $\text{Ni}^{2+}$, $\text{Cu}^{2+}$, $\text{Zn}^{2+}$ | $\text{Cu}^+$, $\text{Ag}^+$, $\text{Hg}^+$, $\text{Pd}^{2+}$, $\text{Hg}^{2+}$, $I_2$, $\text{Br}_2$ |

**b) Fajan's rule:**

The power of an ion to distort the other ion is known as its polarizing power and the tendency of an ion to get polarized or distorted is called polarizability. Fajans proposed a number of empirical rules to explain the polarization of ions. According to Fajans rule, the increased polarization in ionic compounds is depend on

i) Small positive ion, ii) Large negative ion, iii) High charge of the ions.

**Small positive ion:** Small positive ions have higher polarizing power because positive charge is concentrated over a small surface area and therefore it can distort the electron charge cloud of the anion effectively.

**Large negative ion:** Large negative ions have excess electrons, large radii, and diffuse electron clouds which can be polarized easily.

**High charge of the ions:** High charge on the cation has a greater tendency to polarize the anion and high charge on the anion has a greater tendency to get polarized.
