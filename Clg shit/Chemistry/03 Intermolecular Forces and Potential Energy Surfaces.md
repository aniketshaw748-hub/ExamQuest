## Chapter at a Glance

### van der Waals' forces

Besides the primary forces responsible for the formation of molecules there are secondary forces that operate between molecules. Such intermolecular forces are popularly called van der Waals forces. Unlike covalent bonding which is associated with orbital overlap at short intermolecular distances, van der Waals forces may operate at longer distances, where no orbital overlap can occur. Hence such forces are weak.

### Types of van der Waals' attraction

There are several types of forces that contribute to van der Waals attraction.

- **Dipole-dipole interaction:** When the interacting atoms differ significantly in their electronegativity, the resulting molecule is often a polar covalent compound. Thus the compound will have a partial $(\delta^+)$ character on one atom and partial $(\delta^-)$ on the other atom. In such molecules the dipoles are so arranged themselves that a $\delta^+$ end faced a $\delta^-$ end and so on. This weak attractive force holds the molecules together. A very special type of dipole-dipole interaction exists in some associated hydrides like NH₃, H₂O and HF which is called hydrogen bonding.
- **Ion-dipole interaction:** Negative ends of dipolar molecules like H₂O, NH₃ may be attracted towards metal ions to form metal hydrates or metal ammoniates. The weak interaction occurs due to the lone pair electron density of H₂O or NH₃ facing the cations. This type of interaction is responsible for the dissolution of ionic compounds in polar solvents.
- **Dipole-induced dipole interaction:** This type of weak interaction exists between a molecule with a permanent dipole and a molecule (or atom) without one. The polar molecule may distort the electron density of the second molecule and a dipole is induced. An attraction can now result between the permanent dipole and the induced dipole.

### Hydrogen bonding

Hydrogen atom having a single electron in the $1s$ orbital, is capable of forming a single covalent bond. However, when it is covalently bonded to a strongly electronegative element the bond becomes strongly dipolar. The positive hydrogen end of the dipole can weakly link some other negative dipolar end present in the same molecule or in another molecule, thus effectively forming a weak bond to a second electronegative atom. This weak, secondary bond between two electronegative elements through the agency of partially positive hydrogen is known as hydrogen bond (H–F⋯⋯H–F⋯⋯H–F).

### Special properties due to H-bonding

- **Abnormal boiling point of binary hydrides:** Generally, the melting points and boiling points of binary covalent hydrides increase with atomic number in a group, is violated by NH₃ in group V, water in group VI, hydrogen fluoride in Group VII. The abnormal behavior is due to association caused by H-bonding in liquid phase.
- **Abnormal heat of vaporization of binary hydrides:** The heat of vaporization of H₂O at its boiling point is 9.72 KCal/mole, which is almost double that of H₂S. This happens due to H-bonding in H₂O.
- **Dimerisation of acetic acid:** Structure determination of acetic acid reveals a dimer through intermolecular hydrogen bonding.

  *[diagram: two acetic acid (H₃C–COOH) molecules drawn as a dimer, each C=O⋯H–O hydrogen bond linking the two units head-to-tail.]*
- **Modification of acid character:** O-Hydroxybenzaldehyde and O-nitrophenol have their hydrogen bonding restricted within the molecules (intramolecular hydrogen bonding). Such H-bonding may lead to a weakening of the acid character of the compound.

  *[diagram: benzene ring with an ortho –CHO and –OH forming an intramolecular C=O⋯H–O hydrogen bond (salicylaldehyde); and o-nitrophenol with an intramolecular –NO₂⋯H–O hydrogen bond.]*
- **Abnormal solubility:** Organic compounds with strongly electronegative oxygen and nitrogen being present in functional groups are more soluble in CHCl₃, CHCl₂F or CH₂Cl₂ than in CCl₄.

### Real gases

The gas which obeys gas laws is called an ideal gas, while others are real gases. Real gases obey the gas laws under moderate conditions of temperature and pressure. At very low temperature and high pressure, the real gases show considerable deviation from the ideal gas behavior.

### Cause of deviation from ideal behavior

The ideal gas laws were derived from following two assumptions of kinetic theory of gases which did not hold good in all conditions:

- The intermolecular forces of attraction between gaseous molecules are negligible.
- The volume occupied by the gas molecules is negligible in comparison to the total volume of the gas.

### Compressibility factor: Explanation of deviation from ideal behavior

The deviation of gases from ideal behavior can be understood better by evaluating the effect of pressure and temperature on compressibility factor $(Z)$ i.e. $Z = \dfrac{pV}{RT}$, for one mole of gas, when

- $Z = 1$, the gas is ideal at all temperatures and pressure.
- $Z > 1$, the gas is less compressible than expected from ideal behavior and shows **positive deviation**, usually at high pressure, i.e. $pV > RT$.
- $Z < 1$, the gas is more compressible than expected from ideal behavior and shows **negative deviation**, usually at high pressure, i.e. $pV < RT$.

For H₂ and He the value of $Z$ is always greater than 1 while the value first decreases and then increases for other gases.

### Boyle's temperature

The temperature at which $\dfrac{pV}{RT}$ is unity for a gas up to appreciable range of pressure is called Boyle's temperature $(T_b)$.

$$\text{Boyle Temperature } (T_b) = \frac{a}{bR} - \frac{1}{2}T_i$$

where $a$ and $b$ are van der Waals' constant and $T_i$ is called inversion temperature.

### van der Waals' equation

To rectify the errors caused by ignoring the intermolecular forces of attraction and the volume occupied by molecules, Johannes van der Waals proposed the following equation, which is valid for real gases upto a large range of temperature and pressure:

$$\left(p + \frac{n^2 a}{V^2}\right)(V - nb) = nRT$$

where $a$ and $b$ are van der Waals' constants and are called correction terms for pressure and volume.

- Constant '$a$' is indirect measure of magnitude of attractive forces between the molecules. Greater the value of '$a$' more easily the gas can be liquefied. Unit of '$a$' is $\text{l}^2\,\text{atm}\,\text{mol}^{-2}$. `[as printed]`
- The constant '$b$' is introduced to correct that portion of gas, which is not compressible. It is also called **excluded volume** or **co-volume**. If closest distance of approach of two molecules be $2r$, ($r$ is the radius of one molecule), the excluded volume for two molecules is

  $$\frac{4}{3}\pi(2r)^3 = 8\left(\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3\right) = 8 \times \text{volume of one molecule}$$

  Hence, for one molecule the excluded volume $= 4 \times \left(\dfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3\right)$.

  For $N$ molecules,

  $$b = N \times \text{total volume of one molecule, or } b = 4N\left(\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3\right)$$

  '$b$' is measured in $\text{cm}^3/\text{mol}$.

- van der Waals' constant '$a$' and '$b$' are related as

  $$T_B = \frac{a}{bR}, \quad \text{where } T_B = \text{Boyle's temperature.}$$

### Significance of van der Waals' constants

Since an easily liquefiable gas has greater intermolecular attraction hence, the value of $a$ is a measure of the intermolecular attraction. In other words, we can say that the value of $a$ indicates the strength of van der Waals' forces. Thus for high value of '$a$', the ease with which a gas can be liquefied will be high. The easily liquefiable gases (SO₂, NH₃, H₂S, CO₂ etc.) have high value of '$a$' than the gases like H₂, N₂, O₂ etc.

The constant value of '$b$' for any gas indicates that the gas molecules are incompressible.

### van der Waals' equation at different conditions

**(a) When pressure is low**

At low pressure, volume $V$ is very large and hence the correction term $b$ (a constant of small value) can be neglected in comparison to very large value of $V$. Hence, it can be neglected from van der Waals' equation for 1 mole of gas, ie,

$$\left(p + \frac{a}{V^2}\right)(V - b) = RT$$

$$\text{or, } \left(p + \frac{a}{V^2}\right)V = RT$$

$$\text{or, } pV + \frac{a}{V} = RT$$

$$\text{or, } pV = RT - \frac{a}{V}$$

$$\text{or, } \frac{pV}{RT} = 1 - \frac{a}{RTV}$$

$$\text{or, } Z = 1 - \frac{a}{RTV} = \frac{pV}{RT}$$

where $Z$ is compressibility factor.

- **At extremely low pressure:** At extremely low pressure, $V$ is very large therefore the value of $\dfrac{a}{V}$ is very small and hence can be neglected. Hence, $pV = RT$.

**(b) When pressure is high**

At high pressure volume $V$ is quite small and hence the term $b$ cannot be neglected in comparison to $V$. Again, as the term $\dfrac{a}{V^2}$ is quite large but it is so small in comparison to high pressure $p$ that it can be neglected. Thus, the van der Waals' equation may be written as:

$$p(V - b) = RT$$

$$\text{or, } pV = RT + pb$$

$$\text{or, } \frac{pV}{RT} = 1 + \frac{pb}{RT}$$

$$\text{or, } Z = 1 + \frac{pb}{RT}$$

**(c) When temperature is high**

At any given pressure, if the temperature is extremely high, $V$ is very large and hence the terms $\dfrac{a}{V^2}$ and $b$ can be neglected. Thus, the van der Waals' equation reduces to $pV = RT$.

This explains why the real gases behave like ideal gas at high temperature.

**(d) When temperature is low**

At low temperature both $p$ and $V$ are small, hence both pressure and volume corrections are appreciable, so that the deviation are more pronounced.

### Merits of van der Waals' equation

- This equation explains behavior of real gases upto moderately high pressure.
- This equation represents the trend of isotherms representing the variation of $pV$ with $p$ for various gases.
- With the help of this equation, values of Boyle's temperature, critical constants and inversion temperatures may be calculated in terms of $a$ and $b$.
- This equation is also helpful in obtaining a reduced equation of state which being a general equation of state has the advantage that a single curve can be obtained for all gases when the equation is generally represented by plotting the variables.

### Limitations of van der Waals' equation

- Although van der Waals' equation is much more accurate than the ideal gas equation, appreciable deviation at too low temperatures and pressures have been observed.
- The values of van der Waals' constants $a$ and $b$ do not remain constant over the entire range of temperature and pressure.

Hence, van der Waals' equation is valid only over specific range of temperature and pressure.

### Critical state

A state for every substance at which the vapour and liquid states are indistinguishable is known as critical state.

**Critical temperature:** There is a temperature below which the gas can be liquefied but above it the gas defies liquefaction. This temperature is called critical temperature $(T_c)$.

It is given by, $T_c = \dfrac{8a}{27Rb}$

**Critical pressure:** The critical pressure $(p_c)$ is the minimum pressure required to liquefy the gas at its critical temperature. It is given by, $T_c = \dfrac{a}{27b^2}$. `[as printed: the source labels this expression "$T_c$", though it is the critical pressure $p_c$]`

**Critical volume:** The critical volume $(V_c)$ is the volume occupied by a mole of the gas at the critical temperature and critical pressure. It is given by, $V_c = 3b$.

Critical compressibility factor $(Z_c)$ is given by $Z_c = \dfrac{p_c V_c}{RT_c} = \dfrac{3}{8} = 0.375$.

All the gases behaves as van der Waals gas if its critical compressibility factor $(Z_c)$ is equal to 0.375.

## Short & Long Answer Type Questions

**2.1. What is ideal gas equation? Explain the equation of state for an ideal gas.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

The equation which gives the simultaneous effect of pressure and temperature on the volume of a gas, known as ideal gas equation or equation of state for an ideal gas.

The ideal gas equation can be derived by combining Boyle's Law, Charle's Law and Avogadro's Law as follows:

According to Boyle's Law,
$$V \propto \frac{1}{P} \quad [T = \text{Constant}] \qquad \ldots(\text{I})$$
According to Charle's Law,
$$V \propto T \quad [P = \text{constant}] \qquad \ldots(\text{II})$$
According to Avogadro's Law,
$$V \propto n \quad [\text{at constant } T \text{ and } P] \qquad \ldots(\text{III})$$
$n =$ number of moles of the gas.

Now, combining (I), (II) and (III), we get
$$V \propto \frac{1}{P} \times T \times n \qquad R = \text{molar gas constant}$$
$$V = R \times \frac{T \cdot n}{P}$$
$$\boxed{PV = nRT}$$
when $n = 1$ mol, then $\boxed{PV = RT}$

A gas that obeys ideal gas equation called an **ideal gas**.

The ideal gas equation is called equation of state because it explains the state of the gas completely when all the variables have been specified.

**2.2. What is the value of molar gas constant (R)?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

**(I)** At N.T.P. conditions, for 1 mol of the gas:
$P = 1\,\text{atmosphere}$, $n = 1\,\text{mol}$, $V = 22.4\,\text{litres}$, $T = 273\,\text{K}$
$$R = \frac{PV}{nT} = \frac{1 \times 22.4}{1 \times 273} \text{ atmosphere litre mol}^{-1}\,\text{Kelvin}^{-1}$$
$$= 0.0821 \text{ lit atmosphere/mol.K}$$

**(II)** In the C.G.S. unit, for 1 mol of the gas at N.T.P.:
$P = 76\,\text{cm} = 76 \times 13.6 \times 981 \text{ dyne/cm}^2$
$V = 22{,}400\,\text{ml}$, $T = 273\,\text{K}$, $n = 1\,\text{mol}$
$$R = \frac{76 \times 22{,}400}{1 \times 273} = \frac{76 \times 13.6 \times 981 \times 22400}{1 \times 273} \text{ erg/degree mol}$$
$$= 8.314 \times 10^7 \text{ erg/degree mol}$$

**(III)** In the S.I. unit:
$1\,\text{Joule} = 10^7\,\text{ergs}$
$$R = \frac{8.314 \times 10^7}{10^7} \text{ Joules/degree mol}$$
$$= 8.314 \text{ J/K mol}$$

**(IV)** In terms of calories:
$1\,\text{calorie} = 4.184\,\text{Joules}$
$$R = \frac{8.314}{4.184} \text{ calorie/degree mol}$$
$$= 1.987 \text{ calorie/K mol}$$

**2.9. Starting from van der Waal's equation explain the behavior of real gases under different conditions of temperature and pressure.** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

van der Waal's equation $\left(P + \dfrac{n^2 a}{V^2}\right)(V - nb) = nRT$ for $n$ mol gas.

For 1 mol gas $n = 1$. The equation is $\left(P + \dfrac{a}{V^2}\right)(V - b) = RT$
$T =$ temperature, $a, b =$ constant, $P =$ pressure, $V =$ volume, $R =$ molar gas constant.

**(1) At very low pressure:** When pressure is low, volume $(V)$ is large. So the term $\dfrac{a}{V^2}$ is very small and can be neglected. Similarly the correction term '$b$' can also be neglected. So, van der Waal's equation reduced to $\boxed{PV = RT}$ at low pressure.

Therefore, at low pressure the real gases behave as an ideal gas.

**(2) At moderate pressure:** At moderate pressure, $V$ decreases, so $\dfrac{a}{V^2}$ term increases. But $V$ is still large compared to constant '$b$' and '$b$' can be neglected.

Now,
$$\left(P + \frac{a}{V^2}\right)V = RT$$
$$\therefore PV + \frac{a}{V} = RT$$
$$\therefore PV = RT - \frac{a}{V}$$
Both side divided by $RT$,
$$\therefore \frac{PV}{RT} = 1 - \frac{a}{VRT}$$
$$\therefore \boxed{Z = 1 - \frac{a}{VRT}} \qquad Z = \text{Compressibility factor} = \frac{PV}{RT}$$
Now, compressibility factor is always less than 1. When pressure is increased at constant temperature $V$ decreases and the term $\dfrac{a}{RTV}$ increases.

**(3) At high pressure:** At high pressure, $V$ is so small that $\dfrac{a}{V^2}$ term can be neglected. But the term '$b$' cannot be neglected.
$$\therefore P(V - b) = RT$$
$$\therefore PV = RT + Pb$$
Both side divided by $RT$,
$$\therefore \frac{PV}{RT} = 1 + \frac{Pb}{RT}$$
$$\therefore \boxed{Z = 1 + \frac{Pb}{RT}}$$
Now, the value $Z$ is always greater than 1. As pressure increases at constant temperature, the term $\dfrac{Pb}{RT}$ increases.

**(4) At high temperature:** At high temperature, volume is very large at a given pressure. So $\dfrac{a}{V^2}$ and $b$ can be neglected. The equation becomes $\boxed{PV = RT}$.

Now, at high temperature, real gas behaves like an ideal gas.

**2.11. Why do real gases deviate from ideal behavior?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

The real gases obey ideal gas equation under low pressure or high temperature. But at high pressure or low temperature, real gases show some deviations from ideal nature.

At high pressure or low temperature, the following two assumptions of kinetic theory of gases are faulty.

(a) The volume occupied by the gas molecules is negligible as compared to the total volume of the gas.

(b) The force of attraction or repulsion between the gas molecules is negligible.

These two assumptions are applicable under low pressure or higher temperature, because distance between the molecules is large. But at high pressure or lower temperature, molecules are very close to each other. Under these conditions:

(a) The force of attraction or repulsion between the molecules may not be negligible.

(b) The volume occupied by the gas may be so small but the volume occupied by the molecules may not be negligible.

**Critical temperature:** The temperature above which gas cannot be liquefied using only high pressure, called critical temperature. It is denoted by $T_c$.
$$T_c = \frac{8a}{27Rb}$$

**Critical Volume:** The volume occupied by one mole of the gas at the critical temperature and critical pressure, called critical volume. It is denoted by $V_c$.
$$V_c = 3b$$

**Critical pressure:** The pressure required to liquefy the gas at the critical temperature, called critical pressure. It is denoted by $P_c$.
$$P_c = \frac{a}{27b^2}$$

**2.13. How can you calculate critical constant $P_c$, $V_c$ and $T_c$?** *[MODEL QUESTION]*

The mathematical definition of the critical point is.
$$\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial V}\right)_T = 0 \qquad \ldots(\text{I})$$
$$\left(\frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial V^2}\right)_T = 0 \qquad \ldots(\text{II})$$

van der Waal's equation is
$$\left(P + \frac{an^2}{V^2}\right)(V - nb) = nRT \qquad \ldots(\text{III})$$
$P =$ pressure, $V =$ volume, $R =$ molar gas constant, $T =$ absolute temperature, $a, b =$ constant.

From equation (III)
$$P = \frac{nRT}{V - nb} - \frac{an^2}{V^2} \qquad \ldots(\text{IV})$$

Now, we can take the derivatives in equation (I) and (II) and set them (independently) equal to zero.
$$\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial V}\right)_T = -\frac{nRT}{(V - nb)^2} + 2a\frac{n^2}{V^3} = 0 \qquad \ldots(\text{V})$$
$$\left(\frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial V^2}\right)_T = \frac{2nRT}{(V - nb)^3} - 6a\frac{n^2}{V^4} = 0 \qquad \ldots(\text{VI})$$
$$-\frac{nRT}{(V - nb)^2} + 2a\frac{n^2}{V^3} = 0 \qquad \ldots(\text{VII})$$
$$\frac{2nRT}{(V - nb)^3} - 6a\frac{n^2}{V^4} = 0 \qquad \ldots(\text{VIII})$$

There are several ways to solve simultaneous equations. One way is to multiply equation (VII) by $\dfrac{2}{V - nb}$ to get
$$-\frac{2nRT}{(V - nb)^3} + 4a\frac{n^2}{V^3(V - nb)} = 0 \qquad \ldots(\text{IX})$$
Now add equation (VIII) and (IX)
$$-\frac{6an^2}{V^4} + 4a\frac{n^2}{V^3(V - nb)} = 0 \qquad \ldots(\text{X})$$
Divide equation (X) by $2n^2 a$ and multiply it by $V^3$ (and bring the $-Ve$ term to the other side of the equal sign) to get,
$$\frac{2}{V - nb} = \frac{3}{V} \qquad \ldots(\text{XI})$$
$$\therefore 2V = 3V - 3nb$$
$$\therefore \boxed{V = 3nb}$$
$$\therefore \boxed{V = V_c = 3nb} \qquad \ldots(\text{XII})$$

To find the critical temperature substitute the critical volume from equation (XII) into one of the derivatives (which equals zero) say equation (VII)
$$\frac{-nRT}{(3nb - nb)^2} + 2a\frac{n^2}{(3nb)^3} = 0 \qquad \ldots(\text{XIII})$$
$$\frac{nRT}{(3nb - nb)^2} = 2a\frac{n^2}{(3nb)^3}$$
$$\therefore \frac{nRT}{(2nb)^2} = 2a\frac{n^2}{27n^3 b^3}$$
$$\therefore \frac{nRT}{4n^2 b^2} = 2a\frac{n^2}{27n^3 b^3}$$
$$\therefore \frac{nRT}{4} = \frac{2an^2}{27nb}$$
$$\therefore \frac{RT}{4} = \frac{2a}{27b}$$
$$\therefore T = \frac{8a}{27Rb}$$
$$\therefore \boxed{T = T_c = \frac{8a}{27Rb}} \qquad \ldots(\text{XIV})$$

The critical pressure is obtained by substituting $V_c$ and $T_c$ into van der Waal's equation (IV)
$$P_c = \frac{nRT_c}{V_c - nb} - \frac{n^2 a}{V_c^2}$$
$$\therefore P_c = \frac{nR\dfrac{8a}{27Rb}}{3nb - nb} - \frac{an^2}{(3nb)^2}$$
$$\therefore P_c = \frac{nR \times 8a}{27Rb \times 2nb} - \frac{an^2}{9n^2 b^2}$$
$$\therefore P_c = \frac{4a}{27b^2} - \frac{a}{9b^2}$$
$$\therefore P_c = \frac{4a - 3a}{27b^2}$$
$$\therefore \boxed{P_c = \frac{a}{27b^2}} \qquad \ldots(\text{XV})$$
