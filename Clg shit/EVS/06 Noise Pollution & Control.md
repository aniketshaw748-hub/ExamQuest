## Chapter at a Glance

- **Noise** is unwanted sound without agreeable musical quality. Therefore, when the effects of sound are undesirable, then it may be termed as "noise".
- Noise generation is associated with most of our daily activities. A healthy human ear responds to a very wide range of SPL from — the threshold of hearing at zero dB, uncomfortable at 100–120 dB and painful at 130–140 dB.
- **Noise pollution** is excessive, displeasing human, animal or machine-created environmental noise that disrupts the activity or balance of human or animal life.
- The common scientific acoustic unit is **Decibel (dB)**. It is not an absolute physical unit like volt, meter etc., but it is a ratio expressed as a logarithmic scale relative to a reference sound pressure level or intensity level.
- Noise can be broadly classified under three categories:
  1. Transport noise
  2. Occupational noise
  3. Neighbourhood noise.
- **Controlling Noise Pollution:** We can reduce noise pollution by turning off appliances when not in use, use of earplugs, lowering the volume, planting more trees, regular maintenance of vehicles and machines etc.

**1. Define noise and noise pollution. How much is a sound of 100 dB (decibels) louder than a sound of 90 dB?** *[WBUT 2005, 2012(O), 2015(E)]*

Noise is unwanted sound without agreeable musical quality. Therefore, when the effects of sound are undesirable, then it may be termed as "noise".

Noise pollution is excessive, displeasing human, animal or machine-created environmental noise that disrupts the activity or balance of human or animal life.

The common scientific acoustic unit is Decibel (dB). It is not an absolute physical unit like volt, meter etc., but it is a ratio expressed as a logarithmic scale relative to a reference sound pressure level or intensity level.

$$\text{dB} = 10\log_{10}\frac{\text{measured intensity}}{\text{reference intensity}}$$

$$\text{dB} = 10\log_{10}\frac{I}{I_0}$$

The community (ambient) noise levels are measured in the A-weighted SPL, abbreviated dB(A). This scale resembles the audible response of the human ear. Sounds of frequencies from 800 to 3000 Hz are covered by the A-weighted scale. These meters record the dB A scale, which is commonly used for measurement of general noise levels.

*(For the "100 dB vs 90 dB" computation, see Short Answer Question 4 below; the result is that 100 dB sound is louder than 90 dB sound by 10 times.)*

**2. What is $L_{epn}$? Explain different types of noise.** *[WBUT 2015(O), 2017(E)]*

**1st Part:**
This is recommended for aircraft by the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) as the standard for use in noise evaluation (Aircraft Noise).

The noise of aircraft is described in terms of **Perceived Noise Levels ($L_{pN}$)**, a scale of noisiness, expressed in pNdB. There is no simple relationship between the dB(A) value and pNdB value for all noises. However, a useful statement is that the pNdB value for a noise is approximately 13 units greater than the dB(A) value for the noise.

A further refinement resulting from the study of aircraft noise is the effective noise level ($L_{epn}$), a scale of noisiness of a time-varying event, expressed in EPNdB. It is used to describe the noise of a single aircraft activity. In order to describe the noise exposure associated with an airport, the EPNdB values are supplemented with such information as the number of flights of each aircraft type, the flight paths that the aircraft use and the time of day at which the operations occur. The resulting picture is often presented in such terms as **Noise Exposure Forecast (NEF)** contours, which are intended to represent the long-term average noise exposure in communities around airports.

**4. How much is a sound of 100 dB louder than a sound of 90 dB?** *[WBUT 2016(O)]*

We know that, sound level $L\ (\text{dB}) = 10\log\dfrac{I}{I_0}$, where $I$ = measured intensity, $I_0$ = reference intensity.

In the first case, i.e. when sound level is 100 dB then,
$$L_1 = 10\log\frac{I_1}{I_0} \;\Rightarrow\; \log\frac{I_1}{I_0} = \frac{L_1}{10} \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{I_1}{I_0} = 10^{L_1/10}$$
$$\therefore\ I_1 = I_0 \times 10^{L_1/10} \qquad \ldots(1)$$

In the second case, i.e. when sound level is 90 dB then,
$$L_2 = 10\log\frac{I_2}{I_0} \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{I_2}{I_0} = 10^{L_2/10}$$
$$\therefore\ I_2 = I_0 \times 10^{L_2/10} \qquad \ldots(2)$$

Now, with $L_1 = 100$ dB and $L_2 = 90$ dB,
$$\frac{I_1}{I_2} = \frac{I_0 \times 10^{L_1/10}}{I_0 \times 10^{L_2/10}} = \frac{1\times10^{100/10}}{1\times10^{90/10}} = \frac{10^{10}}{10^{9}} = 10 \text{ times}.$$

So, 100 dB sound is louder than 90 dB sound by 10 times.

**5. How much is a sound of 100 dB louder than a sound of 80 dB? (reference intensity $= 1\times10^{-12}\ \text{W/m}^2$).** *[WBUT 2017(E)]*

We know that, sound level $L\ (\text{dB}) = 10\log\dfrac{I}{I_0}$, where $I$ = measured intensity, $I_0$ = reference intensity.

In the first case (100 dB):
$$I_1 = I_0 \times 10^{L_1/10} \qquad \ldots(1)$$

In the second case (80 dB):
$$I_2 = I_0 \times 10^{L_2/10} \qquad \ldots(2)$$

Now, with $L_1 = 100$ dB and $L_2 = 80$ dB,
$$\frac{I_1}{I_2} = \frac{I_0 \times 10^{100/10}}{I_0 \times 10^{80/10}} = \frac{1\times10^{10}}{1\times10^{8}} = 10^{10}/10^{8} = 100 \text{ times}.$$

So, 100 dB sound is louder than 80 dB sound by 100 times.

**6. What is noise dose and explain the same as recommended by Noise Control Board.** *[WBUT 2018(O)]*

It is known that exposure to loud sound causes progressive hearing loss and it is also known that hearing deteriorates with age (presbycusis). In order to determine the damaging power due to exposure to noise, the idea of **noise dose** has been evolved. (The idea here is that a certain amount of sound energy can be tolerated in a working day, but above that amount damage starts.) In some countries the damage risk level is set at 85 dB(A) for 8 hours, but in relevant legislation in the UK and USA a higher dose is prescribed, i.e. 90 dB(A) for 8 hours.

On an equal energy basis, an increase of 3 dB in the exposure may be permitted for each halving of the duration of exposure. However, increase in level cannot be tolerated when the level is increased over a shorter time above 130 dB — hearing damage can occur. If the level fluctuates by more than $\pm 3$ dB(A), it is necessary to use the equivalent continuous level (LEQ).

**1. What is noise? Explain the sources of noise pollution. What are the psychological impacts of noise pollution? How can the noise be controlled at the source?** *[WBUT 2013(E)]*

**1st Part:**
Sound is produced when an object vibrates, alternately compressing and expanding the air. The compression and expansion travel like a wave from the source. They are called sound waves or simply vibrations.

If we treat sound as an auditory sensation produced by these vibrating bodies, it gets characterized by pitch, loudness and tone quality. However, these characters depend on psychological sensation, ear and judgment of the individual. In physical terms, sound may be defined as a fluctuation in pressure in an elastic medium. This definition gives rise to the objective characteristics of sound: frequency, intensity and waveform.

In simple terms, noise is unwanted sound. Sound is a form of energy which is emitted by a vibrating body and on reaching the ear causes the sensation of hearing through nerves. Sounds produced by all vibrating bodies are not audible. The frequency limits of audibility are from 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz.

A noise problem generally consists of three inter-related elements — the source, the receiver and the transmission path. This transmission path is usually the atmosphere through which the sound is propagated, but can include the structural materials of any building containing the receiver.

Noise may be continuous or intermittent. Noise may be of high frequency or of low frequency which is undesired for normal hearing. For example, the typical cry of a child produces sound which is mostly unfavorable to normal hearing. Since it is unwanted sound, we call it noise.

The discrimination and differentiation between sound and noise also depend upon the habit and interest of the person/species receiving it, the ambient conditions and the impact of the sound generated during that particular duration of time. There could be instances where an excellently rendered musical concert, for example, may be felt as noise. Sounds of frequencies less than 20 Hz are called infrasonics and greater than 20,000 Hz are called ultrasonics. Since noise is also a sound, the terms noise and sound are synonymously used.

So, simply we can define sound and noise as — Sound is the form of energy which gives the sensation of hearing and is produced by longitudinal mechanical waves in matter including solid, liquid and gases and transmitted by oscillations of atoms and molecules of matter. Noise is unwanted sound without agreeable musical quality. Therefore, when the effects of sound are undesirable, then it may be termed as "noise".

**4th Part — Noise control at source:**
There are at least three primary areas where noise control at source can be carried out.

**(a) Proper design:** The proper design of equipment to minimize noise generation is a good way, but it is a complex engineering problem which needs a strong background in the fundamentals of vibration, fluid mechanics, dynamics and machine design. In a very general way, the following should be considered while designing:
   i) **Using shock absorption technique** for absorbing impact energy. *(e.g., the use of non-metallic gears to reduce the noise generated by the metal-to-metal impact associated with metallic gears; the use of flexible mounts to support wood-planner knives to reduce the noise.)*
   ii) **Use efficient flow technique** for reducing noise associated with high fluid velocities and turbulence. *(e.g., a new quiet hydraulic pump in the flow path has been redesigned to give less turbulence.)*
   iii) **Reducing fluid jet velocity:** as jet noise is proportional to the eighth power of jet velocity, by reducing jet velocity, noise can be minimized.
   iv) **Reducing the sound radiating areas.** *(e.g., by placing the equipment on a wooden table or by fitting equipment on a concrete base.)*

**(b) Proper Operation:** All equipment should be operated at the design condition. Operating equipment at design pressure and speed should result in minimum noise generation. Several ways relating to operations are:
   i) If acoustic guards, covers or enclosures have been used, make sure that they are in place and all openings are acoustically sealed. (Noise is like water, which rushes out through any crack or opening.)
   ii) If equipment has been equipped with a muffler/silencer system, make sure that it is working according to design. (Many mufflers depend upon absorption within the case.)
   iii) Use proper cutting speed and feeding rate in machinery operations.
   iv) Provide good support for tool-bits to reduce vibration.
   v) Apply additional sound control devices. (Inlet and discharge silencers can often be added with a small investment.)

**(c) Proper maintenance:** When one would like to control noise at the source, then first of all there should be a measurement of noise produced by the source. Therefore, the noise level has to be measured at the source and at points around the source. A large source should be measured at respective points around the source.
   *(Additional maintenance points noted in the source:)*
   iii) Improve lubrication. Inadequate lubrication, degradation due to age, or incorrect lubricants can cause bearing noise.
   iv) Install bearings correctly.
   v) Avoid mechanical run-out of shafts.
   vi) Clean the silencers and ensure cracks on silencers are healed.

Besides these, noise can be controlled by the following ways:
- Reducing the noise levels from domestic sectors.
- Maintenance of automobiles.
- Control over vibrations.
- Low voice speaking.

**4. a) What is the working unit for measurement of sound intensity level (SIL)? Express it mathematically.** *[WBUT 2015(O)]*

There are two important parameters of sound or noise: **sound pressure** and **sound intensity**. They are measured in different units giving varying scales of magnitude. The common scientific acoustic unit is **Decibel (dB)**. It is not an absolute physical unit like volt, meter etc., but it is a ratio expressed as a logarithmic scale relative to a reference sound pressure level or intensity level.

$$\text{dB} = 10\log_{10}\frac{\text{measured intensity}}{\text{reference intensity}}$$

$$\text{dB} = 10\log_{10}\frac{I}{I_0}$$

This logarithmic scale takes care of the wide range of sound pressure and intensity. The reference intensity used is the 'threshold of hearing', which means sound which can be first heard at a sound pressure of $2\times10^{-5}\ \text{N/m}^2$ or sound intensity of $10^{-12}\ \text{N/m}^2$. `[as printed: the source prints the intensity reference using the same "N/m²" units]`

The dB scale is limited in the sense that it is not related to the human-ear frequency response and the environmental circumstance in which noise is produced. This has necessitated the design of noise-measuring meters which reduce the response to low and very high frequencies, characteristic of human-ear capacity. The community (ambient) noise levels are measured in the A-weighted SPL, abbreviated dB(A). This scale resembles the audible response of the human ear. Sounds of frequencies from 800 to 3000 Hz are covered by the A-weighted scale. These meters record the dB A scale, which is commonly used for measurement of general noise levels.

The dB has been the basic unit of sound, designed to fulfill the scientific requirement of giving information about the sound. In air, sound consists of pressure fluctuations about the mean atmospheric pressure. To be audible, these fluctuations must have frequency between 20 to 20,000 Hz.

If the amplitude of pressure fluctuation is $P$, then the sound level in dB is given by
$$L = 10\log_{10}\left[\frac{P}{P_0}\right]^2 \text{ dB}$$
where $P_0 = 2\times10^{-5}\ \text{N/m}^2$ (reference pressure). The dB is thus based on a ratio of pressure.

**4. b) What is the intensity of 100 dB sound?** *[WBUT 2015(O)]*

We know that, sound level $L\ (\text{dB}) = 10\log\dfrac{I}{I_0}$, where $I$ = measured intensity, $I_0$ = reference intensity.

In the first case (100 dB):
$$I_1 = I_0 \times 10^{L_1/10} \qquad \ldots(1)$$

In the second case (90 dB):
$$I_2 = I_0 \times 10^{L_2/10} \qquad \ldots(2)$$

With $L_1 = 100$ dB and $L_2 = 90$ dB,
$$\frac{I_1}{I_2} = \frac{1\times10^{100/10}}{1\times10^{90/10}} = \frac{10^{10}}{10^{9}} = 10 \text{ times}.$$

So, 100 dB sound is louder than 90 dB sound by 10 times.

**4. c) What is threshold limit value? Discuss various mechanisms to control noise.** *[WBUT 2015(O)]*

**1st part — Threshold Limit Value (TLV):**
When daily noise exposure is composed of two or more periods of noise exposure of different levels, their combined effect should be considered, rather than the individual effect of each.

If the sum of the ratios of the total duration of exposure at a specific noise level ($C$) to the total duration of exposure permitted at that level ($T$) is more than 1, then the mixed exposure should be considered to exceed the threshold limit value.

$$\text{i.e., if } \frac{C_1}{T_1} + \frac{C_2}{T_2} + \frac{C_3}{T_3} + \cdots > 1, \text{ then the sound exposure exceeds the threshold limit value.}$$

**5. How does 60 dB of sound level differ from 90 dBA on the basis of sound intensity? What do you understand by TLV of noise level?** *[WBUT 2017(E)]*

We know that, sound level $L\ (\text{dB}) = 10\log\dfrac{I}{I_0}$, where $I$ = measured intensity, $I_0$ = reference intensity.

In the first case (90 dB):
$$I_1 = I_0 \times 10^{L_1/10} \qquad \ldots(1)$$

In the second case (60 dB):
$$I_2 = I_0 \times 10^{L_2/10} \qquad \ldots(2)$$

With $L_1 = 90$ dB and $L_2 = 60$ dB,
$$\frac{I_1}{I_2} = \frac{1\times10^{90/10}}{1\times10^{60/10}} = 10^{9}/10^{6} = 1000 \text{ times}.$$

So, 90 dB sound is louder than 60 dB sound by 1000 times.

**8. b) If two machines produce 50 dB sounds simultaneously, what will be the total sound level?** *[WBUT 2018(E)]*

Consider that the first machine produces sound level $L_1$:
$$L_1 = 10\log\frac{I_1}{I_0} \qquad \ldots(1)$$
where $I_1$ = intensity of sound level $L_1$, $I_0$ = reference intensity.

Similarly, the second machine produces sound level $L_2$:
$$L_2 = 10\log\frac{I_2}{I_0} \qquad \ldots(2)$$
where $I_2$ = intensity of sound level $L_2$, $I_0$ = reference intensity.

When both machines start from the same place and same time, the total intensity of sound:
$$I_3 = I_1 + I_2 \qquad \ldots(3)$$

Rearranging (1):
$$I_1 = I_0 \times 10^{L_1/10} \qquad \ldots(4)$$
Similarly:
$$I_2 = I_0 \times 10^{L_2/10} \qquad \ldots(5)$$
$$I_3 = I_0\left(1\times10^{L_1/10} + 1\times10^{L_2/10}\right) \qquad \ldots(6)$$

The total sound produced is:
$$L_3 = 10\log\frac{I_3}{I_0} = 10\log\left(10^{L_1/10} + 10^{L_2/10}\right) \qquad \ldots(7)$$

As $L_1 = L_2 = 50$ dB:
$$L_3 = 10\log\left(10^{5} + 10^{5}\right) = 10\log\left(2\times10^{5}\right) = 10(\log 2 + 5) = 53.$$

So, when both machines operate from the same place and same time, the total sound would be **53 dB**.

**12. Write a short note on Sound Intensity Level (SIL).** *[WBUT 2018(E)]*

Sound intensity level (also known as acoustic intensity) is defined as the power carried by sound waves per unit area in a direction perpendicular to that area. The SI unit of intensity, which includes sound intensity, is the watt per square meter (W/m²). One application is the noise measurement of sound intensity in the air at a listener's location as a sound energy quantity.

Sound intensity is not the same physical quantity as sound pressure. Hearing is directly sensitive to sound pressure, which is related to sound intensity. In consumer audio electronics, the level differences are called "intensity" differences, but sound intensity is a specifically defined quantity and cannot be sensed by a simple microphone. The rate at which sound energy passes through a unit area held perpendicular to the direction of propagation of sound waves is called intensity of sound.
